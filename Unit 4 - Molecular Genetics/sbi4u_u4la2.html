You began life as a single cell about the size of the dot in this letter “i”—the combination of a haploid sperm and a haploid egg cell.

Now, through cell division, you are a mass of trillions of cells. All those cells contain a copy of the original DNA from the single original cell. Every time your cells divided, your DNA needed to be replicated and the results had to be checked, to make sure there were no errors. Without this process, you couldn’t exist.

Watson and Crick recognized that their model of DNA structure provided an obvious way for the molecule to replicate itself. Because each nucleotide base could only pair up with its complementary base, each strand of DNA could serve as a template for its own replication. But how did this happen?

It was widely recognized that this was the next big scientific question to be solved, so the race was now on to understand the process of DNA replication.

Finding the correct model of DNA replication

Initially, there were three models proposed for how a DNA molecule might replicate.

Model 1: Semi-Conservative Model

In the semi-conservative model, the parent DNA molecule splits apart, with each strand acting as a template for the newly synthesized strand. This produces two new DNA molecules, with each one containing one strand from the original parent DNA, and the other strand made up of new DNA. As in the conservative model, the nucleotide sequence is the same in each daughter DNA molecule.

Model 2: Conservative Model

In the conservative model, the parent, or original DNA molecule, produces an exact copy of itself but remains intact. The result is two daughter DNA molecules—one made entirely of new nucleotides, and the other composed of the same original nucleotides as the parent. However, because they are identical copies, the nucleotide sequence is the same in each.

Model 3: Dispersive Model

The dispersive model proposes that the parental DNA molecule is broken into fragments, and that each daughter DNA is made up of a random mix of parental and new DNA, again with the same nucleotide sequence as the parent.

Proving the semiconservative model of DNA replication

In 1958, the question of which model best described DNA replication was answered in the publication of a famous experiment by American researchers Matthew Meselson and Franklin Stahl.

The Meselson and Stahl experiments

To determine which kind of replication occurred, Meselson and Stahl decided to use isotopes of nitrogen to mark new nucleotides, thus distinguishing them from the ones in the original DNA.

By “tagging” the DNA in this way, they could determine whether the new DNA molecules:

  • were made entirely of new nucleotides (the conservative model)
  • contained half-original and half-new nucleotides (the semiconservative model)
  • were a random mix of each (the dispersive model)

Experiment 1

For their experiment, they used the common bacteria, E. coli, because it reproduced quickly in culture and its biology was well understood.

They also used nitrogen as their indicator atom, because it needs to be absorbed in large quantities in order for DNA replication to occur.

The heavy isotope, N 15 is heavier than the regular N 14 , so it can be separated out by density using a centrifuge. When substances that are suspended in liquids are placed in a test tube and spun in a centrifuge, the denser substances settle to the bottom of the test tube.

A centrifuge is equipment that spins test tubes around at very high speeds
Centrifuges come in different sizes and speeds. There is a gravitational force put on the material in the test tubes as it spins at high speed.
The material being centrifuged separates based on its density as it experiences greater-than-normal g-forces. The most dense material forms the bottom layers.

Expected results for the three DNA replication models

After bacteria grown with N 15 replicated in a medium or substance with N 14 for a few generations, the following results would occur depending on which model was correct:

  • bacterial DNA will have the weight of N 15 and N 14 , and consequently, two separate bands will be visible after centrifuging. (Conservative Model)
  • bacterial DNA will have an intermediate weight between the densities of N 15 and N 14 , and will also have bacterial DNA that is the weight of N 14 only. Consequently, two separate bands will be visible after centrifuging. (Semi-Conservative Model)
  • bacterial DNA will have an intermediate weight between the densities of N 15 and N 14 , and consequently, only one band will be visible after centrifuging. (Dispersive Model)

Understanding checkpoint: Finding the correct model of DNA replication

In the Meselson and Stahl experiment, what pattern of banding would you expect to find in the centrifuge tubes after one generation in the regular nitrogen medium, if DNA replication were, in fact, conservative?

If replication were conservative, there would be one band of heavy DNA and one band of regular DNA in the centrifuge tubes. This would be because one copy would contain the original heavy nitrogen, while the other copy would contain only the regular-weight nitrogen.

Stages in DNA replication

The process of DNA replication in prokaryotes (organisms whose cells don’t contain a nucleus) and eukaryotes (organisms whose cells contain a nucleus) is similar, even though they have differently shaped DNA. Prokaryotes have circular strands of DNA and contain about a thousand times fewer nucleotides than the long, twisted strands of DNA found in eukaryotes. Because of this, DNA replication in prokaryotes is much faster, usually taking less than an hour, while in eukaryotes it takes a few hours.

The process of DNA replication in both eukaryotes and prokaryotes can be broken down into three main phases:

Initiation: DNA is unwound and separated to expose each strand in the pair at multiple sites of replication along the DNA molecule.

Elongation: Enzymes attach complementary nucleotides onto the 3’ (three prime) end of each exposed strand in a linear sequence, and check for errors.

Termination: Nucleotide addition stops, enzymes are removed, and the newly formed strands of DNA coil back into the double helix shape.

Phase 1: Initiation

The first phase in replicating DNA is to unwind and open up the molecule so that the nucleotide base pairs on each strand can be exposed. You might think that the easiest way to do this would be to start at one end of the DNA and split it apart, like unzipping a zipper. This can work, but the process of replication would then be very slow because there would be only one replication site along the entire length of the DNA molecule, occurring just where the two strands are separating.

If replication occurred at only one replication site, it would take about one month to replicate the 200 million base pairs in a typical strand of eukaryotic DNA, and several days to replicate the shorter prokaryotic DNA. That is because in eukaryotic cells, only about 80 nucleotides per second can be added at a replication site. In prokaryotes, the rate is about 10 times faster because the DNA is not packaged in chromatin.

Taking days or weeks to replicate DNA is far too long for meeting the needs of growth and development in organisms, some of which may only live for a few days. The solution is to have hundreds or even thousands of replication sites along the length of a DNA molecule, all replicating different parts of the molecule at the same time. In this way, the molecule can be copied in hours, instead of days or weeks.

Origins of replication

The multiple DNA replication sites are called the origins of replication and occur all along the DNA molecule. They are located in areas with lots of A-T base pairs, because these nucleotides are held together with only two hydrogen bonds, so they are easier to pull apart than C-G base pairs, which are held together by three hydrogen bonds.

Unwinding DNA: Helicase and the replication fork

At each origin of replication point, the double-stranded DNA is unwound and separated by the enzyme helicase. Helicase uses ATP energy to break the hydrogen bonds joining the base pairs at the origins of replication. As a result, the two strands split apart, creating two prongs that resemble a fork. This is called the replication fork.

Replication bubbles

When helicase opens up the DNA molecule somewhere in the middle, a bubble containing two replication forks is formed in the DNA, one on each side of the bubble. Hundreds to thousands of these bubbles, called replication bubbles, form simultaneously along the DNA molecule and each one is a site at which replication can occur. Eventually, the bubbles grow wide enough to merge and the segments of daughter strands of DNA connect together to form a new complete strand.

Eliminating DNA coiling stress: Topoisomerase

There is a lot of activity occurring near the replication fork. For example, the enzyme helicase unwinds the DNA as it separates it. However, this causes some twisting and super-coiling to accumulate further ahead, along the DNA. If this coiling were to continue uncontrolled, it would eventually get so hard to twist the molecule further that replication would have to stop. In order to eliminate this coiling stress, another type of enzyme, called topoisomerase, moves along the DNA, just ahead of the helicase, cutting the DNA and relaxing the coils. In prokaryotes, and some eukaryotes, the particular type of topoisomerase that does this job is called gyrase.

Preventing base pairs from re-annealing: Single-stranded DNA binding proteins (SSBs)

Since the natural tendency of single-stranded DNA is to form hydrogen bonds with its exposed bases, there is a danger that the split DNA will simply re-anneal (join back together) or bond with other molecules in the nucleoplasm and become degraded. To prevent the base pairs from re-annealing and becoming degraded after the helicase enzyme has separated the strands, single-stranded DNA binding proteins (SSBs) bind to the exposed bases in the replication bubbles. The SSBs help prevent the two template parent DNA strands from re-establishing hydrogen bonds and protect the exposed bases until they are ready to bond with their complementary nucleotides.

Understanding checkpoint: The of process of initiation

Hydrogen bonds are quite weak compared to covalent bonds. Explain why this fact is actually advantageous to DNA in its role as the hereditary material in cells.

This is an advantage because it allows these bonds to be easily broken, thus facilitating the process of DNA replication. In order for DNA replication to occur, helicase must break the hydrogen bonds formed between nitrogenous bases.

DNA replication occurs in virtually all cells prior to cell division. Why is DNA replication essential?

DNA replication is essential because it preserves genetic information, from generation to generation, in each new daughter cell. This includes the information for an organism’s genes, growth, repair, and reproduction.

Phase 2: Elongation

Forming the complimentary daughter DNA strands: DNA polymerases

Once the DNA has been unwound and split at the replication fork, the next phase involves stringing together nucleotides along the exposed templates to form new strands.

This process is accomplished by DNA polymerase, which attaches nucleotides, one at a time, to the 3’ end of a pre-existing chain of nucleotides. The free-floating nucleotides it gathers from the nucleoplasm have three phosphate groups attached to their 3’ end, and so are called deoxyribonucleoside triphosphates. After attaching a nucleotide, DNA polymerase also performs checks for errors, to make sure that the correct base has been added.

Removing RNA primers and attaching nucleotides: DNA polymerase I and DNA polymerase III

In prokaryotes, there are five versions of DNA polymerase, while in eukaryotes, there are at least 15. Each version is numbered and has a specific function in the process. You will focus only on the actions of DNA polymerase I and III in this learning activity.

DNA polymerase I is active mostly near the end of DNA replication, where it removes the RNA primer.

DNA polymerase III is responsible for joining the nucleotides together and checking for errors.

Attaching nucleotides to form new DNA requires energy: dNTPs

How nucleotides are attached to form new DNA using dNTPs:

  1. DNA formation requires energy, and this comes from dNTPs (deoxyribonucleoside triphosphates). Essentially, dNTPs are the nucleotides adenine, thymine, guanine and cytosine with two extra end phosphate groups on each nucleotide that form dATP, dTTP, dGTP and dCTP respectively.
  2. New nucleotides are added to the growing DNA strand in a 5’ to 3’ direction. More information about this to follow.
  3. During the joining reaction, the two end phosphates break off from the deoxyribonucleoside triphosphate (dNTP), causing it to release energy.
  4. This energy is used to create the phosphodiester bond that connects the new nucleotide to the growing chain of nucleotides on the strand.
  5. Two phosphate ions are released due to the formation of the phosphodiester bond.

Forming a short sequence for DNA polymerase to bind to: RNA primers and primase

DNA polymerase III can only add nucleotides onto an existing DNA strand; it cannot begin the synthesis of a new strand. To begin the synthesis of a new strand, there must be a starting sequence of nucleotides for DNA polymerase III to attach to. This starting sequence is provided by a short fragment of RNA that is created and attached to the exposed template strand to serve as the starting nucleotides from which DNA polymerase III can extend outwards.

The short fragment of RNA is called an RNA primer, which is about 10 to 100 base pairs long and is complementary to a section of the exposed template strand. The formation of this RNA primer requires the enzyme primase. Once the RNA primer is formed and attached to the DNA template, the primase enzyme is released.

The two strands of newly forming DNA (leading and lagging) are both assembled simultaneously using DNA polymerase; however, because of their different orientations, the process and speed is not the same on each strand. The details of each are described next.

New DNA formation: Leading and lagging strands

Leading strand

On the leading strand, DNA polymerase III continuously adds nucleotides, starting from the initial RNA primer. It joins a new nucleotide to the existing chain of nucleotides by catalyzing the formation of phosphodiester bonds at the 3’ end, between the last nucleotide on the chain and the new deoxyribonucleoside triphosphate. Since this is the natural orientation of the leading strand, the process can continue easily along this strand in the same direction as the replication fork. When replication is finished, the RNA primer is replaced with DNA.

Lagging strand

The lagging strand does not have the 3’ end exposed on its last nucleotide; it has the 5’ end, where it can’t add new nucleotides. Because of this, adding more nucleotides to the growing strand requires a four-step process involving several more enzymes.

As with the leading strand, the DNA synthesis on the lagging strand starts with the creation of short pieces of RNA templates that attach along the exposed template of the lagging strand. The RNA templates serve as starting points for the addition of new nucleotides. The lagging strand is then synthesized discontinuously in short fragments in the opposite direction to the leading strand (that is, away from the fork). Eventually, the short fragments are attached together to form the new DNA strand. It is because of these extra steps that this strand takes longer to assemble, which is why it lags behind the leading strand.

Make RNA primer

Create an initial chain of nucleotides using RNA primase and attach it to complementary exposed bases on the template strand.

Add nucleotides to RNA primer

DNA polymerase III extends the initial chain of nucleotides by adding dNTPs onto the free 3’ hydroxyl (-OH) end of the RNA primer. This extension runs in the opposite direction to the replication fork. The fragment of DNA created is about a few hundred nucleotides long in eukaryotes, and a few thousand long in prokaryotes. These DNA fragments are called Okazaki fragments after Tsuneko Okazaki, the Japanese scientist who first discovered their role in the 1960s.

Remove RNA primer

When an Okazaki fragment has grown long enough to bump into the next fragment along the template, it stops growing and the DNA polymerase III is released. DNA polymerase I attaches at this location and removes the RNA primer between the two fragments, while replacing the RNA bases with the correct DNA nucleotides.

Join Okazaki fragments together

DNA polymerase I is released and the adjacent Okazaki fragments are joined together, using another enzyme called DNA ligase. The result is a new complete strand of DNA.

Proof-Reading and Error Correction: Polymerase III

DNA Polymerase III prevents point mutations by detecting the number of hydrogen bonds a nucleotide forms. If hydrogen bonding does not occur, the bases are mismatched and the DNA Polymerase replaces the incorrect base with the correct one.

Like all reactions in living cells, DNA replication takes place in a chemical environment that is very chaotic. During this process, millions of molecules of phosphates, nucleotides, RNA, and enzymes are all colliding around each other. In this environment it is possible for errors in DNA replication to occur, with the occasional wrong nucleotide being added to the growing template. Errors in copying the genetic code are called point mutations and can be very dangerous for the organism, so mechanisms have evolved to catch errors as they happen.

DNA polymerase III can detect if the nucleotide it has just added to the growing strand has formed hydrogen bonds with its counterpart on the template strand. If hydrogen bonding does not occur, it means that the bases are mismatched, so DNA polymerase III removes the incorrect nucleotide and replaces it with the correct one. This continuous error-checking process is so good that only about one error per billion base pairs occurs.

Phase 3: Termination

When the last step in the elongation phase along the strand is completed, the newly formed strand of DNA spontaneously begins to twist into a helical shape. The replication process of bubble formation, nucleotide addition, and re-twisting continues along the DNA until two complete and separate semi-conservative daughter DNA molecules are made.

Removing the RNA primer from the 5’ end of daughter DNA

Each DNA replication results in a slightly shorter daughter molecule because there is no way to fill in the 5’ end with nucleotides once the RNA primer is removed.

As you saw in step three of elongation, an important act in replication is to remove the RNA primer from the end of a newly formed section and replace it with DNA. However, when the RNA primer is removed from the 5’ end of each daughter DNA molecule, there is no adjacent nucleotide chain with a 3’ end that can be extended to fill in the gap. This means that there is a gap at the 5’ end of each DNA molecule that remains unpaired with the template strand.

The gap is as long as the RNA primer. Eventually, the unpaired nucleotides on the template strand break off, leaving the daughter DNA molecule a little shorter than its parent. So, with each replication, the DNA gets a bit shorter. In humans, about 100 base pairs are lost from each end of each chromosome every time DNA replicates.

Prokaryotes, like bacteria, do not suffer from this problem, because their DNA is circular, so there are no ends with gaps left exposed.

Even though 100 base pairs do not seem like a lot when compared to the millions of base pairs that make up eukaryotic chromosomes, their loss over time could result in significant genetic damage. For this reason, eukaryotic organisms have evolved special regions at the end of their chromosomes called telomeres that help to deal with this problem.

Understanding checkpoint

Special regions at the end of chromosomes: Telomeres

Telomeres are found at the end of DNA, where primers are removed after DNA replication. In humans, the sequence is TTAGGG

A telomere is a region of several thousand nucleotides made up of a simple repeated sequence that does not code for proteins. In humans, the sequence is TTAGGG. This acts as a buffer region of expendable DNA that can be gradually lost over repeated replications without damaging the active genetic code.

Telomeres are of finite length, which means that cells are only able to divide a certain number of times before the DNA loss prevents further division. Cells die when their telomeres become too short to support further cell division. Cells with longer telomeres live longer. When you were born, your telomeres were longer than they are now, and with every division since then, they have been getting slightly smaller.

Some cells, like reproductive cells, need to be able to replicate their DNA more times than somatic cells, so they produce an enzyme called telomerase that extends the telomeres.

Telomerase can become mistakenly active in somatic cells, allowing cells to multiply indefinitely, potentially leading to cancer formation. The discovery of the importance of telomeres to cell aging and cancer formation has led to promising areas of medical research in combating age-related diseases and developing new cancer treatments.

Understanding checkpoint: DNA Replication

What role do telomeres play in aging?

Telomeres protect the genetic information near the ends of a DNA molecule. With each replication, short segments of them break off, resulting in the telomeres getting shorter over time. This sets a limit to how many times a cell can divide because, at some point, the telomeres get too short and DNA replication stops. When this happens, the cell dies. Cells with longer telomeres live longer.
