Importance of biotechnology

The word “biotechnology” means “life tools” (as “bio” means “life” and “technology” means “tools” in ancient Greek). A more technical definition is provided by the United Nations Convention on Biodiversity:

Definition

“Biotechnology is any technological application that uses biological systems, living organisms, or derivatives thereof, to make or modify products or processes for specific use.”

Canadian example: Barcode of Life Project

The Barcode of Life Project is an example of how Canadians are leading the world in developing pioneering biotechnology. In this case, the tool of genetic sequencing is being used for biodiversity conservation.

The Barcode of Life Project uses a simple observation from the revolution in genetic sequencing. Most species have short segments of genetic code (approximately 648 base pairs) that are nearly identical across all individuals of that species, but which are different from other species. If we can determine these sequences for every species, then we can quickly identify each species by reading short sections of their DNA, in the same way that a barcode uniquely identifies a product in a store.

The Barcode of Life Project began in 2003, in the research lab of Prof. Paul Hebert, an evolutionary biologist from the University of Guelph in Ontario. It has now become a worldwide program, with research networks formed in many countries.

Practical applications of the Barcode for Life Project

A recent application of this project has been in identifying species of fish for sale in seafood markets and restaurants around the world. Sometimes species are sold illegally or inexpensive species are given false names to hide their true identity. In 2008, two New York City high school students used the freely available barcode database to prove that about one quarter of fish items they purchased in Manhattan were inaccurately labelled. Their “Sushigate” investigation earned front-page coverage in The New York Times newspaper

One problem with biodiversity conservation is the difficulty that people have in identifying different species. Even highly trained specialists in taxonomy are limited to knowing only about 1,000 species, yet there are at least 10 million species on the planet. Endangered species may be living in threatened areas but may go unnoticed. This is especially true for insects, soil invertebrates, fungi, and small plants.

It takes many years to train a person in taxonomy and such training is very expensive. There aren’t enough highly trained people or enough time to train them, to address the biodiversity crisis facing us today. This is a particular problem in developing nations where most of the world’s biodiversity exists. The Barcode of Life Project is seeking a way for everyone—trained or not—to be able to identify any one of millions of species in a few minutes, using hand-held technology.

Large collaborative scientific projects like the Barcode of Life Project do not come about by chance. It requires a lot of hard work to bring partners together and get the research funding for a project like this. One important job is to write scientific assessment reports, outlining the costs and benefits of the proposed research.

Writing scientific assessments

Scientific assessments, such as the one that follows:

  • have an informative title.
  • start out with an introduction of the project’s goals and technologies.
  • provide detailed evidence in the body of the report so that the reader can see why it is relevant.
  • properly cite references to support scientific points, where necessary. Short links to the references (name and year only) are given in the main text of the report immediately after the information based on them. Notice that if the article cited has three or more authors, then only the first author’s name is given in the brackets, to save space. Full citations for each reference are placed at the end of the report in alphabetical order, based on the last name of the first author (or the name of the organization, if the report has too many authors to list, for example, GBA 1995).
  • end with a conclusion that clearly states the author’s position.

Here is an example of a short (350-word) scientific assessment of the costs and benefits of DNA barcoding. Read “DNA Barcoding” assessment and identify all the parts that make up a scientific assessment.

DNA barcoding: A new tool for cataloguing biodiversity

It has taken over 250 years for scientists to catalogue only about 15% of the world’s biodiversity (GBA 1995). With so many species and ecosystems now under immediate threat, the worry is that most species will be extinct before they can even be identified. Species identification is one of the first steps in biodiversity protection. To speed up species identification, a new genetics tool is being developed that will allow non-experts anywhere in the world to quickly identify species of all kinds. DNA barcodes use a small fragment of an organism’s DNA—a portion of a single gene—to identify the species to which an organism belongs.

The Barcode of Life Project, started by Canadian researcher Paul Hebert at the University of Guelph, is developing a DNA-based identification system that can catalogue all species on earth using these barcodes (Dooh and Hebert 2005).

With this technology, anyone anywhere could rapidly identify any species at any stage of development for less than 50 cents per sample (Dooh and Hebert 2005). This would make bioprospecting much more efficient and economical, speeding up the discovery of organisms yielding life-saving drugs. Fast, cheap, and accurate identification of species will also help in controlling pests in agriculture, policing the illegal traffic in endangered species, and promoting protection for species-rich areas that may have been overlooked.

This technology does not yet work for all species. Some plants and microbes may not have species-level barcodes in the sequences of DNA being examined. This requires more research. Also, barcoding does not replace the need for researchers trained in taxonomy, phylogenetics, or ecology, as some have warned. In fact, because barcoding helps to catalogue biodiversity, it could provide an amazing new source of data that would greatly benefit research in these fields (Hajibabaei et al. 2007).

The process began in Canada, and scientists here continue to lead international work aiming to catalogue the earth’s biodiversity. The DNA barcoding project provides a foundation for the creation of a complete inventory of biodiversity in Canada, which would be the first inventory of its kind anywhere in the world. It is important that this work continue to be funded and develop.

Biotechnology techniques

The lab techniques that are used in genetic biotechnology are incredibly varied. Many different methods are used and new ones are being developed all the time. However, there are some fundamental techniques that are understood by most biotechnology researchers. You will learn about five of these techniques in the following sections, all of which relate to working with DNA:

1. Extraction

Technique 1: Extracting DNA

Most genetic technologies start by extracting DNA from cells.

The four basic steps in extracting DNA are always the same:

Physically break open the cell (lysis)

Break open the cell: The cell wall, cell membrane, and organelle membranes (mitochondria and chloroplasts) must be broken, so that the DNA can be released. Because plants have cell walls, these must be broken by mechanical action, such as grinding or blending. Animal cells may also be ground up as well, to increase the surface area for the next step.

Remove the lipid membranes that protect the DNA, using a detergent

Break the lipid membranes apart: The lipid-rich membranes are torn apart, using a detergent. Most laboratories use the detergent sodium dodecyl sulphate (SDS). This detergent is also found in many household detergents (such as liquid detergents used for washing dishes).

Lipid-rich membranes can be torn apart, using the detergent sodium dodecyl sulphate (SDS).

Protect the DNA from degradation by enzymes

Protect the DNA: After the membranes are torn apart, the DNA mixes with the cytoplasm. The enzymes present in the cytoplasm can quickly degrade the DNA. Many labs add a protease (an enzyme that degrades proteins) to the mixture, to destroy the enzymes. Another technique is to keep the mixture very cold, in order to slow down the destructive action of the enzymes.

Separate the DNA from the other cellular molecules, using a solvent

Separate the DNA, using a solvent: DNA is soluble in water. The DNA can be separated using a water filtration process or it can be extracted out of solution, using alcohol. DNA is not soluble in alcohol, but the other molecules are, so alcohol can be used to separate the DNA from the other cell components.

DNA can be separated using a water filtration process or it can be extracted out of solution, using alcohol.

Isolating individual genes out of chromosomes

The extracted DNA contains the entire genome of the organism. Usually, it is not practical to work with all the genes. Often, researchers are only interested in studying one gene, or parts of a gene. Individual genes can be cut from the chromosome using a specific restriction endonuclease (RE) enzyme. These enzymes work by recognizing and cutting a specific sequence of nucleotides at a location called a restriction site. Biologists use these enzymes as “molecular scissors.” Many different kinds of endonucleases have been found, each targeting a different sequence.

Very small amounts of purified RE are used—around 1 microlitre (where 1 µl = 10–6 L). The REs are added to the DNA sample, using a micropipette. A micropipette is a very accurate instrument that biologists use to transfer small amounts of solution (1–1000 µl) from one location to another.

The DNA restriction fragments often have a few unpaired nucleotides at each end of the fragment. These short sequences, called sticky ends, allow the fragment to join with other DNA that has also been cut by the same RE. The result is that the DNA fragments can be joined together—even with DNA from different species—to form new DNA called recombinant DNA.

2. Electrophoresis

Technique 2: Electrophoresis

Extracted DNA is invisible to the eye, so you do not know exactly what is in your sample. It may even be contaminated with DNA from other sources. One technique for determining whether your sample contains the right fragments of DNA is called gel electrophoresis.

A closer look at gel electrophoresis

Gel electrophoresis combines knowledge from the fields of chemistry and physics. You can think of it as an obstacle course for molecules. Negatively charged DNA molecules are forced to pass through a gelatin-like material called agarose, which slows them down. The negatively charged molecules are pulled towards the positively charged end of the agarose gel. The larger molecules get blocked more easily, so they move through the gel more slowly. Smaller molecules can avoid some obstacles, so they move through the gel more rapidly.

The extracted DNA solution is forced to pass through the gel for a fixed period of time. Special imaging techniques are then used to see how far the different-sized fragments of DNA have moved. The result is that the different sizes of DNA molecules are spread out across the gel in stripes or bands. The size of the DNA molecule making up each band can then be determined, in order to see if your sample contains the fragments you thought it did.

A gel is usually constructed with 8 to 10 wells along its top end, into which the samples are deposited. As the DNA moves downward through the gel from each well, it flows in straight lines called lanes. The first lane is often reserved for a special sample called a DNA ladder. The ladder is a solution made up of a variety of DNA fragments of known length. As the ladder sample is drawn through the gel it will leave many bands, representing DNA fragments of known size, arranged in order from largest to smallest. This series of bands is like having a ruler on the gel, so you can determine the fragment size of the DNA in your samples.

3. Amplification

Technique 3: Amplification using polymerase chain reactions (PCR)

You usually need a lot of DNA material to perform genetic analyses, but sometimes the amount of DNA in your extraction is tiny. What you need is a fast way to make millions of copies of the DNA fragments in your sample (known as amplifying), so that you can analyze it. The polymerase chain reaction (PCR) is a method of replicating specific sequences within a DNA sample. The invention of PCR in 1984 revolutionized genetic research. Kary Mullis (USA) won the 1993 Nobel Prize in Chemistry for inventing PCR, which is now routinely used in biology labs around the world.

Overview of the polymerase chain reaction

PCR works by amplifying a particular sequence of DNA selected by the researcher. The selection is done using a customized DNA primer, which is similar to RNA primers in DNA replication (which you learned about earlier) but is artificially made. Each primer is designed to bind only to a specific sequence on the DNA. This ensures that only the DNA sequence of interest gets amplified. The DNA sequence is then replicated, through an automated series of steps, at temperatures much higher than those naturally occurring in a cell. The high temperatures speed up the replication process significantly.

The importance of Taq polymerase

PCR would not be possible without Thermus aquaticus, a bacterium that lives in hot springs and undersea hydrothermal vents. This bacterium contains a form of DNA polymerase called Taq polymerase, which achieves its optimal activity around 72°C. Mullis recognized the potential of this special DNA polymerase. In experiments, he combined the DNA fragments to be amplified, Taq polymerase, DNA primers, and free DNA nucleotides in vials alternately heated and then cooled. He found that, by doing so, the rate of DNA replication was increased exponentially, producing millions of copies of his target DNA fragment in a few hours.

How Labs Perform PCR?

Over the past 25 years, the PCR process has been refined and automated, allowing researchers to amplify billions of copies of DNA in just a few hours.

A closer look at how PCR works:

  1. The process begins by adding all the necessary ingredients to a vial:
  • the DNA sample
  • a specific primer
  • Taq polymerase
  • free DNA nucleotides.
  1. The vial is then heated to 94°C, which causes the two coiled DNA strands to unravel and separate (denature). As you learned earlier, in normal DNA replication, this unravelling and separation of the DNA strands is done using the enzymes helicase and topoisomerase.
  2. The temperature is then lowered to 60°C, which allows the primer to bind to the template DNA strand at the correct location. This is called the annealing temperature, because the primer anneals (binds) to the DNA.
  3. The temperature is then raised to 72°C, which is the optimal temperature for the Taq polymerase to start adding DNA nucleotides at the 3’ end of the template strand. This elongation process is completed in a few minutes and the result is a doubling of the target DNA in the sample.
  4. The whole process is repeated, cycling the vial’s temperature between 94°C, 60°C, and 72°C, over the course of a few minutes.

This process results in an exponential increase in the amount of DNA in the sample, because with every repetition of the cycle, the amount of target DNA gets doubled. Normally, this process is repeated about 30 to 35 times, yielding billions of copies of the target DNA in only a few hours. New machines called thermocyclers automatically take a sample of vials throughout the three temperature stages and repeat the cycle as often as desired.

Describe the unique feature of Taq polymerase that makes PCR possible.

The unique feature of the Taq polymerase enzyme that makes PCR possible is that its optimal activity occurs at a high temperature (around 72°C). Most enzymes denature (split apart) above 40°C. Therefore, the DNA sample being amplified can be separated cleanly by raising the temperature, while the activity of the enzyme is improved. This speeds up the replication process greatly.

Explain why PCR needs to be carried out at three different temperatures.

PCR requires three steps and each one occurs at a different optimal temperature. The first step is to denature or split apart the DNA molecule. At 94°C, this will happen naturally, without the use of enzymes. The next step is the attachment of the primer molecule to the exposed DNA templates. This is called annealing and happens most effectively at around 60°C. The final step is the addition of new nucleotides to the growing DNA strand. This elongation step occurs with the help of Taq polymerase, which is optimally active at 72°C.

4. Sequencing

Technique 4: Sequencing

Once a segment of DNA has been extracted and amplified, it is relatively easy to sequence it. You may want to know the sequence to understand a gene or to identify a particular individual (such as occurs in DNA fingerprinting). DNA sequencing is a relatively new technology that has opened a new era in genetics and biotechnology. Perhaps the most famous application of this new technology was the Human Genome Project. This project aimed to obtain the sequence of the entire human genome—all 3 billion base pairs. It took thousands of researchers many years to achieve this, but by 2003, it was complete. Since then, the complete genetic sequence of other important species has been obtained, including that of the mouse, fruit fly and various crop plants.

The basis for DNA sequencing was initially developed by Frederick Sanger in Britain, in the 1970s. It is now called chain termination sequencing and uses a modified form of the polymerase chain reaction.

Chain termination sequencing

The process of chain termination sequencing results in the creation of many short segments of the original DNA fragment called DNA oligonucleotides. Their lengths range from one base, right up to the entire sequence.

Dideoxynucleotide triphosphates (ddNTPs) as DNA chain terminators.

The key innovation in Sanger’s method was the use of dideoxynucleotide triphosphates (ddNTPs) as DNA chain terminators. The ddNTPs are like regular nucleotides, except that they don’t have a 3’-OH bonding site for the next incoming phosphate group. This means that no other nucleotide can bind to them, so the elongation process of the DNA strand stops immediately after they are added.

Sanger’s method

  • Sanger added ddNTP versions of each nucleotide (ddA, ddT, ddC, and ddG) to a solution undergoing normal DNA replication, along with regular nucleotides (A, T, C, G).
  • If a normal nucleotide joined the strand, the elongation process would continue.
  • The ddNTPs would bind to the growing DNA strand, just like their normal nucleotide counterparts but the DNA strand would stop growing.
  • Every time the growing DNA strand attempted to add a new nucleotide, it was up to chance whether this nucleotide would be the regular or ddNTP version.
  • The result was a mix of DNA pieces made up of all possible lengths. If you arrange these pieces in order by length and read the last nucleotide in each, you get the sequence of the original DNA fragment. Once the chain termination sequencing reaction is complete, the next step is to read the last nucleotide in each oligonucleotide. This is a two-step process.

  • The first step is to get the oligonucleotides arranged in ascending order, by length.
  • The second step is to identify the nucleotide in the last position. Automated sequencers perform both steps quickly, so they are able to process hundreds of samples in a few hours.

Sorting the oligonucleotides using electrophoresis

You have seen how gel electrophoresis can be used to sort DNA molecules by their length. Labs use several automated electrophoresis methods and new ones are continually being developed. A current popular technique is called capillary electrophoresis. With this technique, a sample is passed through a very thin tube (capillary) containing a special gel, which allows the smaller molecules to move through more quickly, while slowing the larger ones down. The result is that the oligonucleotides get sorted by size, with the shortest one coming out first.

Reading the last nucleotide

In order to identify the last nucleotide in the oligonucleotide sequence, each ddNTP needs to be labelled in some unique way. One popular method is to attach small fluorescent molecules to the ddNTPs, using a different fluorescent colour for each of the four bases. When these fluorescent molecules are exposed to a strong light, like a laser, they fluoresce (glow), giving off a particular colour of light. A camera records the colour of light released as the oligonucleotides pass under the light source. 5. Recombinant DNA

Technique 5: Recombinant DNA

Once biologists learned how to cut specific genes from chromosomes using restriction enzymes, the next step was to take individual genes from one genome and insert them into another genome. This process, called recombination, was developed in 1973 by Boyer and Cohen (as described in the timeline). This was the beginning of recombinant DNA technology.

In 1978, Herbert Boyer achieved another first: he transferred human genes into a bacterium (E. coli) to create synthetic human insulin. This was the first human application of recombinant DNA technology. The genetically modified bacteria are described as transgenic, because they contain genes from another species.

In 1982, human insulin became the first commercially available genetically engineered product created using recombinant DNA techniques. Insulin is required by hundreds of thousands of diabetics daily. Making it in genetically engineered microorganisms provides an allergy-free, abundant, and inexpensive supply. Since then, hundreds of kinds of transgenic organisms have been produced, mostly for use in agriculture and medicine.

How foreign genes are inserted into a host organism

The methods for inserting genes are different, depending on whether the genes are being inserted into prokaryotes (for example, bacteria) or eukaryotes (for example, crop plants and livestock). Since the genomes of prokaryotes are easier to work with, they were the first group to have foreign, usually eukaryotic, genes inserted into their genome. The process for inserting foreign genes into eukaryotes uses a similar principle, so only the details for prokaryotes will be dealt with here.

Role of plasmids

Not all the genetic material in bacteria is stored in their chromosomes. They also have structures called plasmids, which contain some genetic material. Plasmids are small circular ribbons of DNA (about 3 kbp long) that can move between bacteria. Plasmids are separate from the chromosomal DNA but are also used by the bacteria to code for proteins. When the bacterium undergoes replication, the plasmid DNA is copied, along with the cell’s chromosomal DNA.

The key to recombinant DNA is the plasmid. Researchers use the same restriction enzyme (for example, the endonuclease, HindIII) to cut open the plasmid in a bacterium like E. coli, as well as cut up specific fragments of DNA in the eukaryotic cell. This creates a plasmid and DNA fragments that all have sticky ends. These sticky ends can be joined together in a process called ligation. The enzyme ligase is used to guide the sticky ends through the rejoining process. In this way, genes not normally found in the plasmid can be inserted into it to create a recombinant plasmid. The recombinant plasmid is called a vector, because it is the vehicle that carries the foreign gene into the bacteria.

Expressing the new gene

The recombinant plasmid vector is then placed in a culture medium where the bacterium takes in the plasmid. It is now treated as a normal part of the cell. The bacterium makes proteins from the new gene and replicates the plasmid, along with its normal chromosomal DNA, during reproduction. This means that the new gene is now part of the permanent genetic makeup of that organism. Figure 12.6 summarizes the process of making the recombinant plasmid and inserting it into the host bacterium.

Bacteria can reproduce once every 20 minutes, so, within hours, several million copies of the new gene can be made. The bacterial cells act as micro-factories, pumping out the desired protein as instructed by the foreign DNA. Technicians can then isolate and purify the protein for a specific use.

What is recombinant DNA?

Recombinant DNA is the result of removing genetic material from one genome and inserting it into another genome. This is often done across species to create transgenic organisms.

Describe one useful product produced by recombinant DNA.

Insulin is a hormone needed by millions of diabetics around the world. Producing insulin in transgenic bacteria creates a cheap, plentiful supply that does not cause the same allergy problems as the old source of insulin obtained from animals.

What is the role of restriction enzymes in the process of making recombinant DNA?

Restriction enzymes are designed to cut the DNA at particular locations. They are like “molecular scissors” that can be designed to cut out specific genes or fragments of genes.

Biotechnology in Canada

Canada has the second-largest biotech industry in the world, after the United States. Most of the research and product development done in Canada is in the fields of health, medicine, and agriculture.

The various biotechnology fields in Canada can be divided into two sectors:

  • Manipulating DNA

Regulation of biotechnology in Canada

Canada has developed one of the best systems in the world for regulating biotechnology in order to protect people and the environment. The process for getting a biotechnology product approved for use in Canada is longer and more rigorous than in most countries. For example, the cow growth hormone bovine somatotropin (BST) is approved for use in the U.S. but is banned in Canada.

Canada has several federal government agencies that are responsible for regulating biotechnology.

The Canadian Food Inspection Agency (CFIA) is responsible for the regulation of food products produced using biotechnology. This includes plants, animals, animal feeds, and animal medications. For example, for genetically modified crop plants, the CFIA assesses the potential risk of environmental impacts. It also oversees import permits and research.

Health Canada is responsible for assessing the risks to human health of products produced using biotechnology.

This includes:

  • Foods
  • Drugs
  • Cosmetics
  • Medical devices,
  • Pest-control products.

To assess the safety and societal benefit of new types of bioengineered foods, the agency considers:

  • the process used to develop the food
  • its nutritional quality
  • the presence of any toxins
  • the potential for allergic reactions.

There are also some misconceptions about GE foods among the public. For example, some people fear that just by eating a genetically engineered food, they will incorporate a foreign gene into their genetic code, but that is biologically impossible. Just because a bacterial gene has been inserted into a tomato does not mean that the gene will somehow find its way into the consumer’s genome. Your genetic code does not get changed by the foods you eat.

This does not mean that all genetically engineered foods are safe, however. As with all new technology, sometimes unexpected consequences occur. For example, in 2000, U.S. researchers inserted an anti-freeze gene from the flounder, a cold-water fish, into strains of strawberries and oranges to help them tolerate freezing. These genetically engineered fruits could then be grown in colder climates. Unfortunately, the fish’s anti-freeze protein that was being expressed in the “fishfruit” could induce allergic reactions in people who were allergic to seafood. Because of this, these GE fruits were removed from agricultural production.

In theory, inserting the anti-freeze gene from flounder fish into strawberries was a good idea; however, fish’s anti-freeze protein that was being expressed in the “fishfruit” could induce allergic reactions in people who were allergic to seafood. These GMO strawberries were removed from agricultural production.

Some people argue that the safety requirements imposed on GE foods are too strict. They say that common foods like peanuts and scallops also induce allergies in many people, but these foods are not banned from being sold. Consumer protection advocates respond that labels on food products help people to avoid those foods that cause their allergies, but with GE foods, the consumer does not know what genes have been inserted into the food. Someone with a seafood allergy could perhaps get a potentially deadly allergic reaction to a chocolate bar. How can consumer safety be guaranteed?

The answers are not simple. Each biotechnology application should be examined for its risks and benefits to society. We are now living in an era of rapid advancements in biotechnology and this will affect everyone.
