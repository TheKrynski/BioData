In the early 1950s, the race to discover the chemical structure of the heredity molecule, DNA, was intense. Only a few years earlier, it had been demonstrated that DNA carries genetic information. The story of the discovery of DNA demonstrates the process of science, revealing how science works by building on the earlier work of others, the excitement of discovery, and the role that chance plays in the process.

Even though the structure of DNA has been known for decades, work on how its genetic information can be used, changed, and copied is still in its early stages. New technologies are allowing the DNA code of any species to be quickly sequenced and compared.

Chemical composition of DNA

Watson and Crick ended their famous 1953 paper describing the structure of DNA with the modest statement that,

“It has not escaped our notice that the specific pairing we have postulated immediately suggests a possible copying mechanism for the genetic material.”

It was obvious from the basic structure of DNA that each strand in the pair could serve as a template to make a new strand. But how did this happen? Watson and Crick didn’t understand the details of the process yet, but they wanted to let the world know that they were aware of its importance.

We now know how DNA replicates itself and how it encodes genetic information.

But there is still far more to be learned and new discoveries will be based on an understanding of the chemistry of the molecules involved. To understand the potential of DNA you need to know the details of its chemical structure, so you will focus on that in the rest of this section.

DNA consists of two long strands made up of repeating sequences of simple units called nucleotides (or bases). Each nucleotide is attached to a sugar and a phosphate group. The sugar and phosphate groups join together in adjacent nucleotides to form a backbone for the DNA molecule. The nucleotide sequences in the two strands run in opposite directions to each other, which is why we describe the molecule as anti-parallel.

Because the nucleotides are the molecules that give DNA its ability to store and transmit genetic information, it is important to know their chemical structure.

Purines can only bond with pyrimidines and vice versa because of the way in which the hydrogen bonds are positioned on the rings, pyrimidine-to-pyrimidine pairings are energetically unfavourable because the molecules are too far apart for hydrogen bonding to be established. The opposite is true for purine-to-purine pairings, which are energetically unfavourable because the molecules are too close, leading to overlap repulsion.

The only possible pairings are A with C/T and G with C/T. However, it turns out that AC and GT pairings are impossible because the positions of hydrogen donors and acceptors are mismatched.

Each nitrogen base is attached by a glycosidic bond to a cyclic five-carbon sugar molecule called deoxyribose.

The first four carbon atoms in deoxyribose, together with an oxygen atom, form a five-sided ring. The fifth carbon atom extends out from the ring. According to chemical convention, the carbon atoms are numbered clockwise, starting with the carbon atom to the immediate right of the oxygen atom. This first carbon atom is designated as 1 (pronounced “1 prime”), followed by 2 (“2 prime”), and so on. The 5 and 3 carbon atoms play a role in identifying the start and end, respectively, of a strand of DNA, as you will learn next.

From nucleotides to DNA

DNA molecules are built by joining nucleotides together in a linear sequence. The distance between each nitrogen base pair is 0.34 nm.

The nucleotides attach to each other by connecting the sugar group of one nucleotide to the phosphate group of the next. The deoxyribose and phosphate molecules are joined together by a covalent bond called a phosphodiester bond, which forms between the third and fifth carbon atoms of adjacent sugar rings. It is the sugar-to-phosphate linkage that forms the backbone of the DNA molecule.

DNA is made from two anti-parallel DNA strands with 5’ and 3’ ends

The two ends of DNA strands are called the 5’ (“five prime”) and 3’ (“three prime”) ends. This is because the first nucleotide on the DNA strand has a phosphate group attached to the 5’ carbon in the sugar, so it is called the 5’ end. The last nucleotide on the strand has a hydroxyl group (OH) in the 3’ position of the sugar, so this is called the 3’ end. Because the phosphate always joins at the 3’ end of the sugar as nucleotides are added, this means that the DNA strand has a direction.

Nucleotide sequences are always read from the 5’ end to the 3’ end. So, for example, in the figure below the gene sequence on the left would be read as 5’–ACTG–3’ rather than as 3’ – GTCA–5’.

The spiral or double helix structure of DNA

Now we'll cover more about the structure of DNA. The image here shows a short section of a piece of DNA in its natural double helix or “twisted ladder” configuration

  • Each nucleotide will quickly attract its complementary nucleotide to form a base pair, DNA will not remain as a single strand, under normal conditions.
  • DNA usually consists of two strands, each a complement of the other, with one having the 5’ end at the top, and the other having the 3’ end at the top. Therefore, each strand is read in an opposite direction.
  • The reason why paired DNA strands twist into a spiral or double helix shape is because of the bond angles between the phosphate and sugar, as well as between the nucleotides. By twisting into this shape, the molecule achieves its most stable configuration.
  • The amount of coiling will depend on chemical conditions and temperature, but under normal conditions, the helix makes one complete turn about every 10 nucleotides. This means that each full helical twist is about 3.4 nm long.

Although each individual nucleotide is very small, DNA can be a very large molecule because it may contain millions of nucleotide base pairs. For instance, the largest human chromosome, chromosome number 1, is made up of one large coiled strand of DNA that is approximately 245 million base pairs long.

Extraction of DNA

DNA extraction is an important laboratory technique that allows scientists to isolate DNA to study its structure, properties and sequence. The process of isolating the DNA from the tissue is called DNA extraction.

In DNA extraction the following steps are performed on tissues containing the target DNA:

Step 1

First physically broken up to make it easier to get at the DNA.

Step 2

Second, a solution is added, called a lysis solution (“lysis” means to break up), which is sometimes also called a buffer. The solution breaks the remaining cells apart, releasing the DNA from the nucleus and other organelles. (Heating up the lysis and tissue solution will break down any enzymes that degrade DNA).

Step 3

Third, the DNA is physically separated from the other chemicals in the solution through a filtering process. (Filter paper or a centrifuge can be used).

Step 4

Fourth, after filtering, the DNA can be further purified by repeated washing with water and filtering. The result will be a clear fluid containing water and dissolved DNA, ready for any type of analysis.

Lab Activity: Extraction of DNA

Extraction of DNA

Strawberries are an ideal fruit from which to isolate DNA, since they are octoploid, meaning that they contain eight copies of their DNA. Most organisms are diploid, containing only two sets of DNA, one from each of their parents. So, using strawberries gives you more DNA to work with. This is an optional activity. To extract DNA from the tissues of a strawberry plant, using a four-stage process. After completing this lab, you will have a sample of pure strawberry DNA that you can hold in your hand.

Ripe strawberries are used because they contain enzymes (pectinases and cellulases) that help to break down the cell walls, making it easier for you to extract the DNA.

From the video, you saw that the museum scientists extracted DNA using a process that included heating and cooling. This ensures that the sample is as undamaged and pure as possible. However, here you are only concerned with extracting DNA for visual observation, so it does not have to be of high purity, which is why this simpler procedure does not require heating and cooling.

  • Resealable plastic bag
  • Scissors
  • One medium-sized ripe strawberry (it is best if it is slightly soft)
  • 10mL of dishwashing liquid (about 2 teaspoons)
  • 3g of table salt (about 1/2 teaspoon)
  • Water
  • 50mL of 70–95% isopropyl alcohol (also called “rubbing alcohol”), which is available at any drugstore. Place the alcohol in the freezer for at least one hour to ensure that it is cold. If you do not have access to a freezer, put the alcohol in the refrigerator overnight to get it as cold as possible.
  • Coffee filter (or a thick piece of paper towel that doesn’t have any visible holes)
  • Small sieve with a small bowl over which the sieve can fit. If no sieve is available, then use a coffee cup with the filter paper placed over the top of it.
  • A 250mL or 500mL measuring cup
  • One small clear glass
  • One plastic stir stick

You will extract the DNA using a four-stage process, with each stage involving several steps. In the first stage, you will break open the cells of the ripe strawberry by crushing it. Second, you will use a lysis solution to break down the cellular and nuclear membranes to separate the DNA from the other cell parts. Third, you will filter out the solid material using a coffee filter or a thick piece of paper towel and collect the liquid containing the DNA. In the fourth and final stage, you will precipitate the DNA from the solution using very cold alcohol.

  • Prepare the lysis solution: In a measuring cup, dissolve the salt in about 50mL of water. Add the 10mL of dishwashing liquid and then add the remaining water to make 100mL of solution. Gently stir but do not shake; you do not want a lot of bubbles.
  • Obtain a ripe strawberry. If the green leaves (sepals) are still on the strawberry, remove them.
  • Place the strawberry in the resealable plastic bag. Press the air out and seal it.
  • Gently mash the bagged strawberry with your fingers for five minutes. Don’t break the bag.
  • Add 10mL of lysis solution to the bag. Press the air out carefully and reseal the bag.
  • Mash the bagged strawberry with the lysis solution for two minutes. This will separate out the DNA from the other cell parts.

Important: Mash the strawberry and lysis mixture carefully to avoid creating bubbles. The fewer bubbles you create, the better your results will be.

  • Prepare your filter apparatus. If you have a sieve and a small bowl, place the sieve across the bowl mouth so that any liquid can drip through the sieve and be caught in the bowl below. Lay the coffee filter paper or piece of paper towel onto the sieve screen. If you do not have a sieve handy, you can just use a coffee cup and place the coffee filter or paper towel over the top of the cup, but you may have to hold onto it as you start pouring.
  • Cut one of the bottom corners off the bag and squeeze the strawberry pulp, along with the liquid, onto the coffee filter or paper towel.
  • After the pulp mixture has been filtering for about a minute or two, pour approximately 10mL of water over the pulp on the filter. This rinsing will ensure that you obtain as much DNA as possible.
  • Once the filtering appears complete, dispose of the bag, filter, and strawberry pulp into appropriate waste containers. Do not eat the mashed strawberry mixture as it contains dishwashing liquid.
  • Pour the filtrate into a small, clear drinking glass.
  • Raise the glass to eye level so that you can see what is happening and slowly drizzle the ice-cold isopropyl alcohol along the inside of the glass until DNA strands begin to appear in the solution. Do this carefully as you do not want the alcohol to mix with the filtrate. The appearance of DNA is called precipitation.
  • Observe the characteristics of the DNA as it precipitates.
  • Once it appears that no more DNA is going to precipitate out, you can begin to collect the DNA on the plastic stir stick. Insert the stir stick into the mixture and slowly wind it around. You will notice that strands of material are wrapping themselves around the stick. Pull out the stir stick and observe. Those strands are the strawberry’s DNA.
  • When finished, dispose of the materials safely. Pour the contents of the glass down the drain, along with running water. Do not drink any of the contents as they contain isopropyl alcohol, which is mildly poisonous.

Why did you have to mash the strawberry before adding the lysis solution?

What did the lysis solution do in the extraction process?

Why did you rinse the filter paper?

To wash the DNA out of the filter paper, since DNA is soluble in water
