Building macromolecules

Macromolecules are large biological molecules. Biochemical reactions usually involve using building blocks or smaller repeating units known as monomers to build macromolecules or breaking down macromolecules to form the monomers.

Building Macromolecules: Condensation Reactions

In order to join monomers to form macromolecules or polymers, water must be removed in a condensation reaction. It is also sometimes called a dehydration synthesis reaction (because the molecule becomes “dehydrated”) and an anabolic (building-up) reaction occurs, since larger units are formed from smaller units.

Breaking down macromolecules

In order to break down macromolecules into smaller units, a catabolic (breaking down) reaction called hydrolysis must occur. In this reaction, water is used to break down the macromolecule into its smaller monomer units.

These reactions take place with the help of enzymes, which are protein catalysts that speed up chemical reactions. The role of enzymes will be discussed in more detail in a later lesson. Hydrolysis and Condensation are the vital biological reactions neceessary to make and break the four macromolecules of life listed below.

  1. Carbohydrates
  2. Lipids
  3. Proteins
  4. Nucleic acids

Carbohydrates

Carbohydrates are used as:

  1. Building materials for energy
  2. Cell identification and communication.

Carbohydrates contain: Carbon, Hydrogen, and Oxygen in a 1:2:1 ratio.

Carbohydrates are a vital part of the food groups humans must eat each day to remain healthy and active. Examples of carbohydrates humans ingest are bread, cereal, pasta and grains. Carbohydrates are classified into two groups:

  1. Simple carbohydrates: Monosaccharides, Disaccharides, Oligosaccharides

Simple carbohydrates

Monosaccharides

  • Ribose, Glucose and Galactose are Aldose Monosaccharides
  • Ribulose and Fructose are Ketose Monosaccharides

Glucose is the most important monosaccharide and it is the body’s preferred source of energy.

Disaccharides and oligosaccharides

Many of the sugars in the foods we ingest are disaccharides, sugars made up by combining two monosaccharides. Table sugar is sucrose, which is made from glucose and fructose.

Disaccharides are formed when two monosaccharides combine by a covalent bond, called a glycosidic linkage, from a dehydration synthesis or condensation reaction. A glycosidic linkage is the covalent bond that forms between carbohydrates or carbohydrates and another group.

Oligosaccharides (“oligo-” means “few”) contain three to ten simple sugars, attached to one another by covalent bonds, called glycosidic linkages and are also formed from dehydration synthesis.

The word equations for the formation of the disaccharides lactose, maltose and sucrose are summarized below:

Glucose + glucose → maltose + water

Glucose + fructose → sucrose + water

Glucose + galactose → lactose + water

Complex carbohydrates

Polysaccharides

Polysaccharides are polymers composed of several hundred to several thousand monosaccharide subunits, held together by glycosidic linkages.

Complex carbohydrates are very important in our diet and we ingest them as breads, cereals, pasta and grains for our daily energy requirements and convert the excess into storage energy.

These carbohydrates also provide much of nature’s building material such as in plants, insects and crustaceans.

There are four types of polysaccharides:

  • Starch
  • Glycogen
  • Cellulose
  • Chitin

Alpha (α) 1-4 glycosidic bonds means that the carbon-1 on the first carbohydrate and the carbon-4 on the other carbohydrate are covalently bonded and the –OH group on the Carbon-1 is below the glucose ring

Beta (β) 1-4 glycosidic bonds means that the carbon-1 on the first carbohydrate and the carbon-4 on the other carbohydrate are covalently bonded and the –OH group on the Carbon-1 is above the glucose ring.

The four types of complex carbohydrates
  • Starch
  • Glycogen
  • Cellulose
  • Chitin

Starch is composed of amylose (with α1-4 links) and amylopectin (with α1-6 links).

It is the primary energy storage molecule for plants. Root vegetables, such as carrots and potatoes, have an abundance of starch, which humans can digest for energy.

Starch is a polysaccharide combining the unbranched amylose (top) with the branched amylopectin (bottom). The carbons 1, 4, and 6 are numbered in the first two glucoses of amylose as a reference. Amylose is linked together using the 1 and 4 carbons. Note the linkage across two monosaccharides (from the 1 carbon to the 6 carbon), which creates a branch in the amylopectin.

Like starch, glycogen is composed of α1-4 links, and α1-6 links where it branches, but glycogen is more branched than starch.

It is the main energy storage molecule for animals. Glycogen regulates blood glucose levels. As glucose increases, the liver and muscles convert excess glucose to glycogen. Enzymes in the liver and muscles can also break glycogen molecules apart, when glucose is required.

For example, adrenalin stimulates the breakdown of chemical bonds in a glycogen chain for quick energy, such as that needed for an animal’s “flight or fight” reaction when it is confronted with

The polysaccharide glycogen is the storage form of glucose in animals. It is a long molecule with many branches. In humans, it is typically stored in the liver and muscles.

Cellulose is composed of β1-4 links. Every other glucose subunit becomes inverted to accommodate this link. Unlike starch and glycogen, it is not coiled or branched. This allows cellulose to form long chains that are tough, making it an ideal building material for plants.

For example, it is used in plant cell walls, to make them rigid and firm.

Humans cannot digest cellulose. However, it is a form of fibre, which is an important part of our daily diet, as it aids in regular egestion (bowel movements).

Humans also use cellulose to make paper and fabrics. Cellulose is the tough “thread” that plants use to make them rigid, and that humans began to use long ago as a manufacturing material.

Cellulose is a polysaccharide composed of β1-4 links. Notice that every glucose molecule is upside down, in relation to its neighbours.

Chitin is a cellulose-like polymer of N-acetylglucosamine. The monomer is a glucose molecule with a nitrogen-containing group attached at the second carbon position.

Chitin is used by insects and crustaceans (for example, shrimp, lobster, and crabs) to form their hard exoskeletons.

Since chitin is such a strong material, we use it to manufacture items such as surgical thread.

Two N-acetylglucosamine joined together. Many of these together makes chitin.

Proteins

Proteins:

  • are involved in almost everything cells do.
  • can be enzymes, immunoglobulin, hemoglobin, keratin, fibrin, and so on.
  • are made up of many amino acid monomers. The number, type and arrangement of the amino acids determine what type of protein is produced.

Amino acids:

  • Amino acids are the building blocks (or monomers) of proteins.
  • There are 20 different amino acids that make up proteins.
  • The 20 amino acids consist of three distinct parts which are all attached to a central carbon atom. Every amino acid contains:
    • An amino group (NH2)
    • A carboxylic acid group (COOH)
    • A radical group (denoted by the letter “R”) which is sometimes called a side chain. These “R” groups or sides chains make each amino acid unique because they are a group of atoms which make the amino acid polar (hydrophilic), non-polar (hydrophobic), or charged (acidic/basic).
    • Nine of the amino acids are essential, meaning that we must obtain these from our diet, while manufacturing the other 12 in our cells.

Amino acids contain a carboxylic acid group, an amino group, and a unique “R” group

Protein structure

Once proteins are formed, they fold up into compact shapes. Although the shape of each protein is generally globular (like rolled-up balls), these shapes are still very specific, enabling them to carry out their specialized functions within the cells.

The final shape of a protein is also called its conformation. The conformation is the result of the amino acid sequence it contains and the interactions among those amino acids.

There are four stages of protein structure possible.

The four possible levels of protein organization, as the polypeptide chain twists and folds into its final conformation

Primary (1°) structure

When proteins are first assembled, they are said to be in their primary (1°) structure. This is a simple chain of amino acids joined together by peptide bonds, or a “polypeptide chain,” as it is commonly called. The sequence of amino acids is determined by the nucleotide (DNA) sequence of a particular gene (you will learn more about this in the next section). With 20 different amino acids, there are endless possibilities for the primary structure. For instance, in a protein containing X number of amino acids, the number of possibilities is 20x.

The primary structure of a proteins is like the letters of the alphabet joined together in a pattern.

Secondary (2°) structure

As the amino acid assembly process creates the primary structure, the protein chain begins folding and coiling as it grows. This is called its secondary (2°) structure. Secondary structures are formed by hydrogen bonds between the oxygen atoms of a carboxyl group and the hydrogen atoms of an amino group. There are two types of secondary structures:

α helix: A tight coil produced by hydrogen bonds occurring at every fourth peptide bond. This bonding is repeated along the entire length of the polypeptide to form a twisted “rope.”

β pleated sheets: Hydrogen bonds formed between parallel stretches of a polypeptide to form sheets

The secondary structure of a proteins is like the letters of the alphabet joining together to make words.

Tertiary (3°) structure

The polypeptide chain undergoes additional folding, due to side- chain (“R”-group) interactions. The most common are covalent bonds between sulphur atoms, called disulphide bridges. These bridges help to form the globular structure called the tertiary (3°) structure. All proteins eventually settle into their tertiary structure.

The tertiary structure of a proteins is like the letters of the alphabet joining together to make words in a sentence or phrase.

Quaternary (4°) structure

In some cases, two or more polypeptides join together to make a functional protein. In this case, the final quaternary (4°) structure depends on how the two or more polypeptides fold together. Example of quaternary structures are collagen and hemoglobin.

The quaternary structure of a proteins is like sentences that now form short statements or paragraphs.

Lipids

Lipids

Lipids (fats) are:

  • a group of organic molecules that dissolve in non-polar substances, but generally not in water (insoluble in water).
  • very efficient energy-storage molecules that yield about twice the amount of chemical energy per gram than carbohydrates or proteins.
  • important because they provide physical and thermal insulation for the body, are key components in cell membranes, and act as raw materials for the synthesis of hormones.
  • mostly made up of hydrogen, carbon, and oxygen.
  • some lipids form chains, while the others form rings.

There are four families of lipids:

  1. Fats
  2. Phospholipids
  3. Steroids
  4. Waxes

Fats

Fats are the most common form of energy storage and insulation in plants and animals.

Fats are composed of:

  • one glycerol molecule
  • a maximum of three fatty-acid chains

Each fatty-acid chain contains between 16 and 18 carbons and has a terminal carboxylic acid.

What type of chemical reaction do you think will take place when a fat molecule is formed?

A condensation/dehydration synthesis, anabolic reaction


As you may have just predicted, a fat molecule is formed by:

  • A dehydration synthesis (condensation) reaction occurs as a molecule of water is removed when the (-OH) group on the glycerol combines with the (-H) from the (-COOH) group in the fatty acid chain.
  • The new linkage that attaches the fatty acids to the glycerol after a molecule of water is removed in the condensation reaction is called an ester linkage and the process is called esterification.
  • If the fat is made up of one fatty-acid chain, it is called a monoglyceride, two fatty-acid chains, it is called a diglyceride and three fatty-acid chains, it is called a triglyceride.
Triglycerides are formed by dehydration synthesis (condensation) reactions. Water is produced, and the molecule contains ester linkages.

Phospholipids

Phospholipids make up the cell membranes of an animal cell.
Phospholipids consist of a hydrophilic head and a hydrophobic two-stranded tail. They are composed of one glycerol, two fatty acids, and a highly polar phosphate group.
The phospholipid bilayer in cells is virtually impermeable to macromolecules, relatively impermeable to charged ions, and quite permeable to small, lipid-soluble molecules.

Steroids

Sterols are a subgroup of steroids which are also lipids.

Sterols are compact hydrophobic molecules containing four fused hydrocarbon rings.

Types of sterols include cholesterol and many other important hormones.

Cholesterol is an important component of cell membranes because it affects the membrane’s fluidity, it provides support to the membrane and acts as a messenger in cell communication during development. Cholesterol can also be converted into vitamin D. Too much cholesterol in your diet can lead to clogged arteries and cardiovascular disease.
Steroid molecules are derivatives of the molecule cholesterol. Other steroid compounds, such as sex hormones, control the development of sex traits and gametes (egg and sperm cells).

Waxes

Waxes are:
  • hydrophobic molecules that contain long-chain fatty acids linked to alcohols or carbon rings.
  • Waxes often form waterproof coatings, such as beeswax, paraffin, and the cuticle on plants’ leaves.
  • Waxes are used to manufacture items such as fuel, candles, and furniture polish

Nucleic acids

Nucleic acids are found in:

  • DNA (deoxyribonucleic acid)
  • RNA (ribonucleic acid)
  • ATP (adenosine triphosphate)
  • Nucleotide coenzymes (NAD+, NADP+, and FAD)

Nucleic Acids are composed of nucleotides. DNA and RNA are called nucleotide polymers and they are made up of monomers called nucleotides.

General Nucleotide Structure:

  • Nucleotides consist of a nitrogenous base, a five-carbon sugar, and a phosphate group.
  • The phosphate and ribose groups are joined together by a phosphodiester linkage. You will learn more about this important linkage in later lessons.
Nucleic acids are composed of nucleotides, which are made up of a sugar, a phosphate, and a nitrogen base.

A closer look at the nucleotides

There are five nitrogenous nucleotide bases. These are amongst the most famous molecules in biology because they are the alphabet of life and their arrangement in DNA is the blueprint or code that dictates everything for building and maintaining an organism.   • Cytosine, thymine, and uracil are called pyrimidines and have a single-ring structure.

Title

Testing food for macromolecules


Purpose

To test unknown substances for the presence of protein, starch, simple sugars and fat.


Materials

  • Test tube rack
  • 12 test tubes
  • 7 pipettes or droppers
  • Benedicts solution
  • Iodine
  • Sudan IV indicator
  • Biuret’s reagent
  • Sample of substance A
  • Sample of substance B
  • Sample of substance C
  • Wax pencil to label test tubes
  • Hot plate
  • Beaker
  • Water
  • Test tube tongs
  • Gloves
  • Goggles

Method

Simple Sugar Test - Benedict’s

  1. Put a 3 mL sample of each unknown substance into labelled test tubes (A, B, C)
  2. Put 5 drops of Benedicts into each sample and mix
  3. Put each test tube into a boiling water bath (beaker of water boiling on a hot plate) for 10 minutes
  4. Remove test tubes using tongs/holder and put into test tube rack
  5. Record observations

Starch Test - Iodine

  1. Put a 3 mL sample of each unknown substance into labelled test tubes (A, B, C)
  2. Put 5 drops of iodine into each sample and mix/swirl
  3. Record observations

Lipid Test - Sudan IV

  1. Put a 3 mL sample of each unknown substance into labelled test tubes (A, B, C)
  2. Put 2 drops of Sudan IV solution into each sample and mix/swirl
  3. Record observations

Protein Test - Biuret’s

  1. Put a 3 mL sample of each unknown substance into labelled test tubes (A, B, C)
  2. Put 5 drops of Biuret’s reagent into each sample and mix/swirl
  3. Record observations
