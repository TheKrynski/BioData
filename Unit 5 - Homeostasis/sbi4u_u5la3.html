Introduction

The glands of the endocrine system and the hormones they release influence almost every cell, organ, and function of your body. It is crucial to maintaining homeostasis in internal systems.

The endocrine system is instrumental in:

  • regulating mood
  • growth and development
  • tissue function
  • metabolism
  • sexual function
  • reproductive processes

Importance of the Endocrine System

The endocrine system is a system of glandular tissues that produce and secrete special messenger molecules called hormones, into the blood. The word “hormone” means “to excite or set into motion.” Hormones are often produced in one part of the body and then travel in the blood to a target tissue cell, bind to it, and affect it in some way. Epinephrine (adrenalin)—is produced in times of stress to promote action responses

Types of Hormones: Steroid and Protein

Hormones don’t affect all cells. Cells may have receptors for one hormone, but not for another. For example, liver cells and muscle cells have many receptor sites for insulin, whereas less active cells, like bone and cartilage cells, have fewer receptors for insulin.

There are two types of hormones that differ, both in chemical structure and action: steroid and protein hormones.

Steroid Hormones

Steroid hormones are derivatives of cholesterol. The three types of steroid hormones and their structure are illustrated below. They are composed of complex, fused rings of carbon, hydrogen, and oxygen molecules. Steroid hormones are not soluble in water but are soluble in fat.

The mechanism of action for steroid hormones

These hormones diffuse from the capillaries into the interstitial fluid and then into the target cell.

  1. The hormones combine with receptor molecules located in the cytoplasm.
  2. This hormone-receptor complex then moves into the nucleus of the cell.
  3. The hormone-receptor complex and attaches to a segment of DNA that has a complementary shape, causing transcription of an mRNA strand.
  4. The newly produced mRNA diffuses out of the nucleus and into the cytoplasm where it is translated into a specific protein.

Protein hormones

Protein hormones include insulin and growth hormone.

  • They contain chains of amino acids that vary in length.
  • They are soluble in water.
  • They bind at specific receptor sites

The mechanism of action for protein hormones

Proteins must work in a different way from steroid hormones as they cannot cross the phospholipid bilayer easily. Some form a hormone-receptor complex that activates the production of an enzyme called adenyl cyclase, which, in turn, causes the cell to convert ATP into cyclic adenosine monophosphate (cyclic AMP). The cyclic AMP functions as a messenger, activating enzymes in the cytoplasm to carry out their normal functions.

Pituitary Gland

The pituitary gland is often called “the master gland,” because it exercises control over other endocrine glands. It also produces and stores several hormones. It is a small sac-like structure that is connected to the hypothalamus. The pituitary gland has two lobes: the posterior lobe and the anterior lobe.

The pituitary gland is controlled by the hypothalamus, which is part of the brain and the nervous system.

Recall from an earlier learning activity, where you learned about antidiuretic hormone (ADH), the hormone responsible for increasing water reabsorption into the blood. The pituitary gland stores and releases ADH, produced by the hypothalamus. ADH acts on the kidneys and helps regulate water balance in the body. ADH and other hormones, such as oxytocin, travel by way of specialized nerve cells from the hypothalamus to the pituitary gland. The pituitary gland stores the hormones, releasing them in the blood, when necessary.

Hormones that regulate blood sugar levels

The cells that produce hormones in the pancreas are located in structures called the islets of Langerhans. There are more than 2,000 tiny islets, each containing thousands of beta or alpha cells. The beta cells produce the hormone insulin, and the alpha cells produce the hormone glucagon.

Pancreatic hormones: Glucagon and insulin

The hormones glucagon and insulin, which are both secreted by the pancreas, have opposite effects in response to high or low blood glucose levels. Hormones that have opposite effects are called antagonistic hormones. Explore the table below to learn more about the action of both these hormones in response to varying blood glucose levels.

Blood Glucose Levels High (hyperglycemia) Low (hypoglycemia)
Time frame After a meal
  • Long period after last meal
  • Fasting
  • Not enough food ingested
Body’s response to glucose levels Insulin is released from the beta cells in your pancreas Glucagon is released by the alpha cells of the islets of Langerhans.
Result of body’s response
  • Cells in your muscles, liver, and other organs to become more permeable to glucose.
  • When the glucose is drawn out of your blood, this lowers your blood glucose level.
  • Once in your liver, the glucose is converted to glycogen (the storage form of glucose).
 During periods of fasting, glucagon is released and promotes the conversion of glycogen to glucose, which is released into the blood, thus raising the blood sugar level.

Diabetes mellitus

An individual with diabetes mellitus cannot produce enough insulin to regulate blood glucose levels. If left untreated, diabetes can cause blindness, kidney failure, nerve damage, and in extreme cases, the need for limb amputation.

If a deficiency of insulin exists, blood sugar rises substantially, resulting in hyperglycemia or high blood sugar. The symptoms associated with hyperglycemia are:

  1. Glucose in the urine.
  2. Dehydration and thirst caused by glucose in the nephron, which in turn draws water out of the blood through osmosis.
  3. Low energy levels because of a lack of insulin, the permeability of glucose into the cells does not increase, so less glucose is burned and less ATP is made.

The adrenal gland and stress hormones

During times of stress, your body may need to tap into energy reserves. At such times, your adrenal glands release hormones that trigger the release of sugar into the blood.

The adrenal glands are found above each kidney. Each adrenal gland has:

  • an outer cortex
  • an inner medulla

The medulla releases epinephrine (adrenalin) and norepinephrine, which respond to short-term stress.

The Body’s physiological response to short-term and long-term stress

The following describes the body’s physiological response to short-term and long-term stress.

  • Short-term stress - Adrenal medulla
  • Long-term stress - Adrenal cortex

Hormones secreted

  • Epinephrine
  • Norepinephrine

Physiological response pathway

  • Epinephrine and norepinephrine are released into the blood when nerve signals are sent from the hypothalamus via the nervous system.

Physiological response

  • Blood sugar level rises, as glycogen is quickly converted to glucose for a quick energy source.
  • The heart rate, breathing rate, and cell metabolism increase.
  • The blood vessels dilate, allowing more oxygen and nutrients to reach the tissues.
  • The iris of the eye dilates to allow more light to reach the retina, which, in turn, increases visual perception and awareness of the external environment.

Hormones secreted

  • Glucocorticoids (e.g. cortisol)
  • Mineralocorticoids (e.g. aldosterone)

Physiological response pathway

  • The hypothalamus sends a releasing hormone to the anterior lobe of the pituitary gland, which stimulates the release of a hormone called adrenocorticotropic hormone (ACTH).
  • The blood carries the ACTH to the adrenal cortex.
  • The ACTH stimulates the adrenal cortex to release glucocorticoids as well as mineralocorticoids, which are, in turn, carried to target cells in the liver and muscles.

Physiological response

  • Effects of glucocorticoids (e.g. cortisol)
  • Proteins and fats are broken down to glucose
  • Liver converts amino acids and fatty acids to glucose thus elevating blood glucose levels
  • Effects of mineralocorticoids (e.g. aldosterone)
  • Aldosterone increases sodium retention and water reabsorption by the kidneys, thereby helping to maintain body fluid level.
  • Increase blood volume and blood pressure
  • Partial suppression of the immune system

In general, short-term stress hormones provide more blood glucose to help the body cope with increased energy requirements, as outlined below

Reproductive hormones

Reproductive hormones control the development of reproductive systems during growth, as well as their use when sexually mature. Each sex has its own set of reproductive hormones.

Male reproductive hormones

Male hormones are called androgens (male sex hormone). The main androgen is testosterone.

Testosterone:

  • is manufactured by the interstitial cells of the testes.
  • secretion increases sharply at puberty and is responsible for the development of the so-called secondary sexual characteristics (for example, beard growth, deepening of the voice, pubic hair development, and so on) of men.
  • is also essential for the production of sperm (spermatogenesis). Testosterone is also associated with sex drive.

The male reproductive system is illustrated in the figure below:

Production of sperm

The hypothalamus and the pituitary gland in the brain control the production of sperm and male sex hormones in the testes. This is a negative feedback mechanism as seen in the diagram below.

Here are detailed steps for the production of sperm:

  • At puberty, the hypothalamus secretes the gonadotropin-releasing hormone (GnRH).
  • GnRH activates the pituitary gland to secrete and release follicle-stimulating hormone (FSH) and luteinizing hormone (LH).
  • FSH acts directly on the sperm-producing cells of the seminiferous tubules.
  • LH stimulates the interstitial cells to produce testosterone.
  • Testosterone increases sperm production.
  • When high levels of testosterone result, the hypothalamus detects it and regulates the process through negative feedback.
  • It is believed that the FSH acts on support cells, known as Sertoli cells, which produce a peptide hormone called inhibin that sends a feedback message to the pituitary gland, inhibiting production of FSH.

Female reproductive hormones

The female’s reproductive system is more complicated than that of the male. Males are constantly producing sperm, while females develop one mature ovum each month.

At puberty, females contain about 400,000 follicles. In each female reproductive cycle, many follicles develop in a process called oogenesis. However, usually only a single follicle becomes dominant and reaches maturity during a reproductive cycle. In total, approximately 400 eggs will mature during a female’s reproductive life.

The actual age of the follicles can be linked to the higher incidence of genetic defects in children produced by older women. This means that the longer the follicle lives, the greater the chance of genetic damage. Menopause marks the end of a female’s reproductive life and signals a drop in the production of female hormones.

When a woman is in the reproductive phase of her life, she undergoes ovulation once a month.

Ovulation is the process of releasing a mature haploid egg (which contains 23 chromosomes instead of 46).

  • The hormone FSH is released from the pituitary gland and stimulates the follicle (an immature egg with 46 chromosomes) to develop into a haploid (a 23-chromosome) egg.
  • Luteinizing hormone (LH), also released by the pituitary gland, causes the follicle to be released into the fallopian tube or oviduct.
  • The surrounding follicle cells remain within the ovary and are transformed into the corpus luteum, which secretes the hormones necessary for pregnancy, only if the egg is fertilized within 10 days.

If the egg is fertilized, the embryo then becomes attached to the lining of the uterus (implantation). The cells of the placenta which nourishes the egg produce a hormone called the Human chorionic gonadotropin (hCG). This hormone will build up rapidly until about 11 weeks of pregnancy. Both urine and blood pregnancy tests can detect the presence of hCG at about 12–14 days after conception.

Menstrual cycle

The entire menstrual cycle takes 28 days, with some degree of variation. The cycle is divided into four distinct phases, as shown in the figure below.

Here is what is happening at each of the four stages:

  1. Flow phase—this is the shedding of the endometrium, or menstruation. This phase marks the beginning of the menstrual cycle and lasts for approximately 5 days.
  2. Follicular phase—this phase is characterized by the development of the follicles within the ovary. As the follicle cells develop, the hormone estrogen is secreted, and its concentration increases. This takes place during days 6 to 13 of the cycle.
  3. Ovulatory phase—estrogen levels decline and the egg bursts from the ovary and follicle cells differentiate into the corpus luteum.
  4. Luteal phase—in this phase, estrogen levels are restored as the corpus luteum develops. This occurs between days 15 and 28 and prepares the uterus to receive a fertilized egg, as the corpus luteum releases both estrogen and progesterone. These hormones stimulate the endometrium (lining of the uterus or womb) and prepare the uterus for an embryo. They also inhibit further ovulation and prevent uterine contractions from occurring. If progesterone levels fall, uterine contractions begin. If fertilization does not occur, the concentration of estrogen and progesterone drops, causing the endometrium to shed. This shedding marks the end of the cycle and the beginning of the next.

Hormonal control of the female reproductive system

The hypothalamus-pituitary complex regulates the production of estrogen and progesterone, the hormones in the ovary as illustrated in the diagram below.

Both FSH and LH regulate the control of estrogen and progesterone. The amount of estrogen and progesterone, in turn, regulates the release of FSH and LH from the pituitary gland in a negative feedback loop.

  • At puberty, GnRH is released from the hypothalamus.
  • GnRH activates the pituitary gland, which releases FSH and LH.
  • FSH travels through the bloodstream and stimulates follicle development. The follicles then secrete estrogen, which initiates the development of the endometrium.
  • LH stimulates ovulation and the remaining follicular cells to become the corpus luteum.
  • The corpus luteum secretes both estrogen and progesterone, which both further develop the endometrium.
  • As these two hormones build up, a second negative feedback mechanism causes FSH and LH to become inhibited, which causes the corpus luteum to degenerate, and menstruation begins.

Hormones affecting metabolism

Have you ever known anyone who can eat copious amounts of food and never put on any weight? Or perhaps you know someone who must closely monitor their food intake to remain at a healthy weight. An individual’s metabolism is largely influenced by the endocrine system.

There are three different kinds of glands that affect metabolism:

  1. Thyroid gland - regulates body metabolism and the rate at which blood glucose is metabolized (hormones: triiodothyronine, thyroxine, and calcitonin).
  2. Parathyroid glands - regulate calcium ion levels in the blood (hormone: parathyroid hormone).
  3. Anterior pituitary gland - influences the growth of long bones and accelerates protein synthesis (hormone: growth hormone (somatotropin)).

Thyroid gland

The thyroid gland is located at the base of the neck, in front of the windpipe and trachea.

The thyroid secretes three hormones:

  • Thyroxine (T4) and triiodothyronine (T3) which both regulate body metabolism and the growth and differentiation of tissues.
  • Calcitonin which acts on the bone cells to lower the level of calcium ions found in the blood. More on this in the next section.

Here, we will focus on the metabolism regulating hormones of thyroxine (T4) and triiodothyronine (T3).

Negative feedback mechanism of T4 and T3

The control of T4 and T3 occurs through a process of negative feedback. This process is summarized below.

  1. Receptors in the hypothalamus are activated when the metabolic rate decreases.
  2. Nerve cells secrete thyroid-releasing hormone (TRH).
  3. TRH stimulates the pituitary gland to release TSH.
  4. TSH is carried through the blood and reaches the thyroid gland, which, in turn, releases T4 and T3.
  5. T4 and T3 concentrations increase in the blood and stimulate sugar oxidation.
  6. When levels of thyroxine and triiodothyronine increase to a critical point, they cause the pathway to be “turned off ” by inhibiting the release of TRH from the hypothalamus, thus stopping the production of TSH in the pituitary gland (negative feedback).

Health conditions associated with the thyroid: Hyperthyroidism and hypothyroidism

Hyperthyroidism

Individuals who produce high levels of T4 and T3 have a condition called hyperthyroidism. These people may lose weight and may often feel warm, due to the higher respiratory rates they experience because of increased glucose oxidation.

Hypothyroidism

Conversely, those who gain weight easily due to low levels of T4 and T3 production have hypothyroidism. In this condition, glucose is not consumed at normal rates, and it gets stored in the liver. Once the liver reserves are full, the body converts sugar into fat. People who secrete low levels of T4 and T3 often experience muscle weakness, cold intolerance, and have dry skin and hair.

Parathyroid glands

The parathyroid glands secrete parathyroid hormone (PTH). PTH regulates blood calcium levels and is another example of a negative feedback system. Low blood calcium levels trigger the release of PTH.

What happens when there is a decrease in blood calcium levels?

Parathyroid hormone will work to increase the calcium levels using three methods:

  1. Bones: It increases the blood calcium levels by accelerating the breakdown of bones to produce higher calcium levels.
  2. Kidneys: causes reabsorb calcium ions from the filtrate to increase levels.
  3. Small intestine: causes absorption of calcium ions from undigested food to increase levels.
What happens when there is an increase in blood calcium levels?

The thyroid gland produces calcitonin which acts on bone cells to decrease the loss of calcium from bones. Thus, decreasing the levels of calcium in the blood.

Anterior pituitary gland

The anterior pituitary gland secretes growth hormone (GH), which stimulates cell division. Low secretions of GH during childhood can result in dwarfism, while high secretions can result in gigantism.

BPA: Are water bottles safe?

A recent controversy in the food packing industry is the use of the chemical BPA or bisphenol A, as many people regard it as a health hazard. BPA has been used for over 50 years in the plastics industry and has many uses. It is found in many hard plastic toys, bottles, and food containers. It is also used to line the cans used for canned goods, where it acts as a seal to prevent rust from contaminating the food. Recently, many scientists and consumer groups have questioned the safety of using BPA, especially in food packaging such as water bottles or food containers.

Critics of the use of BPA say that it may be linked to obesity, infertility, and insulin- resistance in rodents. Some studies done on animals suggest that BPA may mimic estrogen and other hormones when ingested, causing normal hormone functioning to be disrupted. This has led to concern that BPA may cause early-onset puberty, and possibly breast and prostate cancer in adults. It may also be linked to causing aggression in toddlers.

In the past, the Canadian FDA (Food and Drug Administration) proclaimed BPA to be safe, but recent political action has been taken to initiate further studies investigating the long-term effects of BPA. For now, it is still uncertain whether BPA is safe.

In response to growing consumer concern, some Canadian-based stores have stopped selling bottles containing BPA. As a consumer, you can purchase alternative containers made of glass, stainless steel, or porcelain. This is particularly important if the containers are to be heated.
