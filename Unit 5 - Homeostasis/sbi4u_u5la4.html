Introduction

As you have learned, homeostasis is about maintaining an optimal internal environment in the body. To do this, your body needs to sense the external environment and then act to restore its optimal internal conditions. Sensing and acting are two specialties of the nervous system.

The nervous system is a specialized organ system that connects the external environment to the internal environment and helps to maintain homeostasis. For example, this girl is running through a water sprinkler to cool down on a summer day because her nervous system senses her internal temperature is rising, and consequently, the action she consciously chooses is to run through a water sprinkler to cool down.

Your nervous system is a network of specialized cells that communicate information about your surroundings and your internal state. It processes this information and causes reactions throughout your body. The nervous system is linked to all systems of the body, including the cardiovascular, respiratory, and endocrine systems. It is crucial to maintaining homeostasis within your body

Overview of the nervous system

Your nervous system is active all the time, even when you are asleep.

It receives information about:

  • the depth of your breathing
  • the pressure on your skin
  • temperature
  • light
  • odours

The nervous system controls:

  • unconscious muscle movements such as blinking and breathing
  • conscious muscle movements like scratching your nose

Other functions of the nervous system:

  • memory
  • thought
  • language

The nervous system has two main divisions:

  • The Central Nervous System (CNS)
  • The Peripheral Nervous System (PNS)

The central nervous system (CNS)

The CNS acts as a coordinating centre for incoming and outgoing information. The CNS consists of the brain and spinal cord.

The peripheral nervous system (PNS)

The PNS carries information between the organs of the body and the CNS. The PNS consists of nerves.

The PNS is further subdivided into the somatic nervous system and the autonomic nervous system.

The somatic nervous system controls the:

  • skeletal muscle
  • bones
  • skin

Somatic nerves can be either:

  • sensory nerves (which relay sensory information)
  • motor nerves (which generate muscle responses)

The autonomic nervous system controls the internal organs of the body.

It is also divided into two parts:

  • the sympathetic nervous system, which controls, for example, the “fight or flight” reaction
  • the parasympathetic nervous system, which controls functions such as sleep and digestion

Anatomy of nerve cells

The nervous system contains two types of nerve cells:

  • Glial cells
  • Neurons

Glial cells

Glial cells are non-conducting cells. They provide structural support and perform metabolic functions for the nervous system. For example, the myelin sheath cells that surround nerves act as a kind of insulation, ensuring efficient transmission of electric charge.

Neurons

Neurons are the functional units of the nervous system, as seen in the image below. There are three types of neurons:

  1. Sensory neurons: These neurons sense and relay information from the environment to the CNS for processing. They are located in clusters called ganglia.
  2. Interneurons: These neurons link neurons within the body and are found mostly throughout the brain and spinal cord. They integrate and interpret the sensory information and connect neurons to outgoing motor neurons.
  3. Motor neurons (also known as efferent neurons): These neurons send information to the “effectors.” Effectors are muscles, organs, and glands.

Anatomy of a neuron

Take a closer look at the anatomy of a neuron by examining the image below, which shows the parts of a typical neuron, including the parts of the axon. All neurons are made up of three parts: dendrites, the cell body, and the axon.

Dendrites

Dendrites are the parts of a neuron that receive information. This comes either from sensory receptors or from other nerve cells, such as motor neurons. Dendrites conduct nerve impulses toward the cell body.

Cell Body

The cell body is the portion of the neuron that contains the nucleus and the majority of the cytoplasm.

Axon

The axon is an extension of a neuron. It carries nerve impulses away from the cell body towards other neurons or effectors. Axons are extremely thin; about 100 of them could fit inside the shaft of a single human hair. The axon has several parts.

Axons are often covered with a white coat of fatty protein called the myelin sheath. The myelin sheath provides insulation for the neurons, to prevent the loss of charged ions from the nerve cell (like insulation around an electrical wire). The myelin sheath is formed by special glial cells called Schwann cells, which wrap themselves around the axon.

The areas between the sections of myelin sheath are known as the nodes of Ranvier. Nerve impulses jump from node to node, which speeds up the conduction process. Nerve impulses move much faster along myelinated nerve fibres than they do along non- myelinated nerve fibres.

Neural circuits

Communication among nerves results in neural circuits.

Some are very complex, like the ones that enable you to talk to a friend. This involves several parts of the brain sending signals to many parts of the body.

Sometimes, a situation calls for immediate action, where you don’t have time to think about it. For example, when you touch something really hot, your immediate response is to pull away. This is called a reflex and it occurs even before the brain gets the message. This neurological process is called a reflex arc.

Reflex arc

A reflex arc is a simple neural circuit that runs through the spinal cord. It provides a way for a reflex action to occur.

The neural circuit of a reflex arc is shown with the steps detailed below.

A reflex arc begins with a stimulus, such as a burning your finger.

  1. The heat on your finger triggers a sensory receptor in your skin. The sensory receptor sends the stimulus to the spinal cord.
  2. Interneurons in the spinal cord receive the information and immediately send a signal to the motor neurons.
  3. The motor neurons activate an effector organ (for example, the muscles).
  4. The effector organ, in this case a muscle, causes an immediate response, such as pulling your hand away.

Reflexes are involuntary because you don’t have time to think about them. In a fraction of a second, the stimulus has gone from the sensory receptors to the spinal cord and back to the motor neurons.

The process of a reflex arc can be summarized as follows:

Stimulus → Sensory neuron → Interneuron → Motor neuron → Response

The nerve impulse

An electrical current is generated in the nerves and muscles of the body. The electrical current is carried through the body in nerve impulses that travel along neurons. Nerve impulses are electrochemical messages created by the movement of ions through the nerve cell membrane. When a nerve gets stimulated (for example, by a pinprick or the sight of someone’s face), a rapid change occurs in the electrical potential difference (commonly just called “potential”) across the nerve membrane.

How a nerve impulse is formed

The neuron has a membrane that has differing amounts and types of ions inside and outside. At rest, the potential across the axon membrane is around –70 mV. This means that the inside of the axon is more negative than the outside. The voltage difference across the membrane during the resting stage is called the resting potential.

When the axon becomes stimulated, a rapid change occurs in the electrical potential difference across the membrane, so it now registers +40 mV. This means that the inside of the axon is now more positive than the outside. This reversal of potential is called an action potential, which is the voltage difference across a nerve cell membrane when the nerve is excited.

An action potential lasts only a few milliseconds before the axon returns to its resting potential value of –70 mV.

Polarization: How a resting potential is formed

The following explains how a resting potential is formed in a neuron.

Potential of a resting neuron is about –70 mV

The separation of an electrical charge has the potential to do work, which is measured in millivolts (mV). The potential of a resting neuron is about –70 mV, which indicates the difference between the number of positive charges found on the inside of the membrane in relation to the outside.

Negatively charged membrane due to the movement of positive ions out of cell

At rest, the membrane becomes negatively charged due to the movement of two positive ions out of the cell:

  • Sodium (Na+)
  • Potassium (K+)

The negative ions are generally too big to move across the membrane.

Specialized sodium (Na+) and potassium (K+) gated channels

The membrane contains specialized gated channels that allow sodium and potassium ions to pass through it. However, there are more potassium gates than sodium ones, so the resting membrane is 50 times more permeable to potassium than it is to sodium. This means that more potassium ions diffuse out of the nerve cell than sodium ions diffuse into it.

The Transport of Sodium (Na+) and Potassium (K+) is helped along by the Na+/K+ pump.

This pump is used to move ions out through the gates. The cell uses active transport to pump out Na+ ions and pump in K+ ions at a ratio of 3:2. This means that for every three Na+ ions pushed out, only two K+ ions are pulled in. The result is that the nerve cell loses a greater number of positive ions than it gains, making the inside of the membrane negative, in relation to the outside. This unequal concentration of positive ions across the nerve cell membrane establishes the negative resting potential. This also creates a concentration gradient so that potassium is high inside and sodium is high outside. The resting membrane is now charged and is called a polarized membrane. This process is called polarization.

Depolarization: How an action potential is formed

When the nerve is excited by a stimulus, a section of the cell membrane becomes more permeable to sodium than potassium.

  • The gates of the sodium channel open.
  • The gates of the potassium channel close.

Sodium ions rush into the nerve cell because of the electrochemical gradient. The rapid influx of sodium ions causes a charge reversal known as depolarization. Once the charge on the inside becomes positive, the sodium gates close, stopping the influx of sodium.

Depolarization does not last long. As soon as the inside of the cell becomes positive, the sodium-potassium pump in the cell membrane starts up again to push sodium out and pull potassium in. The process of restoring the original polarity of the nerve membrane is called repolarization. Nerves can’t be activated again until the repolarization process is complete. The time required for the nerve cell to become repolarized is called the refractory period, and it usually lasts 1 to 10 ms.

Propagation of the action potential

The movement of sodium ions into the nerve cell causes the local depolarization of the membrane. This triggers an action potential in that region of the membrane.

In order for this impulse to be conducted along the axon, it must move from the point of initial depolarization down along the axon in a wave. How does this happen?

In the local region of the action potential, three things happen at the membrane:

  1. The positively charged sodium ions move into the cell through the open gates of the sodium channel. This causes local depolarization of the membrane.
  2. The positively charged sodium ions are attracted to their neighbouring negative ions, which are lying on the same side of the membrane.
  3. If enough sodium ions move, this stimulates the sodium gates on the nearby resting membrane to open, so that it will become depolarized. Because the membrane on the other side of the action potential had previously been depolarized, it is still in its refractory phase, so it can’t depolarize for a while. This forces the nerve impulse to travel in only one direction along the nerve. This is shown below.

Positive ions are attracted towards negative ions in adjacent membrane areas as an action potential happens at one site. This triggers the sodium gates to open in the neighbouring membrane area so that depolarization can occur.

Since the membrane to the left of the action potential is still in its refractory phase, it can’t be depolarized again, which means that the nerve impulse can only travel to the right in this diagram. The result is a wave of action potential that moves in one direction along the cell membrane. The wave of action potential and depolarization is followed by a wave of repolarization, which gets the nerve ready to transmit the next signal.

Threshold levels and the all-or-none response

The stimulus must generate a depolarization at or above a certain voltage in order to produce a response in the nerve. This critical level is known as the threshold level.

However, increasing the intensity of the stimuli above the critical threshold level will not produce an increased response. The intensity of the nerve impulse and speed of transmission are constant. This is called the “all-or-none” response. It means that neurons either fire at full intensity or not at all. The graphs here illustrate how an action potential is only triggered when the depolarization caused by a stimulus reaches a threshold level.

However, even though the responses of individual nerves to stimuli are either “all” or “none,” the brain is still able to recognize the differences between two stimuli of different intensities. The simplest way it does this is by counting up the number of nerves responding. In most cases, the more intense the stimulus, the greater the number of nerve cells stimulated, and so the higher the number of impulses reaching the brain.

For example, holding a cup of soup warmed to 60°C causes more heat-sensitive neurons in your fingers to fire than if you were holding a cup at 50°C. In most cases, the brain adjusts the size of its response to the number of nerve impulses it receives.

Synaptic transmission

Once an action potential has raced down the axon, how does it cause the next nerve in the chain to fire? How does the nerve impulse travel between nerve cells? The process by which one nerve cell communicates with another nerve cell is called synaptic transmission.

Nerves are not directly connected to each other or to their effectors (for example, the muscles). There is a slight gap between the axon of one nerve and the dendrite of another. These small spaces between neurons, or between neurons and effectors, are called synapses.

Synapse structure

  1. The neuron transmitting the impulse is called the presynaptic neuron.
  2. The neuron receiving the impulse is called the postsynaptic neuron.
  3. At the end of the axon in the presynaptic neuron is a plate where small vesicles containing chemicals called neurotransmitters are released.

Neurotransmitters diffuse across the synapse and attach to receptors on the postsynaptic neuron, where they cause depolarization.

Neurotransmitters

Acetylcholine is a common neurotransmitter found in the end plates of many nerve cells. Acetylcholine acts as an excitatory neurotransmitter on many postsynaptic neurons by opening their sodium ion gates and causing depolarization. After depolarization has occurred, the postsynaptic neuron releases the enzyme cholinesterase to destroy acetylcholine. This allows the sodium gates to close so that the postsynaptic neuron can begin its recovery phase.

There are many different types of neurotransmitters. You will only be required to understand how acetylcholine works as a neurotransmitter. These other chemicals have various effects on cognition, mood and other behaviours, such as addiction.

The central nervous system

The central nervous system (CNS) includes the brain and spinal cord.

The brain is a concentration of nervous tissue that acts as the coordinating centre of the nervous system. The brain is covered by a three-layered protective membrane known as meninges. These three membrane layers form the blood-brain barrier, which determines what chemicals in the blood will reach the brain.

A fluid called cerebrospinal fluid is found between the innermost and middle layers of the meninges of the brain, and also through the central canal of the spinal cord. It functions as both a shock absorber and a transport medium for carrying nutrients to the brain cells, while it takes wastes from the brain cells to the blood.

The spinal cord

The spinal cord carries sensory nerve messages from receptors in the body to the brain, as well as carrying nerve messages from the brain to the muscles, organs, and glands. Below is a flowchart describing this process in more detail.

A stimulus activates receptors in the body.

↓

Nerve messages from receptors in the body are sent to the spinal cord.

↓

The brain receives sensory messages from the spinal cord and processes them.

↓

The spinal cord carries nerve messages from the brain to effector organs.

↓

Muscles, organs and glands in the body receive and respond to nerve messages from the brain.

A cross-section of the spinal cord, as seen below, shows that it consists of two types of nerve tissue:

  • White matter—the outside edge of the cord contains both sensory and motor neurons that are covered in myelin, which makes this nerve tissue look white.
  • Grey matter—the core of the spinal cord consists of unmyelinated interneurons, which makes them look grey.

The brain

The human brain weighs about 1.5 kilograms. It is divided up into many sections, each responsible for processing different kinds of information. A simplified diagram of the human brain is shown below.

The human brain can be divided into three main regions: the forebrain, the midbrain, and the hindbrain.

The forebrain

The forebrain contains:

Cerebral cortex

The cerebral cortex, which is made of grey matter and contains many folds and fissures. This is the region of the brain where higher-level thinking occurs.

Hemispheres

Two halves or hemispheres, each with its own special functions:

  • The right-hand side of the forebrain is linked to visual patterns or spatial awareness.
  • The left-hand side of the brain is linked to verbal skills.

Corpus callosum

A bundle of nerves called the corpus callosum, which allows both hemispheres to communicate with each other.

Lobes

Sections called lobes are responsible for processing information from your senses, as well as higher-order thinking.

4 Lobes

There are four main lobes of the forebrain:

  • Frontal
  • Parietal
  • Temporal
  • Occipital

Each lobe of the brain has a specific function. The table below summarizes the functions of each of the lobes.

Lobe Function
Frontal lobe
  • responsible for movement of voluntary muscles (walking/speech)
  • linked to intellectual activities, mood and personality, especially self-control, reasoning, and abstract thought
Temporal lobe
  • responsible for vision and hearing
  • linked to memory and interpretation of sensory information
Parietal lobe
  • responsible for touch and temperature awareness
  • linked to emotions and interpretation of speech
Occipital lobe
  • responsible for vision
  • linked to interpretation of visual information

The hypothalamus is located under the forebrain. It is the direct connection between the nervous system and the endocrine system.

The midbrain

The midbrain:

  • Consists of four spheres of grey matter.
  • Acts as a relay centre for some eye and ear reflexes.

The hindbrain

The hindbrain:

  • Joins with the spinal cord.
  • Controls breathing movements, the diameter of blood vessels, and heart rate.
  • Consists of the cerebellum, pons, and medulla oblongata.
  • The cerebellum controls limb movements, balance, and muscle tone.
  • The pons, which means “bridge,” relays information between the two regions of the cerebellum, and between the cerebellum and the medulla oblongata.
  • The medulla oblongata is the connection between the peripheral and central nervous systems. It controls involuntary muscle action and acts as the coordinating centre for the autonomic nervous system.

A case study

Phineas Gage was working as a young railroad construction supervisor in 1848. In an accidental explosion, an iron rod 2.5 centimetres thick and nearly a metre long was driven through his skull. The rod entered his head through his left cheek, passed up behind his left eye, and destroyed part of his brain. Amazingly, Gage recovered. However, he began to have startling changes in personality and mood. He became rude, loud, and antisocial, and could no longer hold down a job. His friends said that, “Gage was no longer Gage”. He died in 1861, 13 years after the accident, penniless and suffering from epilepsy. No autopsy was performed on his brain.

The autonomic nervous system and homeostasis

The autonomic system is part of the peripheral nervous system that regulates organs of the body without conscious control. It works with the endocrine system to adjust the body to changes that take place in external and internal environments.

The following examples are all controlled by the motor neurons of the autonomic nervous system:

  • breathing
  • blood CO2 and O2 levels
  • diversion of blood to various parts of the body
  • responses to temperature stresses and shock
  • dilation of the pupils

The autonomic nervous system, as seen below, is made up of two distinct, opposing units:

  • the sympathetic nervous system
  • the parasympathetic nervous system

Comparison between the sympathetic and parasympathetic nervous systems

Points of comparison Sympathetic nervous system Parasympathetic nervous system

How it works
  • Increases heart rate
  • Decreases peristalsis
  • Increases release of glucose
  • Dilates pupils
  • Relaxes bladder sphincter
  • Increases blood flow to the skin
  • Causes adrenal gland to release epinephrine
  • Decreases heart rate
  • Increases peristalsis
  • Decreases release of glucose
  • Constricts pupils
  • Contracts bladder sphincter
  • Decreases blood flow to the skin
  • Has no effect on adrenal gland
