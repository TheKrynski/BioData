Introduction

Living organisms function in a way that avoids environmental extremes. This “steady state” of being is known as homeostasis. In this unit, you will learn how homeostasis operates through feedback mechanisms, and why it is critical for maintaining a healthy body.

You will examine three body systems that play vital roles in maintaining homeostasis: the excretory, endocrine, and nervous systems. Throughout the unit, you will learn how your lifestyle choices can affect the functioning of these systems and the consequences that this has on your health.

What is homeostasis?

Your body is constantly adjusting to changes in its external environment to keep your internal environment at optimal levels for your health. These optimum levels include an internal body temperature of 37°C, blood sugar level of about 4 mmol/L and a blood pH of 7.35. The external environment does not usually provide these ideal conditions for living.

So, how does your body cope? In order to maintain a functional internal environment, your body’s organ systems must work together to maintain homeostasis when confronted with a fluctuating external environment.

Without you being aware of it, there is a lot going on inside you that needs to be regulated, such as:

  • water levels
  • food distribution and waste removal from your cells
  • temperature and pH adjustments
  • cellular activity (for example, biochemical metabolism, cellular respiration, DNA replication, cell division, protein synthesis, and so on)

In this unit, you will explore the mechanisms used to maintain homeostasis. All homeostatic control systems start with a stimulus, have three functional components, and end with a response, as seen in the flowchart below.

All homeostatic control systems have three functional components:

  • A receptor (or monitor)
  • A coordinating centre
  • A regulator (or effector)

As soon as any organ is operating outside normal or tolerable conditions, a special sensor (or monitor) located throughout the body sends signals to a coordinating centre. The centre, which could be either in the brain or spinal cord, processes the information and, if necessary, sends a signal to the appropriate regulator (also called an effector). The regulator then acts on the target organ, usually to restore it to its normal operating condition.

The homeostatic control process can be:

  • conscious, such as when you decide to put on a jacket when you feel cold. These are behavioural responses.
  • unconscious, such as when you start to shiver. These are physiological responses.

Homeostatic control systems

There are two types of feedback systems in living organisms:

  • Negative feedback
  • Positive feedback

Negative feedback systems

Negative feedback systems (also called negative feedback loops), are mechanisms that make adjustments to bring the systems of the body back to within an acceptable range. The system reacts to stimuli by counterbalancing them (i.e. when the body gets too hot, the system cools it by sweating).

Before you are introduced to a negative feedback system in the human body, let’s understand the components by using an analogy that you may be familiar with - a building’s thermostat system.

Thermostat systems

The figure below illustrates how a thermostat system uses negative feedback to control the temperature of a room.

  1. The temperature monitor, the receptor in the thermostat, registers the room temperature, which is the stimulus.
  2. The coordinating centre compares this temperature to the temperature set by the resident.
  3. If the temperature in the room is too low, the thermostat sends a signal to the furnace to turn on. The furnace produces heat that warms up the room, and, thus, is the effector in this homeostatic control system.
  4. The temperature monitor records this warmth, and when it equals or exceeds the set temperature, the thermostat sends a signal to the furnace to turn off so that the room won’t get too hot.

This is called a negative feedback system because a change in the variable being monitored (for example, room temperature) triggers the control mechanism to counteract any further change in the same direction. Negative feedback systems prevent small changes from getting too large and return the body back to homeostasis.

The regulation of blood pressure

Most homeostatic control systems in your body use negative feedback. Using the room thermostat system as a model of a negative feedback look, let’s use the same components and see how it relates to the regulation of blood pressure in the human body.

  • High blood pressure
  • Low blood pressure

An increase in your blood pressure, the stimulus, is detected by receptors in the blood vessels that sense the resistance of blood flow against the vessel walls.

The receptors relay a message to your brain, the coordinating centre, which, in turn, sends a message to your heart and blood vessels.

Your heart and blood vessels, the effectors, change to decrease your heart rate and increase the diameter of your blood vessels, both of which cause your blood pressure to fall back to within the normal range (the response).

This negative feedback loop also occurs if blood pressure falls too low (the stimulus). In this scenario, the receptors in your blood vessels relay a message to your brain, indicating that your blood pressure is too low.

Your brain, the coordinating centre, then sends out signals to the heart and blood vessels.

Your heart and blood vessels, the effectors, cause heart rate to increase and your blood vessels decrease in diameter, both of which increase your blood pressure (the response).

Positive feedback systems

A positive feedback system is the opposite of a negative feedback system. It reinforces small changes to make them larger or amplified. Positive feedback moves the target variable even further away from its steady state, or increases the magnitude of the change away from the steady state, resulting in a change in the body’s status, rather than a return to homeostasis.

This can sometimes be beneficial because it causes a process to be accomplished rapidly. Once the goal is reached, an external force is required to cause the positive feedback system to stop. Positive feedback systems are rare compared to negative feedback systems in organisms, because they can lead to instability if not carefully controlled.

Childbirth: An example of a positive feedback system

Stress

A decrease in progesterone levels near the end of pregnancy stimulates the uterus to contract. The contractions bring about the release of another hormone, oxytocin, which causes even stronger contractions of the uterus.

Receptors (Sensors)

As the contractions increase in force and duration, the baby’s head pushes against the cervix.

Coordinating centre

This sends nerve impulses to the brain.

Effectors

More oxytocin is released due to the nerve impulses sent to the brain, and it is carried by the bloodstream to the uterus. Consequently, this triggers even greater contractions which push the baby toward the cervix until it is born.

Once the baby is born, the brain signals the contractions to stop, which also stops the release of oxytocin.

Thermoregulation

Like many other animals, humans can regulate their internal temperature. It is usually around 37°C. Even when you are using heaters and air conditioners, your body still needs to work to maintain a constant internal temperature. The maintenance of body temperature within a range that enables cells to function efficiently is called thermoregulation.

Most organisms cannot maintain a constant internal body temperature, regardless of their surroundings. These organisms are called ectotherms, which means that their metabolic rates are dependent on external temperatures and other external elements. Invertebrates, as well as most fish, amphibians, and reptiles, are ectotherms.

Mammals (including humans) and birds are endotherms, because they are able to maintain a constant internal body temperature, regardless of their surroundings. They have evolved metabolic processes that generate internal heat from organs such as the kidney and liver. As a consequence, they are less vulnerable to changes in external temperatures.

However, endotherms still need to use a variety of physiological adaptations to counteract external changes in temperature. For example, when external temperatures drop, endotherms can compensate by increasing their rate of cellular respiration to generate internal heat, and if their internal temperature gets too high, they can use a variety of measures to shed the heat.

For many endotherms, including humans, the “thermostat” for thermoregulation is located in a region of the brain called the hypothalamus. In humans, this thermostat is “set” at 37°C, but this is allowed to vary by one degree up or down, depending on conditions.

Although your body works hard to maintain this constant temperature, it can’t always manage it. Problems arise if the internal temperature gets too far above or below this optimal level, resulting in either heat or cold stress. Heat stress response

On October 7, 2007, race officials for the Chicago Marathon (held in Chicago, Illinois) called off the race after four hours when temperatures soared over 30°C under a hot sun. Although this may seem like a good temperature for a visit to the beach, it is much too hot for the average person taking part in an extreme athletic sport, like running a marathon.

Despite stopping the race early, over 300 runners were treated for heat-related problems, 50 were hospitalized, and one 35-year-old male runner died. The hospitalized runners were likely suffering from heat stress.

Heat stress occurs when the body heats up beyond its tolerance limit because its built-in cooling mechanisms are unable to prevent overheating. This often happens as a result of extreme physical activity or spending too much time outdoors under intensely hot sunshine, or staying too long in a very hot location, like a sauna.

Physiological response to heat stress

The body's response to heat stress is outlined in the steps below:

Stimulus

The process of heat stress begins with your body’s thermoregulatory negative feedback system going into action to cool you down when your body temperature rises.

Receptor/sensor

As your body starts to get overheated, thermoreceptors in your skin send messages to your brain that your body is getting too hot.

Coordinating centre

The hypothalamus in your brain then coordinates a response by sending a signal to your sweat glands to initiate sweating.

Effector

At the same time, a nerve message is sent to the blood vessels in your skin, causing them to dilate in a process called vasodilation. The dilated blood vessels increase the blood flow to the surface of your skin. This makes your skin look redder. Sweating also occurs. The sweat droplets on the surface of your skin evaporate, which cools the skin and the blood flowing beneath it so that the blood returning to the core of your body is much cooler.

Along with changes in circulation, your brain sends signals to the respiratory system to increase your breathing rate, using the lungs and moist airways as additional sites of heat exchange.

Your brain also sends a signal to increase your heart rate, so that blood can be sent to the skin surface more rapidly, in order to cool it down.

Response

All of these homeostatic systems attempt to cool the body down.

There is a limit to how much heat can be eliminated using these approaches. When this limit is reached, people start to suffer from heat stroke, resulting in fainting, dizziness, and, occasionally, death. Even before the heat stroke limit is reached, complications can arise from the cooling system’s activities.

For example, cooling the body using sweat requires a lot of water. If the lost water is not replenished, it can lead to many problems, including loss of cellular functions, kidney problems, thickened blood, muscle cramps, and nausea. Also, sweat carries valuable salts with it, so when the body sweats, it loses these dissolved substances (which are left behind on the skin’s surface). That is why athletes need to drink fluids that contain electrolytes (salts) in order to ensure that they are replenishing the water and electrolytes lost. Your kidneys also play a role in this process, as you will learn in the next lesson.

Cold stress response

Something likely familiar to all Canadians is the feeling of being too cold, at one time or another. Usually, it is just a feeling of being too cold for comfort and the effects are temporary. But occasionally, people experience real cold stress, and in some cases, it is severe enough to lead to frostbite, or even death. The physiological response of the body to cold is outlined below.

Physiological response to cold stress

Stimulus

Just like in the heat stress response, when your body temperature starts to drop below the optimal level, a negative feedback process, called the cold stress response, begins.

Receptor/sensor

The process starts when thermoreceptors in your skin send messages to your brain that your body is getting too cold.

Coordinating centre

The hypothalamus in your brain coordinates a response by sending a signal to your organs and tissues to increase body temperature.

Effector

At the same time, nerves going to the arterioles of your skin cause smooth muscles to contract around your blood vessels, resulting in vasoconstriction, which limits the blood flow to your skin and slows heat loss. The blood is shunted away from your extremities and flows to the core of your body, to keep your vital internal organs at the correct temperature. That is why your fingers, toes, nose, and ears feel numb when you’ve been out in the cold for too long. If this condition persists for long enough, your extremities may even begin to suffer tissue damage, and in the worst case, the tissue dies, resulting in severe frostbite. This is a common danger faced by mountain climbers who attempt to reach the world’s tallest peaks, such as Mount Everest, as well as for people working outside during arctic winters.

In addition to blood flow changes, nerve messages are also carried to the smooth muscle that surrounds the hair follicles in your skin. The smooth muscle contracts, which causes your skin to tighten and your hair to stand up (a sensation commonly known as “goosebumps”). When the hair is erect, it traps a layer of warm air next to the surface of the skin, which helps to reduce heat loss. This is especially important for animals that have lots of hair or fur. Thus, this mechanism does not help for humans.

The hypothalamus also sends messages that initiate shivering, a rhythmic contraction of skeletal muscle that stimulates ATP production and generates heat, so that the blood can transport the heat around your body. If exposure to the cold is prolonged, your body creates a hormonal response that elevates its metabolic rate, which ultimately provides the source of the heat in your body.

Response

All these homeostatic systems attempt to reduce heat loss, as well as encourage the production of more heat within the body.

But, just as with heat stress, they have their limits. If the cold stress is increased, it can eventually become hard to move limbs and fingers—all the joints feel stiff. This can make it hard for you to move yourself out of the cold situation. In the most extreme cases, cold stress can also cause disorientation and delusions of being too hot, causing some people to actually tear off their protective clothing and die shortly thereafter.
