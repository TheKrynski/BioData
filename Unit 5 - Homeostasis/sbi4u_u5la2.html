Introduction

As you learned in the last learning activity, your cells are constantly carrying out chemical reactions to maintain homeostasis. Many of these chemical reactions produce waste that must be removed from cells and from your body. Many of these waste products are small, water-soluble molecules that become dissolved in your blood, along with other small molecules that are not waste. How is your body able to separate out and excrete the waste products of metabolism? In this learning activity, you will learn about the role of your urinary system. You will also learn about various urinary system disorders.

Importance of Excreting Waste

In this section you will learn about:

  • the excretory system
  • forms of waste
  • the human urinary system

Role of the excretory system

All living organisms produce and excrete waste. The waste is a result of the nutrients metabolized through the process of cellular respiration. Unless this waste is removed, the cells will die.

Your excretory system has three main jobs:

  1. to remove waste
  2. monitor water balance
  3. control pH

Main organs of the excretory system

The excretory system also plays a key role in maintaining homeostasis in internal systems. The main organs responsible for this job in humans are the:

  • kidneys
  • liver
  • large intestine
  • Kidneys
  • Liver
  • Large intestine

The kidneys remove metabolic waste and keep your blood chemistry and blood water levels balanced. To maintain a proper balance of water levels in the blood and body tissues (known as “water balance”), the amount of water taken in each day must match the amount lost through the kidneys, during urine production. As you will learn later in this lesson, minimizing water loss is a large part of kidney function.

The liver transforms ingested toxins, such as alcohol, into soluble compounds that can be eliminated by the kidneys. The liver also transforms the hazardous products of protein metabolism into metabolites, which are then eliminated by the kidneys.

The large intestine removes toxic waste from the digestive system.

Forms of Waste

There are two types of waste produced by the human body:

  1. Digestive waste
  2. Cellular waste (e.g. carbon dioxide and nitrogenous waste)

Simple by-products of cell respiration are called cellular waste. Cellular waste must be eliminated in order to prevent toxic levels from building up in the cells and body and disrupting normal biochemical processes.

For example, one simple product of cellular respiration—carbon dioxide—can become toxic if it is not removed. Excess carbon dioxide will dissolve in the blood, forming carbonic acid, which lowers the pH of the blood below its optimal level of 7.4. Your lungs eliminate most of the carbon dioxide produced from cellular respiration and so help to maintain homeostasis.

Nitrogenous Waste

The waste of living organisms is often abundant in the element nitrogen, which is a common element in proteins. Recall that proteins are made up of smaller building blocks called amino acids, all of which contain nitrogen. Because of the presence of nitrogen, this type of waste is known as nitrogenous waste.

There are three main types of nitrogenous waste:

  • Ammonia
  • Urea
  • Uric acid

Ammonia

NH 3

This type of waste is a by-product of protein digestion. The amino group is removed through a process called deamination. Deamination occurs in the liver of humans. The by-product of deamination is ammonia, a water-soluble toxic gas. Some aquatic organisms can immediately excrete the toxic ammonia, but in most mammals, including humans, the ammonia is too toxic to store in the bladder and is converted to urea.

Urea

CH 4 N 2 O

To avoid poisoning of the body, mammals detoxify ammonia by combining two molecules of ammonia with one molecule of carbon dioxide to form urea. Urea is 100,000 times less toxic than ammonia.

Uric acid

C 5 H 4 N 4 O 3

Another nitrogenous waste product, uric acid, is formed through the breakdown of nucleic acids.

The human urinary system

The most important waste-removal system in your body, the human urinary system, is the one that removes waste from your blood. When your cells undergo cellular respiration they produce waste, which diffuses into your blood. Your heart pumps this blood, which is rich in carbon dioxide and metabolic waste, into your kidneys via the renal arteries. After processing, the renal veins return “clean” blood to your heart, so that it can be circulated back around your body. At any given time, about 20% of your blood is being processed by your kidneys.

The waste that filters out of the blood collect in the ducts of the kidneys and are moved into the bladder via tubes called ureters. This waste fluid is called urine. At the base of the bladder, there is a sphincter muscle that acts as a valve, permitting the storage of urine. As the bladder fills between 200 mL and 400 mL, it activates stretch receptors and sends a message to the brain to instruct the bladder to urinate. If this is ignored, and the bladder fills up to 600 mL, the sphincter loses control, causing the urine to enter the urethra, where it is eventually released from the body.

Kidneys

The kidneys are the most important organs in the urinary system, so it’s important to understand how they work.

The structure of the kidney

The figure below illustrates a cross-section of a kidney. The kidney has three layers.

  • The outer layer is the cortex and encircles the kidney.
  • The inner layer, called the medulla, is found beneath the cortex.
  • A hollow chamber, known as the renal pelvis, joins the kidney with the ureter.

The functional unit of the kidney is the nephron. This is where the filtering of the blood takes place. You will see the details of how that works next.

Nephrons

Each kidney contains about 1.25 million tubules called nephrons. They are located in the cortex and extend down into the medulla. Nephrons are surrounded by a mesh of fine blood vessels, called capillaries, that allows waste to diffuse out of the blood.

Each nephron consists of five main parts:

  • Bowman’s capsule (containing the glomerulus)
  • Proximal tubule
  • Loop of Henle
  • Distal tubule
  • Collecting duct

Blood flow in a nephron

Using the image below, track the path of blood flow in a nephron by following the steps.

  1. Each nephron has its own blood supply via the afferent arteriole. The afferent arteriole brings blood into the nephron to be processed.
  2. The afferent arteriole branches into a capillary bed, called the glomerulus. The glomerulus is a cluster of capillaries enclosed in a capsule, called the Bowman’s capsule. Fluid is forced out of the blood into the capsule, where it begins the process of filtration.
  3. Blood leaves the glomerulus via the efferent arteriole. This blood now has less fluid in it, which will need to be replenished later in the process.
  4. The efferent arteriole eventually branches into tiny convoluted tubules, called peritubular capillaries, which wrap around the nephron tubules and pick up water and nutrients.

Note: The Bowman’s capsule, the afferent arteriole, and the efferent arteriole are located in the cortex of the kidney.

Urine formation – An overview

In each nephron, the process of urine formation begins when the waste material in the blood gets squeezed out, under high pressure, and enters the Bowman’s capsule.

The fluid that filters out of the blood is called filtrate, and it will eventually become urine. You will investigate the process of urine formation in more detail in the next section.

To understand how a nephron functions, it may help by following the flow of fluid through its tubules. The image below is a schematic diagram showing this flow of fluid through a single nephron.

Here is a basic description of what happens as the fluid flows through the nephron.

  1. Blood enters the glomerulus and is filtered, through pressure, into Bowman’s capsule, which is located in the cortex.
  2. The filtrate passes through the proximal tubule, which is also located in the cortex. This is where glucose and amino acids are reabsorbed to the blood.
  3. Next, the filtrate moves down into the loop of Henle, which is located in the medulla of the kidney. Here, water is removed from the filtrate and returned to the blood.
  4. The filtrate then moves through the distal tubule located in the cortex, where hydrogen ions and drugs that are to be excreted in the filtrate are extracted (or secreted) from the blood. Even more water is removed from the filtrate and returned to the blood.
  5. Finally, the filtrate moves into the collecting duct, which is located in the medulla. These ducts collect filtrate (now called urine) from many nephrons and eventually merge in the renal pelvis of the kidney. The urine exits the renal pelvis via the ureter, to be stored in the bladder.

Formation of urine: A closer look

There are three steps in urine formation, described in detail below:

  1. Filtration
  2. Reabsorption
  3. Secretion

Filtration

The first step in urine formation, called filtration, involves a simple filtering process in which waste fluids, under pressure, move out of the blood into Bowman’s capsule.

  • The blood enters the nephron through the afferent arteriole and goes into the glomerulus.
  • The glomerulus is a high-pressure capillary bed where the blood is squeezed through narrow capillaries. The pressure in the glomerulus is about four times as high as it is in normal blood capillary beds.
  • These capillaries act as semi-permeable membranes (filters) so that larger molecules, like plasma protein, blood cells, and platelets, cannot pass through their walls, whereas smaller molecules and wastes are able to pass through.
  • The resulting filtrate is collected in Bowman’s capsule and then flows into the proximal tubule.
  • Not all of the molecules in the filtrate are waste products, however. Many beneficial small molecules, like glucose and water, also get forced out of the blood and eventually need to be reabsorbed.

Reabsorption

The second step in urine formation involves reabsorption, which is the process by which the materials needed by the body (mostly water, glucose, and amino acids) are returned to the bloodstream from the filtrate.

  • Reabsorption begins when the filtrate leaves Bowman’s capsule and enters the proximal tubule and ends by the time the filtrate has reached the distal tubule.
  • Reabsorption is a multi-step process.
  • It involves the transfer of essential solutes and water from the filtrate back into the blood that flows through the peritubular capillaries surrounding the nephron.
  • As the filtrate moves through the proximal tubule, glucose and amino acids are removed and returned to the blood.
  • Next, the filtrate passes through the loop of Henle, where water is drawn out by osmosis because the surrounding medulla has a high sodium concentration.
  • This water then moves through the capillary membranes back into the blood.
  • Reabsorption continues until the threshold level of a substance is reached, so that no more movement across membranes occurs. Reabsorption uses both passive and active transport to help move materials out of the urine and into the blood.
Passive transport

Passive transport occurs through the processes of diffusion and osmosis. It does not directly require the input of additional energy.

For example, as the filtrate flows down the descending loop of Henle, water moves out of it by osmosis because the surrounding medulla has a high sodium concentration.

Active transport

Active transport involves the use of ATP energy to move molecules from areas of low concentration to areas of high concentration - this is the reverse of diffusion and osmosis.

If the kidneys relied only on diffusion or osmosis to filter the blood, you would lose too much water and nutrients in your urine, along with the waste. Active transport helps move these essential molecules back into the blood after the initial filtration.

Active transport is used in two regions of the nephron.

  • First, in the proximal tubule, it is used to move glucose and amino acids out of the filtrate.
  • Second, in the loop of Henle, active transport moves sodium (Na+) ions out of the filtrate and into the medulla, in order to create the conditions in which water can be returned to the bloodstream by osmosis.

Not all ions require direct applications of energy to get them to move against their concentration gradient. For example, negative ions, such as chlorine (Cl-) and bicarbonate (HCO3-), follow the positive Na+ ions out of the urine and into the medulla because of charge attraction (since opposite charges attract). This movement of negative ions also helps to regulate the pH of the blood.

Water reabsorption

The water reabsorption process is one of the most important functions of the nephron. The process ultimately happens by osmosis, but creating the conditions for this to happen involves both active and passive transport. Water reabsorption is achieved through a two-stage process:

  1. As the filtrate flows down the descending loop of Henle, it passes through increasing concentrations of sodium ions in the medulla. This draws the water out by osmosis. As a consequence, at the bottom of the loop, the filtrate is now quite concentrated, especially with sodium ions.
  2. As the filtrate begins to move up the ascending loop, active transport is used to move the sodium ions out of the filtrate and into the medulla. Because this part of the loop is impermeable to water and loses salt through active transport, the filtrate becomes increasingly dilute as it proceeds up the ascending loop.

The sodium ions that are actively transported out of the filtrate in the ascending loop move into the medulla, where they create the sodium gradient that draws water out, in the descending loop.

Secretion, the third step in urine formation occurs in the distal tubule. Secretion of the remaining waste products involves active transport, which pulls excess H + ions, nitrogenous waste, and even some drugs like penicillin, out of the blood

Kidney functions

Besides filtering waste, kidneys maintain water balance in the body.

To maintain homeostasis, your body adjusts for:

  • increased water intake by increasing urine output
  • low water levels in the blood by reducing urine output

In order for these adjustments to take place, the following two body systems must interact:

  • the endocrine system
  • the nervous system

The roles of the endocrine and nervous systems in water balance

Antidiuretic hormone (ADH), helps to regulate the osmotic pressure of body fluids by causing the kidneys to increase water reabsorption. ADH moves along specialized fibres from the hypothalamus to a gland called the pituitary gland, which stores and releases ADH into the blood.

Changes in osmotic pressure in the blood are picked up by specialized nerve receptors in the hypothalamus called osmoreceptors.

  • If the blood volume drops because of sweating, blood solutes become more concentrated. This, in turn, increases the blood’s osmotic pressure, causing water to move out of cells and into the bloodstream. When the cells of the hypothalamus shrink, a nerve message is sent to the pituitary gland, signalling the release of ADH. The ADH is carried by the blood to the kidneys, making the tubules more permeable to water, which, in turn, allows for more water to be reabsorbed into the blood. As a result, more concentrated urine is produced. When ADH is released, more concentrated urine is produced, thereby conserving water in the body.
  • As soon as the amount of water in the blood increases, it begins to re-enter the hypothalamus cells, causing the cells to swell. This causes the hypothalamus to send nerve messages to the pituitary gland, signalling it to stop the release of ADH. This process is repeated continuously to regulate water levels in the blood.

How does ADH work?

  1. About 85% of the water that is reabsorbed from the nephron is done so at the proximal tubule, since it is this part of the nephron that is very permeable to water.
  2. The descending loop of Henle is permeable to water and ions.
  3. The ascending tubule is only permeable to salt (NaCl) . Active transport of sodium ( Na + ) ions from the ascending section of the loop concentrates solutes within the medulla of the kidney.
  4. Without ADH, the rest of the tubule, distal and collecting, remains impermeable to water, but continues to actively transport Na + ions from the tubules.
  5. This means that the remaining 15% of the water filtered into the nephron would be lost, if no ADH were present.

ADH makes both the upper part of the distal tubule, as well as the collecting duct, permeable to water. The water leaves the upper part of the distal tubule and enters the blood, making the urine more concentrated. With the release of ADH by the pituitary gland and its effect on the distal tubule’s permeability to water, the kidney is able to regulate the osmotic concentration of body fluids.

Blood pressure

Kidneys also play a role in maintaining normal blood pressure.

A hormone called aldosterone acts on the nephrons to increase Na + reabsorption. Aldosterone is produced in the cortex of the adrenal.

As the reabsorption of (NaCl) increases, the osmotic gradient increases and more water moves out of the nephron and into the blood. When there is an increased loss of fluids, the overall pressure of the blood drops. This could reduce the blood flow, which would ultimately reduce the delivery of oxygen and nutrients to tissues. To avoid this, blood pressure receptors located near the glomerulus detect low blood pressure at the glomerulus. If low pressure is detected, nearby specialized cells in the adrenal glands, release an enzyme, renin, that converts a plasma protein called angiotensinogen into angiotensin.

The angiotensin accomplishes two things, both of which help to increase blood pressure:

  • It constricts blood vessels, thus increasing blood pressure.
  • It stimulates the release of aldosterone from the adrenal gland.

The aldosterone travels in the blood to the kidneys, where it acts on the cells of the distal tubule and collecting duct to increase Na + transport. When Na + is reabsorbed, water follows, increasing the blood volume and thus increasing blood pressure.

Kidneys balance blood pH

During cellular respiration, cells produce excess acids, which dissociate into H + ions, lowering the pH of your blood. How do your kidneys adjust pH to maintain homeostasis?

The kidneys do this by using a special buffer found in the blood called the bicarbonate buffer. This buffer neutralizes the effects of decreasing or increasing pH levels beyond tolerable ranges, using the following chemical reaction:

H + (aq) + HCO 3- (aq) → H 2 CO 3 (aq)  →  H 2 O (l) +   CO 2 (g)

The bicarbonate ion removes the excess H + ions out of the blood, forming carbonic acid ( H 2 CO 3 ). The carbonic acid breaks down into carbon dioxide and water, and the CO 2 is transported to the lungs, where much of it is exhaled.

Kidney Disease

Kidney function can be affected when other systems break down. The reverse is also true: when the kidneys break down, other systems can be affected.

A urinalysis is a test of an individual’s urine that can detect kidney disorders, as well as the effects of other non-functioning systems on the kidneys.

There are several kinds of kidney diseases, the most common of which are described next.

Diabetes Insipidus

Diabetes insipidus occurs when the ADH-producing cells of the hypothalamus, or the nerve tracts leading from the hypothalamus to the pituitary gland, are destroyed. Without ADH to regulate water reabsorption, urine output increases considerably, so none of the remaining 15% (after the other 85% has been reabsorbed at the proximal tubule), is recovered. As much as 20 L of dilute urine may result, creating a strong thirst response, and a severe need to replace lost water.

Bright’s Disease

This disease is also known as nephritis or the inflammation (swelling) of the nephrons. one type of nephritis affects the tiny blood vessels of the glomerulus. Toxins produced by invading microbes destroy the tiny blood vessels, altering the permeability of the nephron. This means that proteins and other large molecules are able to pass into the nephron. When the nephrons are destroyed, no mechanism occurs in the nephron membrane to reabsorb protein; instead, the protein remains in the nephron and draws water from the neighbouring peritubular capillaries, which, in turn, increases the output of urine.

Diabetes Mellitus

When a deficiency of insulin occurs because of a defect in the pancreas, blood sugar levels tend to rise. The cells of the proximal tubule are supplied with enough ATP to reabsorb 0.1% of blood sugar. When higher blood sugar concentrations exist, such as in a person with diabetes, the excess sugar remains in the nephron. This excess sugar in the nephron draws water into the nephron, increasing the volume of urine. Individuals with diabetes mellitus expel large volumes of urine, which explains why they are often thirsty and have to replace the lost water.

Urinary Tract Infection

If bacteria accumulates in the urethra it may cause an infection in the bladder. Once it is in the urinary tract, the infection may cause an immune response which may include increased white blood cells and/or fever. If the infection spreads upwards through the ureter into the kidney, there can be more severe health complications such as kidney failure or the bacteria entering the bloodstream (a condition known as sepsis).

Kidney Stones

Kidney stones occur when solutes from the blood precipitate (solidify) out of the urine. These solutes can accumulate and lodge themselves in the renal pelvis or move into the narrow ureter. As the kidney stones move into the bladder, they can tear delicate tissue and cause severe pain and discomfort. They can also work their way further down the excretory passage and lodge in the urethra, causing a burning sensation, along with excruciating pain.

Dialysis Technology

The term “dialysis” means the exchange of substances across a semipermeable membrane. A dialysis machine is used for people whose kidneys cannot effectively process bodily wastes. The principles of diffusion and blood pressure both influence the operation of a dialysis machine.
