Introduction

“You are what you eat” is a familiar saying that reminds us of the importance we should place on our dietary choices. Behind this saying is the realization that the material we are made of, the energy we use, and the chemical compounds that make our metabolism work all come from the things we eat and drink.

This first learning activity in this unit, Metabolic Processes, introduces you to the role that energy plays in metabolism.

Energy for life

In living things, a complex system of chemicals goes through a constant sequence of reactions, and all of these reactions either require or release energy. Energy is the ability to do work, and all living things devote a large part of their existence to collecting, processing, storing, and using energy. While it is obvious that activities like walking, running, and swimming require energy, it is easy to overlook the fact that processes such as thinking or digesting also require energy.

The sum of all chemical reactions occurring in a living cell or organism is termed its metabolism (derived from the Greek word, metabole, which means “change”).

  • The metabolic reactions that build larger molecules from smaller molecules or atoms are called anabolic reactions.
  • Those reactions that break large molecules down into smaller molecules or atoms are called catabolic reactions.
  • Two of the most important metabolic processes that are fundamental to all life are cellular respiration and photosynthesis, which you will study in detail in the next couple of lessons. These processes take place in specialized structures within cells called chloroplasts and mitochondria.

Here is an example:

amino acid + amino acid → dipeptide + water

Since two small monomers are combined to make a larger polymer, this reaction is considered anabolic.

Dietary energy

One of the characteristics of living things is that all living things require energy. Living things, such as humans, obtain energy from the food they eat. The carbohydrates, lipids, and proteins previously described in Unit 2 all provide food energy, as well as the building material for new cellular matter

The basic currency of energy in eukaryotic cells is glucose and in prokaryotes a variety of molecules other than glucose can be used for energy. Eukaryotic cells are found in organisms that contain a nucleus surrounded by a membrane and specialized organelles not present in prokaryotic cells. For example, protozoa, trees and vertebrates have eukaryotic cells. On the other hand, prokaryotic cells are usually unicellular microorganisms that do not have a distinct nucleus or membrane bound organelles. Bacteria are an example of prokaryotic cells.

The energy from glucose, and from food in general, is most commonly reported in units of calories (cal), but in the scientific literature, the units of joules (J) and kilojoules (1 kJ = 1000 J) are used instead.

A calorie is the amount of energy required to raise the temperature of 1 g of water by 1°C. While joules are the International System of Units (SI)—the energy unit of choice for nutritionists, biologists, and chemists—consumers are more familiar with the calorie. To convert between the two systems, use the following factor: 1 cal = 4.184 kJ.

Metabolic rate

The food we eat is converted to energy to meet our daily energy requirements and the amount of energy our bodies need is influenced by many factors.

To maintain a constant weight, your energy input from food should match your energy output, based on the amount you expend while resting, plus whatever you expend doing activities. Simply put, if you want to lose weight, then you need to expend more energy than you take in. Any extra energy ingested but not immediately used is eventually converted to fat.

The more active you are, the more energy you use

Daily energy requirements, for basic body functions, such as breathing, digesting, and thinking can be calculated using basal metabolic rate (BMR) and its more useful derivation, the resting metabolic rate (RMR), represent the amount of energy used by a person at rest over a 24-hour period. This is the amount of energy required to run the basic energetic needs of organs like the heart, lungs, liver, and kidneys.

Although it might be interesting to calculate your own value, it is important to understand the implications of such calculations. Researchers have learned that physically active individuals have an easier time keeping excess weight off. People who don’t exercise burn less energy all the time and may still gain weight, even if they eat less than the active person. Simply by being more active, you increase the amount of energy you expend, even while you are just resting or watching TV.

Healthy dietary and lifestyle choices

Your personal choices about diet and lifestyle are important to your overall health. Good choices can promote good health, whereas poor choices can lead to significant health problems.

Three of the most common health problems resulting from our modern lifestyle include:

  1. Diabetes
  2. Obesity
  3. Cardiovascular disease

Diabetes

Diabetes is a serious world-wide problem and now affects an estimated 246 million people globally (Source: Canadian Diabetes Association). It is the world’s 4th leading cause of death and is increasing daily.

Currently, over 1.6 million Canadians are diagnosed, and living with, some form of diabetes.

Type 2 Diabetes

Type 2, diabetes usually results from:

  • The pancreas’s gradual inability to produce insulin
  • Cells becoming resistant to absorbing glucose, a type of sugar, from the blood
  • Individuals with the lack of ability to control blood sugar levels

High levels of blood glucose over time can cause many health problems in a variety of areas, including:

  • the cardiovascular system
  • the kidney
  • the nerves
  • the feet
  • the eyes
  • the bone
  • the joints
  • skin problems
  • digestive problems
  • sexual dysfunction
  • problems with teeth and gums
  • premature death

In North American society, Type 2 diabetes is being found in increasingly younger patients due to lifestyle choices such as inactivity and poor diet become more prevalent.

Medical research has shown that chronically high blood glucose levels are responsible for most new cases of Type 2 diabetes. This is because chronically high blood glucose levels:

  • reduce the ability of cells to respond to insulin
  • reduce the pancreas’s capacity to make sufficient insulin.

While it is impossible to control some of the risk factors for Type 2 diabetes, individuals can choose their diets and their level of physical activity.

Making informed lifestyle choices plays a central role on our health.

Obesity

Obesity is a condition resulting from the accumulation of excessive amounts of fatty tissue called adipose tissue. It is estimated that nearly 10–12% of Canadian adults are obese, and perhaps up to 50% have a weight problem.

Health Problems associated with Obesity

When an overweight person consumes food energy in greater amounts than they are able to expend it, the remainder is stored as body fat. Excess adipose tissue, and the weight that accompanies it, interferes with the functions of a variety of organ systems and can lead to multiple health problems, including cardiovascular disease, Type 2 diabetes, high blood pressure, cancer, arthritis, gall bladder dysfunction, and reproductive disorders.

Calculating body fat: BMI

There are sophisticated medical tests available for determining your exact body fat percentage, but a relatively quick measure is called the body mass index (BMI).

A BMI is calculated by dividing your weight (in kilograms) by the square of your height (in metres).

A high BMI means that you are heavier than normal for your height.

A very low BMI indicates that you are underweight.

The following scale shows how the BMI is used to categorize people.

Underweight = <18.5 kg/m2

Normal weight = 18.5-24.9 kg/m2

Overweight = 25-29.9 kg/m2

Obesity = BMI of 30 or greater kg/m2

Cardiovascular disease

Cardiovascular disease is an important health concern for Canadians.

Diet and lifestyle can play significant roles in causing cardiovascular disease. The risk factors that could lead to circulatory system diseases include:

  • high blood pressure
  • high cholesterol
  • diabetes
  • smoking
  • stress
  • excessive alcohol consumption
  • physical inactivity
  • being overweight

Two of the most common cardiovascular problems in Canada are heart attacks and strokes.

Heart attack

The heart, like any other muscle, gets the energy it needs from blood vessels carrying oxygen, sugars, and other nutrients.

Heart disease has many causes, all of which affect the heart’s structure and functions.

For example, coronary artery disease occurs when fatty materials, calcium, and scar tissue combine into a sticky substance called plaque, which is carried in the blood. The plaque can accumulate on the walls of coronary arteries and narrow their diameter.

Sometimes, if enough plaque builds up, large clumps of it can become dislodged and completely block the blood flow through the artery that supplies blood to the heart’s muscle tissue. This can lead to a heart attack.

Stroke

Stroke is caused by interruption of blood flow in the brain. Blood vessels leading to the brain can become blocked by plaque, or can rupture due to high blood pressure. When either happens, regions of the brain lose their blood supply and the neurons can fail to function properly, or may die. This results in temporary or permanent paralysis, or even death.

Strokes are the third leading cause of death in Canada, but their incidence is declining as more Canadians become aware of the importance of diet, exercise, and eliminating tobacco use.

Caloric intake and dietary choices

How much is too much? If you are at a healthy weight for your age, height, and biological sex (male/female), then your goal should be to maintain your weight.

  • If you want to find out your ideal weight, you may want to check with your physician. To maintain your ideal weight, the amount of energy you take in should match the amount of energy you expend. In this way, there would be no surplus energy to convert into fat.
  • If your goal, however, is to lose weight, then the amount of energy you expend should exceed the amount of energy you take in. Therefore, it is important to know how much energy content there is in the food that you eat.
  • Many factors influence diet, including: cultural background, family, body image, societal norms, economic status, health, medications, nutritional supplements, frequency and intensity of exercise, age, sex, and genetics.
  • Think about your own situation. What factors determine what you eat? Are you influenced by your friends and family or cultural background? Are there certain foods or dietary habits that are central to your family gatherings? What role does the media play in determining what you eat or how much? If you think about it, you might be surprised by how many of your food decisions are based on the expectations of others.

Careers: Careers related to nutrition

There are many career fields related to nutrition, including that of a dietitian, nutritionist, health worker, personal fitness trainer, naturopath, food services manager, and chef. The qualifications for these jobs vary but having a university degree or college diploma with some university-level biology is necessary for most health-related jobs, particularly for reaching higher levels of responsibility, pay, and status. One of these careers—that of a dietitian—is discussed next in more detail, outlining the educational qualifications required.

Dietitian

Dietitians, as experts in food and nutrition, counsel individuals, families, and communities and work as integral parts of family health-care networks. They often work in health-care institutions, such as retirement homes and hospitals, or may work for food production companies or as university professors, researching improvements in diet.

Dietitians help to create balanced menus and educate people about healthy eating and lifestyles.

In Ontario, the College of Dietitians of Ontario is the professional organization responsible for regulating the profession. Becoming a registered dietitian in Ontario requires a four-year undergraduate degree from an accredited university. Such a degree would include courses in science (for example, chemistry and biology), nutrition, management, communication, and psychology or sociology, among others.

Following the degree, a 10- to 12-month supervised practice period (called an internship) would be required, as well as successfully passing a board certification exam in nutrition and dietetics. This career is well-suited to health-minded, independent people who like helping others.

Energy for life processes

You just learned how the choices you make daily play a role in your well-being. Your body needs a certain amount of energy to carry out its basic functions and you obtain this energy by ingesting food. Ingesting too much food with reduced physical activity is a detriment to your health because the extra energy from food is turned into fat. Maintaining a healthy lifestyle is all about managing your diet and activity level. Essentially the ‘currency’ you must manage, in terms of your health and well being, is energy using the metabolic processes in your body.

In the human body and ecosystems, the ‘currency’ of energy is captured and managed by one of the most basic laws of science which states that energy cannot be created or destroyed but it can be changed from one form to another. This is called the Law of the Conservation of Energy.

Examples of the Law of the Conservation of Energy are seen in ecosystems where energy is transferred between biotic (living) and abiotic (non-living) factors.

For example:

All the metabolic energy in a functioning ecosystem comes from the process of photosynthesis which occurs when a green plant is exposed to sunlight. Sunlight is an abiotic factor. As a result, photosynthesis cycles nutrients and transforms solar energy (energy from the sun) into chemical energy (energy stored in the bonds of glucose molecules). The chemical energy stored in the bonds of glucose is used by organisms and converted to metabolic energy during the process of cellular respiration. The organisms are biotic factors.

Thus, biotic factors and abiotic factors interact together in a functioning ecosystem and these interactions cycle nutrients (matter) and transfer energy.

Energy and the laws of thermodynamics

Living things are defined in part by their capacity to collect and transfer energy to meet their metabolic needs. Consequently, all living things must obey the physical laws of nature regarding how energy is stored, used, and transferred.

What is energy?

Energy is defined as the ability to do work. It is available in a variety of forms, including light, sound, mechanical energy, chemical energy, electrical energy, radiation and heat.

All forms of energy can be divided into two types: kinetic and potential energy.

Kinetic energy is the energy that causes objects to move. This is the energy that is being used up.

Potential energy is energy that is stored for later use.

The laws of thermodynamics

Thermodynamics is a branch of science that deals with the relationship between all forms of energy. There are two Laws of Thermodynamics that will help you learn more about energy in reactions.

Definition

First law of thermodynamics

The first law of thermodynamics states that energy can be changed from one form to another, but it cannot be created or destroyed. The amount of energy in the universe is always conserved.

All forms of energy can be transformed into other forms, such as turning electricity into heat. People do this all the time when they use electric stoves, hair dryers, and electric heaters. However, whenever energy is transformed into another form, there is always some loss of energy. For example, if you were to try and convert the heat from the electric stove back into electricity, you would not get all of the energy back. This energy is not destroyed—it just becomes unusable for doing any further work.

Definition

Second law of thermodynamics

The second law of thermodynamics states that the disorder (called “entropy”) in the universe is always increasing. Each time energy is used, some will be converted (“lost”) to heat (random motion).

Every time energy is transformed into another form, some of the potential energy is converted into heat, but the heat is not always able to do further work. Some of the heat produced becomes permanently unavailable.

This means that disorder is always more likely to occur than order. What this means for life on earth is that energy must continually be added to it, in order to maintain order. For example, a sandcastle on the beach can fall apart, but it can’t spontaneously construct itself. It takes energy to create or maintain order.

Energy in reactions

The chemical energy stored in the bonds of chemicals, specifically reactants and products, is changed during the course of a chemical reaction. Let’s take a closer look at these reactions below.

Endergonic reactions

Reactions in which the reactants have less energy than their products require energy and are thus termed endergonic reactions.

These non-spontaneous reactions require continual inputs of energy until the reaction is completed.

Press here for a long description (Opens in new window)

Endergonic reactions require additional inputs of energy to raise the atoms of their reactants beyond the target energy level of their products, until they settle into a stable energy plateau. The activation energy required is usually prohibitively high, necessitating the presence of enzymes to catalyze the reaction.

Exergonic reactions

Reactions in which the reactants have more energy stored in their bonds than their products release energy, and are termed exergonic reactions.

Once initiated, these spontaneous reactions do not require any energy inputs and will continue to their completion.

Press here for a long description (Opens in new window)

Spontaneous reactions do not occur at random. For any reaction to occur, an initial amount of activation energy must be supplied, in order to destabilize the reactants’ bonds so that they may be broken and reformed. This energy of activation temporarily raises the energy level of the reactants in the system, whether the reaction proceeds spontaneously or not.

Most reactions in living systems would not proceed at all if it were not for the catalytic role provided by enzymes. As you learned in Unit 2, enzymes facilitate reactions, lowering the required activation energy by straining the reactants’ bonds, making them unstable, so that less energy is required to break them apart.

Redox reactions

In Unit 2, you learned about reduction-oxidation (redox) reactions. These reactions are very important in the metabolism of living systems.

Recall the acronym OIL RIG.

Oxidation is Loss of Electrons (and gain of oxygen). Substances that are oxidized are called reducing agents because they cause the reduction of other substances.

Reduction is Gain of Electrons (and loss of oxygen). Substances that are reduced are called oxidizing agents because they cause the oxidation of other substances.

Redox reactions occur when both oxidation and redox reactions occur simultaneously. When a reducing agent and an oxidizing agent react, a redox reaction takes place.

How is energy transferred in redox reactions during the metabolism of living things?

  • Electrons that are transferred from one atom or molecule to another atom or molecule carry energy with them. This energy can be stored or released in molecules that are involved in a metabolic process (such as photosynthesis or cellular respiration).
  • Reduced molecules or atoms receive an electron from oxidized molecules or atoms. Consequently, reduced molecules or atoms are raised to higher energy levels and oxidized molecules or atoms have lowered energy levels.
  • Reduced molecules or atoms are raised to higher energy levels because electrons repel each other. Adding more electrons to an atom is like pressing down on a spring. Just as pushing on a spring increases the potential energy of the spring, adding more electrons increases the potential energy of the atom.
  • Specialized molecules, called energy carriers or coenzymes, become reduced and oxidized as electrons are shuttled from one enzyme to the next through metabolic pathways, delivering or harvesting energy at each stage.

Let’s look at a specific examples of redox reactions in living organisms which uses a molecule called Nicotinamide Adenine Dinucleotide (NAD+),

Nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide (NAD+)

  • One energy-carrying, hydrogen-accepting coenzyme known as nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide (NAD+), is a key player in the transfer of energy.
  • When oxidized, NAD+ is electron-deficient. It is in a low-energy state, so it carries a positive overall ionic charge (as indicated by the positive symbol on the top right-hand side).
  • NAD+ readily accepts H atoms and their associated high-energy electrons as they are stripped from the higher-energy molecules in the pathways that are donating them. When NAD+ accepts a hydrogen proton and two electrons from the active site of an enzyme, it becomes its reduced, energy-rich form, NADH (see Figure X).

The reduction of NAD+ to NADH. During a metabolic reaction, high-energy electrons are stripped from molecules by enzymes that donate them to low-energy coenzymes (such as NAD+). These become high-energy reduced forms (such as NADH), which are now ready to pass on the electrons and energy associated with them.

ATP: Adenosine triphosphate

Living things need energy for their metabolism.

All aspects of metabolism require energy, including:

  • Active transport
  • Production of complex molecules like sugars and proteins
  • Provision of activation energy for chemical reactions

In living things, energy is carried and released by a special molecule called adenosine triphosphate (ATP).

If glucose is the primary source of energy for cells, how is glucose connected to the formation of ATP?

From a cellular perspective, one molecule of glucose is packed with a lot of energy (3012 kJ/mol, under typical cellular conditions)–far more energy than is required for most enzyme–catalyzed reactions to occur.

Cells can only get their energy from glucose. Glucose is packed with a lot of energy and cells need to save the unused energy by storing the energy they get from processing glucose into smaller packets. These smaller energy packets can then be used to run a variety of different processes, all while wasting as little energy as possible. These smaller energy packets are ATP.

A mole of ATP stores and releases 54 kJ of energy which is less than 2% of the energy available in the 3,012 kJ/mol of glucose.

ATP is sometimes described as the energy “currency of the cell.”

A useful analogy for ATP

Here is an analogy to help you understand the connection between glucose molecules and ATP.

You can think of glucose as a $100 bill and ATP as the $1 coins of change you get when you “break” large bill, such as glucose.

Cells need to make “metabolic change” by storing the energy they get from processing glucose into smaller packets, ATP or “loonies”, which can then be used to run a variety of different processes, all while wasting as little energy as possible.

How does ATP store and release energy?

The secret to how ATP stores and releases energy is in its structure.

The stable adenosine subunit has three phosphate groups (PO43–) attached to it. The single-bonded oxygen atoms of the phosphate groups are highly negative and repulse each other, making them unstable. This results in the formation of a high-energy (54 kJ/mol) bond between the second and third phosphate.

Cells get energy from ATP by hydrolyzing the unstable high-energy bond, which creates the two products, adenosine diphosphate (ADP) and inorganic phosphate (Pi), and the release of 30 kJ/mol of energy. This is an exergonic reaction

A T P   +   H 2 0   ↔   A D P   +   P i   +   H +

A positively charged hydrogen ion is also created because the water molecule splits up during the reaction, with the OH– ion attaching to the ADP molecule and the H+ ion floating free.

During an exergonic reaction, hydrolysis removes a phosphate group from ATP, resulting in the release of about 30 kJ/mol of energy and two products, ADP and Pi. The ions from the water molecule are shown in bold.

The ATP cycle

ATP is used to provide the energy to drive endergonic reactions involving enzymes, such as the synthesis of polypeptides, DNA, and complex polysaccharides. The energy required to make ATP is from the exergonic reactions of metabolizing (breaking down) carbohydrates, proteins or lipids.

In such cases, the Pi (inorganic phosphate) can act as an allosteric activator. It attaches to the allosteric site on the enzyme, which changes the shape of its active site, thus “turning it on” to accept substrate(s).

By providing energy for the reaction, as well as by activating the enzyme, the hydrolysis of ATP speeds up the reaction in two ways and so improves the cell’s energy efficiency.
