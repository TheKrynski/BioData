Comparing photosynthesis and respiration

You may have noticed that the process of photosynthesis and cellular respiration share some similarities.

  • Some of the same molecules are involved (for example, ATP)
  • Both use specialized organelles within the cell to carry out the process

Is it possible that the two processes are related? In the rest of this section, you will compare photosynthesis and cellular respiration in detail to see how they are related structurally and in terms of energy.

Summary equations

Photosynthesis and cellular respiration are complementary processes of one another; one creates energy storage molecules, while the other consumes them.

This is clearly seen when the two summary equations are compared, side by side. The reactants of photosynthesis are the products of cellular respiration, and vice versa.

However, though it may appear to be the exact opposite reactions, this is far from reality. The processes are different, and the intermediate compounds and enzymes used may differ.

Similarities between chloroplasts and mitochondria

The fact that these two processes are similar in so many ways suggests that they share a common origin. The similarities between chloroplasts and mitochondria are shown below:

Similarities include the following:

  1. Both have a double membrane.
  2. A cyclic process occurs in both organelles - Krebs cycle in mitochondria and Calvin cycle in the stroma of the chloroplast.
  3. An electron transport chain (ETC) occurs in both thylakoid and inner mitochondrial membranes, and it contains some proteins and cytochromes that are identical or very similar.
  4. ATP synthase uses proton motive force to power chemiosmosis during the main ATP generation phase (oxidative phosphorylation), both in the final stage of the light reactions (in the chloroplast) and in the final stage of respiration (in the mitochondria).

Plants and their role in climate change

Data available from glacial ice cores and seabed sediment cores reveal that CO 2 levels have risen and fallen over the past 2 million years. Starting in 1950, direct measurements of the actual atmospheric CO 2 levels began to be recorded annually. Atmospheric carbon dioxide concentration increased from about 280 ppm in 1950 to 385 ppm in 2009, and is currently increasing at an average rate of 2 ppm per year. The data clearly shows that CO 2 has risen dramatically. This increase appears to be in accordance with the human population explosion and increased use of fossil-fuel energy.

There is a historically significant increase in CO 2 and other greenhouse gases from natural and human sources, which is contributing to an increase in air and water temperature resulting in:

  • changing precipitation patterns
  • changing weather patterns
  • shifting climate zones

These changes influence the following:

  • species extinction rates
  • increased melting of glacial and polar ice caps
  • water levels rising and coastal flooding
  • extreme weather events to occur more often and more severely

During that time, the average global temperature has increased by about 1°C and global climate patterns have changed dramatically. The global climate system is an incredibly complex system, making establishing cause-and-effect relationships difficult.

The threat of global warming caused by excessive amounts of CO 2 in the atmosphere may be reduced if carbon could be removed from the atmosphere more quickly than we are adding it. Plants could play an important role in this removal, since carbon dioxide is required by plants.

Part 2: Connect your learning

Can plants save the world?

Consider and reflect on the following to help connect your learning to real world situations:

A global increase in the concentration of CO 2 in the atmosphere, combined with an increase in temperature, should provide an opportunity for enhanced plant growth. It should particularly benefit those that use the C4 photosynthesis pathway to fix atmospheric carbon, especially during warmer temperatures. By genetically modifying plants, scientists may be able to increase the photorespiration efficiencies of many C3 crop plants, giving them the same advantages of C4 plants. Increasing the CO 2 that is trapped by plants through increased photosynthetic production could remove enough carbon dioxide from the atmosphere to reduce or even reverse some potential climate change.
