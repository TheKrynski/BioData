Introduction

All living things require energy in order to carry out all aspects of their lives. You get your energy from the plants and animals you eat. As you learned in the previous learning activity, your lifestyle, diet and genetics play a large part in determining how your energy level and general health are affected by your choices. In this learning activity, you will learn how the process of cellular respiration turns food into useable energy for the cell.

Overview of aerobic cellular respiration

Aerobic cellular respiration is the process of capturing the energy of the electrons that are shared in the covalent bonds (C-H) of glucose, using oxygen as a final electron acceptor. The process results in the release of energy and the formation of water and carbon dioxide as products:

C 6 H 12 O 6   +   6 O 2 →   6 C O 2 +   6 H 2 O +   energy in the form of ATP

Aerobic cellular respiration can be broken down into four main stages:

  1. Glycolysis
  2. Pyruvate oxidation
  3. Krebs cycle
  4. Electron transport chain/Chemiosmosis

Aerobic cellular respiration uses energy-carrying coenzymes, enzymes, and specialized structures within the mitochondria to incrementally extract the energy of the electrons as they move toward their final electron acceptor, oxygen.

This catabolic, exothermic process follows the laws of thermodynamics. It generates free energy in the form of ATP and loses energy as waste heat during every reaction.

The electrons that are stripped from glucose have a lot of potential energy. As they move through the electron transport chain via redox reactions, the electrons lose some energy in order to drive the proton pumps, and even more is lost as heat (disorder). You can think of the electrons as being at the top of a flight of stairs and that at every stage of the respiration process, they move down the staircase, converting their potential energy to useable energy with every step.

The mitochondrion

Eukaryotic animal and plant cells are filled with cytoplasm. The cytoplasm of the cell is the medium in which all the cell components and free-floating compounds and ions are found. Cellular respiration (both aerobic and anaerobic) begins in the cytoplasm.

The rest of the process occurs in the specialized organelle called the mitochondrion.

The mitochondrion has an outer membrane and an inner membrane. The inner membrane has folds called cristae, which provide a greater surface area and specialized environments for energy-carrying reactions. The space within the inner membrane is called the matrix. Most of the reactions take place in the matrix and on the internal membrane.

The role of enzymes and coenzymes

Recall that enzymes (made up of proteins) in the body are biological catalysts that speed up chemical reactions without being used up themselves, and coenzymes are non-protein compounds that are necessary for the proper functioning of an enzyme.

You were already introduced to the energy-carrying coenzyme, NADH, in the previous learning activity. It is joined here by another, weaker energy-carrying coenzyme called FADH2.

Glucose: An energy source

Glucose is the main energy source of cellular respiration. Glucose is absorbed quickly into the bloodstream, once ingested. Cells are made permeable to glucose by the pancreatic hormone, insulin. Once inside the cytoplasm of a cell, glucose is processed for its free energy—harvested as ATP—to do immediate cellular work.

You are now going to learn more about the four stages of aerobic cellular respiration in more detail. The description of each stage starts off with an overview of the process, highlighting what happens to the main carbon-containing molecules.

Stage 1: Glycolysis

Details

Glycolysis is a 10-step enzyme catalyzed reaction that takes place in the cytoplasm of a eukaryotic cell. During glycolysis, each glucose (6 Carbons) that has entered the cytoplasm of the cell is split into two 3-carbon molecules that eventually become pyruvate (2 × 3 Carbons). Collectively, this pathway prepares the energy-rich glucose molecule for the extraction of the energy stored in its bonds. To accomplish this, however, some energy has to be invested first, in order to destabilize the glucose molecule. Two ATP molecules are expended initially by the cell, while four ATP and two NADH molecules are then produced. Thus, the net yield of energy-carrying products is two ATP and two NADH.

The glycolysis stage of aerobic cellular respiration does not require oxygen.

Stage 3: Krebs cycle

Summary: Two acetyl-CoA (2 × 2 carbons) molecules are reacted in a metabolic pathway to produce four CO2 (4 × 1 carbon) molecules, and energy carriers are reduced, harvesting electrons and protons. The Krebs cycle occurs in the mitochondrial matrix. This eight-step enzyme-catalyzed process is summarized in the flow chart below. Note that citrate is turned into isocitrate in the first step, but the number of carbons does not change and no energy is released. Note: previously, the Krebs cycle was known as the citric acid cycle as citrate is one of the intermediate molecules.

Details

The Krebs cycle carries each of the two acetyl-CoA molecules produced from one glucose molecule through further enzyme-facilitated oxidation reactions, dehydrogenation, decarboxylation, and phosphorylation. Many of the descriptive details of each of these eight reactions have purposely been omitted here. However, the following points are important to remember:

  • The actions of the eight different enzymes involved in the cycle result in the transformation of each acetyl-CoA molecule into the following molecules:

Citrate → isocitrate → α-ketoglutarate → succinyl-CoA → succinate → fumarate → malate → oxaloacetate

Each stage serves to prepare for carrying out the harvesting of energy in successive reactions or stages. (Note: you are not responsible for knowing all the intermediate steps or molecules)

  • Energy is removed from the transformed molecules in the form of ATP, NADH, and flavin adenine dinucleotide (FADH2). These energy carriers feed their energy into the fourth and final stage of aerobic cellular respiration.
  • Oxaloacetate, which begins the cycle as a reactant, is regenerated as a product during the last reactions in the cycle.

Stage 4: Electron transport chain (ETC) and chemiosmosis

  • Stage 4 occurs in the cristae of the matrix, the inner membrane, and the intermembrane space.
  • The electrons harvested from the oxidation of glucose move from electron donors (NADH and FADH2) to a final electron acceptor (O2) via a series of redox reactions called the electron transport chain (ETC).
  • The ETC is made up of a sequence of molecules called cytochrome complexes that release some energy from the electrons at each step in the chain.
  • The energy released is used to power three proton pumps that push H+ ions out across the mitochondrial matrix membrane into the intermembrane space. The resulting transmembrane proton gradient is used to make ATP by chemiosmosis.

Details

  • The 10 NADH and two FADH2 generated during the first three stages of aerobic respiration through the oxidation of glucose now move into the folds of the inner membrane within the matrix, called the cristae.
  • Various membrane-associated proteins and special compounds called cytochromes comprise a series of increasingly strong reducers, called the electron transport chain (ETC). This linear series of molecules carries out coupled redox reactions to pass on electrons from the energy carriers FADH2 and NADH to oxygen.
  • The electrons are passed along the ETC like a baton handed from runner to runner in a relay race. With each transfer, the electrons occupy a slightly more stable position on the carrier molecule. The energy they give up at each transfer is used to power the proton pumps that pump the H+ ions out of the matrix into the intermembrane space. There are five steps in this series, which will be discussed next.

Detailed steps of the electron transport chain and chemiosmosis

The NADH and FADH2 produced in the Krebs cycle have their high-energy electrons and associated protons removed in the ETC.

Step 1: Electron and Proton Removal

A membrane-bound enzyme in the cristae called NADH dehydrogenase removes the high-energy electrons from NADH. The protons ( H + ) associated with the electrons are now free to be moved by the enzyme into the matrix. The energy released is used to drive the three proton pumps that pump the H+ ions out of the matrix into the intermembrane space.

The NAD+ produced goes back into the Krebs cycle, where it gets turned again into NADH.

Step 2: Electron Transport by Ubiquinone

The FADH2 has its high-energy electrons removed by the next molecule in the chain, ubiquinone. The low-energy FAD molecule is also returned to the Krebs cycle.

The mobile electron carrier, ubiquinone, moves within the membrane. It carries electrons to the proton pump, called the b-c1 complex, which forces more H+ out of the matrix into the intermembrane space.

Step 3: Electron Transport by Cytochrome C

The electron carrier, cytochrome C, carries electrons to the third proton pump, called cytochrome C oxidase.

Most of the energy of the high-energy electrons carried by ubiquinone and cytochrome C is used to pump H+ out of the matrix into the intermembrane space, while the rest is lost as heat. The H+ that are pumped out become concentrated as they build up in the intermembrane space. These charged atoms cannot easily move back across the membrane into the matrix.

The result is that an electrochemical gradient is built up across the membrane, with H+ concentrated on the outside. This gradient contains potential energy, which eventually gets used to make ATP.

The electrons that were oxidized from glucose are at a high-energy level, and as they move through the ETC, they give up their energy in small amounts. One way to visualize this is to think of the ETC as a flight of stairs and the electrons as tennis balls bouncing down the stairs.

The energy changes taking place during the ETC are compared to a tennis ball bouncing down the stairs

As the balls bounce toward the bottom of the stairs, they transfer their potential energy to kinetic energy.

As electrons move through the electron transport chain via redox reactions, they lose some energy through driving the pumping of protons, and some is wasted as heat.

Step 4: Electron Acceptance and the Formation of Water

All of the electrons that were stripped from glucose in the Krebs cycle have had their energy used to pump protons (H+) out of the matrix and are now at a very low energy level.

These lower energy electrons must be accepted by an atom for the preceding electrons to continue to flow through the ETC.

At the end of the ETC, two electrons passing through the final complex (cytochrome oxidase) are donated to the final electron acceptor, oxygen. As one oxygen atom accepts two electrons, it also binds two protons (2H+) together, resulting in the formation of one molecule of water (H2O).

Step 5: Production of ATP by Chemiosmosis

The electrochemical gradient created by the pumping of H+ out of the matrix provides the energy needed to generate ATP.

Inside the matrix, the H+ concentration is low, so the trend is for the H+ to equalize its concentration (as with diffusion/osmosis).

Specialized H+ channel proteins that span the inner membrane open to let the H+ flow into the matrix. As with busy commuters rushing through turnstiles in a train station, the protons stream through the protein channel, to the end of which the enzyme ATP synthase is attached.

The kinetic energy delivered by the movement of the H+, called the proton motive force (PMF), powers ATP synthase to phosphorylate ADP with Pi to form ATP.

The energy that was stored in those C-H bonds of glucose that were transferred to energy carriers, and transported and harvested along the ETC to pump protons out of the matrix in order to generate the PMF, is now being used to form ATP!

Chemiosmotic ATP generation depends on constant maintenance of the H+ reservoir in the intermembrane space, which, in turn, is accomplished by the movement of electrons through the ETC. The electrons will only keep moving if oxygen is present to accept them at the end of the process—hence the need for oxygen in aerobic cellular respiration.

Summary

Thus, during the four stages of aerobic cellular respiration, the energy of the electrons that are shared in the covalent bonds (C-H) of glucose is harvested, using oxygen to produce carbon dioxide, water and the release of energy which is stored as ATP.

The overall chemical equation for the four stages of aerobic cellular respiration is:

Alternative metabolic pathways

  • So far, you have only seen glucose as a source of energy a cell can use, but you already know that fats and other lipids contain a lot of energy that gets used up when glucose is unavailable.
  • The aerobic breakdown (catabolism) of fats is accomplished by a process called β-oxidation.
  • Proteins can also provide a source of energy through a process called deamination.

β-oxidation of fats

Enzymes in the matrix add CoA to the terminal ends of fatty-acid tails, allowing for the stripping of electrons from the numerous C-H bonds by NAD+ to NADH. The end result is the production of acetyl-CoA molecules. These enter the Krebs cycle and respiration continues as usual with its regular production of NADH and FADH2 as seen below.

Deamination of proteins

  • Proteins can be broken down into amino acids, which can then be deaminated to produce energy-rich molecules that feed into cellular respiration pathways.
  • Amino acids all contain an amino group (-NH2), which is removed during the process of deamination. Because of their unique functional groups, when different amino acids are deaminated, they form products that can feed into glycolysis or the Krebs cycle as seen below.

For example, alanine becomes pyruvate, and glutamate becomes α-ketoglutarate (one of the compounds of the Krebs cycle). The process of deamination, however, results in the production of the toxic substance, urea, which is filtered from the body by the kidneys and excreted in urine.

Anaerobic cellular respiration

Whether oxygen is present or not during glycolysis, the first set of electrons that are oxidized from glucose reduce NAD+ to NADH. Cells do not have limitless supplies of NAD+ available to undergo reduction reactions to gain the electrons. Until the NADH can offload their electrons to an acceptor like oxygen, they become unavailable for further electron removal.

In anaerobic conditions where oxygen is not present, another electron acceptor must be used. In the absence of oxygen, animal muscle cells and yeast cells have evolved different mechanisms to handle this problem.

Lactic acid fermentation in muscle cells

Humans undertaking strenuous exercise use up oxygen faster than they can metabolize glucose, so they will temporarily shift to producing lactic acid (lactate), which is toxic and is the cause of muscle cramps. In the absence of oxygen, the protons and electrons that were removed from glucose during glycolysis attach back onto pyruvate, creating lactic acid. Increasing oxygen delivery reduces the uncomfortable symptoms produced by this process and restarts aerobic cellular respiration.

Alcohol fermentation in yeast cells

In the case of alcoholic fermentation in yeast cells:

  • Pyruvate is decarboxylated (CO2 is removed), resulting in the formation of the intermediate compound, acetaldehyde.
  • Acetaldehyde is then reduced as NADH dumps its electrons onto it to form ethanol (alcohol).
  • Yeast used in the brewing of beer and winemaking undergoes this process until the alcohol reaches a toxicity concentration of about 12%, killing all the cells.

Energy efficiency of respiration

While anaerobic respiration can be accomplished by every type of cell, it still can only provide the meager glycolytic payoff of two ATP per glucose molecule, which is much less than the 32 ATP produced by aerobic respiration as a whole.

Even with this great payoff, aerobic respiration is still governed by the very real problem posed by the second law of thermodynamics, which states that disorder is always increasing. This reminds you that with every one of the 30 or so reactions that make up the oxidation of glucose, energy is being incrementally stored; however, even more is being lost.

Only 32%? That may not sound like a great payoff, but it is a lot better than anaerobic cellular respiration, which only produces two ATP, translating to 2% efficiency.

In aerobic respiration, some of the H+ leaks out of the proton reservoir and makes its way back into the matrix, thus avoiding ATP synthase, and some of the energy from the proton pumps is actually used to power processes other than just ATP production. Therefore, the real amount of net ATP actually available is more like 30 ATP, reducing the real efficiency of the process to 30%. Losing this much energy isn’t unusual in the context of energy transfers, all of which have to follow the second law of thermodynamics. The most efficient internal combustion engines are only able to transfer about 25% of gasoline energy into the desired kinetic energy to turn the tires of a car.

Part 2: Connect your learning

Recall that you had been asked if you know of any fermented food products at the start of the learning activity. You may now understand why organisms undergo this alternate pathway to metabolize glucose. Fermented products are not just new trendy products in grocery stores. Archaeological evidence from the Zagros Mountains of Iran suggests that fermentation has existed as a food-processing method since around 5,000 BCE. Today, every region of the Earth seems to have a cultural delicacy that uses biological organisms to ferment food or drink, in order to create a new flavour. Kimchee, sauerkraut, pickles, injera, sago, yoghurt, and sour cream are just a few examples of how varied fermented foods from around the world can be.

When most people think of fermentation, they think of brewing beer and winemaking. Many cultures around the world discovered how to create fermented beverages early on in their history. Some theorists suggest that the aristocracy of European nations stayed healthier than their peasantry by drinking fermented beverages, rather than the contaminated water available to their supporting classes. Currently, alcoholic beverages support a multi-billion-dollar industry, internationally. Beer and wine producers often hire microbiologists, organic chemists, and engineers, all of whom require a B.Sc.

Often, a graduate degree or an additional two years of study in brewing or winemaking from a specialist institution may be required, if one plans to become a master brewer, vintner (someone who makes or sells wine), oenologist (someone who studies wines), or sommelier (wine steward). With so many successful microbreweries in Canada, there is likely to always be some market for these skills. The transferability of the training is high, considering the large number of foods that rely on fermentation by living organisms, as part of their production.

Fermentation is used in more industrial applications than just food manufacturing. The use of maize (corn), which is fermented to make ethanol fuel, has supported a growing agricultural and industrial sector in North America in the last few decades, as increasingly more fuels contain an ethanol component. Ethanol is mixed with gasoline to reduce our reliance on fossil fuels and to reduce carbon dioxide emissions, which have been implicated as a contributing factor in global climate change.
