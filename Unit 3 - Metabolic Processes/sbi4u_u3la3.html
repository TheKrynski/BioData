Photosynthesis

All living things get their energy from the sun, either directly or through the consumption of organic carbon-containing compounds (mainly sugars) made by plants. Understanding which conditions and reactants can provide the best results has greatly increased crop yields and the nutritional value of foods. The term “photosynthesis” literally means, “making with light.” Although this definition makes photosynthesis sound simple, the complex structures, processes, pathways, and metabolic participants involved actually make it quite a complicated process.

In this learning activity, you will learn how glucose is made from the simple ingredients of carbon dioxide, water and light energy.

Overview of photosynthesis

The process by which light energy is captured and channeled into the production of food molecules is called photosynthesis. During this process there are dozens of reactions, involving many enzymes and coenzymes at every step. Reactions occur in different structures and under varying conditions, but can be summarized in the following photosynthetic equation:

6CO2 + 6H2O + light energy → C6H12O6 + 6O2

Carbon dioxide + water + light energy → glucose + oxygen gas

Plants, algae, and some bacteria all use light energy to form carbohydrates. Some bacteria are chemosynthetic and do not need light energy at all; they get their energy from other molecules. These ancient organisms likely evolved before oxygen-producing organisms existed.

In this course, you will concentrate on the process of photosynthesis in green oxygen-producing eukaryotic plants.

Structures involved in photosynthesis

Photosynthesis occurs in specialized organelles within plants, called chloroplasts. Within the chloroplasts, there are structures containing special light-sensitive pigments called chlorophyll.

Chloroplasts

In green leaves, the cells just below the epidermal layer contain the green organelles called chloroplasts. They are the small and abundant circles that can be seen in the palisade mesophyll cells. They are also seen in the spongy mesophyll cells, but are found there less frequently.


Chloroplast

The chloroplast is the organelle that is responsible for carrying out photosynthesis in plants. Its appearance is like that of a miniature cell with an outer membrane.

Thylakoids

The chloroplast contains stacks of membranous disks called thylakoids. The thylakoid membrane is embedded with many proteins.

Granum

Thylakoids are grouped into columns, each called a granum (plural: grana, from Greek, meaning “stack of coins”).

Lumen

The internal environment of the thylakoid is called the lumen.

Lamellae

Some thylakoids from different grana are connected together by flat bands of ribbon-shaped membrane, called lamellae

Stroma

All of these structures are suspended in an aqueous medium called the stroma.

Chlorophyll

Chlorophyll, which gives the chloroplast its green colour, also provides it with its light-absorbing capability. Chlorophyll is a molecule which is bound to the membranes of the thylakoid.

Chlorophyll comes in two main forms (called a and b) that differ only in one functional group: methyl (-CH3) in chlorophyll a, and carbonyl (-CHO) in chlorophyll b. These two forms of chlorophyll belong to a group of molecules called pigments. Pigments absorb light energy at specific electromagnetic wavelengths, depending on the pigment’s colour. The following image shows the electromagnetic spectrum. Plants’ pigments absorb light mostly within the visible spectrum. Other pigments, called accessory pigments, include carotenoids and xanthophylls.

Why are plants green?

Chlorophylls a and b absorb light at the blue-violet and red wavelengths (400–500 nm and 600–700 nm respectively), and generally do not absorb, but instead reflect, the green light wavelengths from 500–600 nm. This is why green plants are green. Only chlorophyll a can absorb the energy and transfer the electrons required for photosynthesis. However, chlorophyll b, and various other accessory pigments, can absorb energy and pass electrons to chlorophyll a, thus allowing a wider range of light energy to be harvested. Their collective absorptions add to form a combined absorption spectrum that allows for the entire range of visible light wavelengths to be captured. Their combined effect is called the action spectrum.

The sum total of the absorption of light energy by all of the pigments in plants is called the action spectrum. None of these pigments absorb wavelengths of light in the green part of the spectrum between 500 and 600 nm. This means that those wavelengths are reflected by the leaf, while all other colours are absorbed.

Two stages of photosynthesis

Photosynthetic reactions are traditionally divided into two stages. The first stage requires light, so its reactions are called “light-dependent reactions,” or just “light reactions,” while the second stage’s reactions do not require light and are therefore called “light-independent reactions,” or “dark reactions.” It is important that you do not assume that the “dark reactions” only occur in the absence of light, as they occur whether or not light is present. Thus, this stage is now referred to as the Calvin Cycle. The Calvin Cycle, which is responsible for combining carbon into sugar molecules, is powered by the energy harvested during the light reactions. The process of combining the carbon atoms from carbon dioxide into a glucose molecule is called “fixing” the carbon.

Each stage can be broken down into three steps:

Stage 1: Light-dependent reactions, or light reactions

  1. Capture of light energy
  2. Transfer of light energy by intermediate energy carriers
  3. Transformation of light energy into chemical potential energy (ATP and NADPH)

Stage 2: Light-independent reactions, or Calvin cycle

  1. Carbon fixation
  2. Reduction of PGA
  3. Regeneration of RuBP

    The details of each of these two stages will be described in the next sections.

1. Capture of light energy

  • Photon
  • Antenna System
  • Primary electron acceptor

Photon

When a photon of light energy hits a chlorophyll molecule that is bound to the thylakoid membrane, an electron absorbs much of this energy.

Antenna System

The electron becomes excited to a higher energy level and leaves the chlorophyll a in order to move to neighbouring pigments. This association of several chlorophyll a and carotenoids, called an antenna system, passes on the excited electron to the reaction centre.

Primary electron acceptor

From there, the excited electron is captured by a primary electron acceptor which oxidizes the reaction centre to gain the electron.

Photosystem II

There are two different arrangements of pigments and proteins connected with antenna systems and a reaction centre. These are known as:

a. photosystem II (or photosystem 680)

b. photosystem I (or photosystem 700).

The numbers 680 and 700 differentiate the two photosystems by indicating the wavelengths at which each absorbs light most effectively. Note: This diagram only shows photosystem 680.

Note: the naming system of photosystem I and II, are numbered contrary to their actual sequence in the cycle since II comes before I. The reason is that photosystem I was discovered before photosystem II.

  • Photolysis

  • Chemiosmotic gradient

Photolysis

The energy passed through photosystem 680 is used to remove electrons from water and release protons (H+ ions) in an enzyme-catalyzed process called photolysis (from “photo,” meaning “light,” and “-lysis,” meaning “split”). The oxygen that is released diffuses out of the chloroplast (which explains how oxygen is a product of photosynthesis).

Chemiosmotic gradient

The protons are pumped into the lumen (interior) of the thylakoids and create a chemiosmotic gradient. Photosystem II feeds the same electrons to photosystem I.

Electron transport chain

The transfer of electrons from photosystem II to photosystem I is accomplished along a pathway made up of an intermediate protein and metal ion energy carriers. The electrons are transferred between the molecules following the sequence:

plastoquinone (pQ) → 6-f complex → plastocyanin (pC).

These are known collectively as the electron transport chain (ETC). As you learned in the last learning activity, the electron transport chain is a metabolic pathway in which an electron enters a carrier complex at a specific site and is transferred within the protein to emerge at a different site, to then be passed from a carrier to another protein complex via redox reactions.

Another intermediate energy carrier called ferrodoxin (Fd) transfers the excited electron from photosystem I to the enzyme, NADP reductase.

During the redox reactions, every transfer of electrons that occurs among the intermediate energy carriers results in some transformation of energy, which is used to move protons, via proton pumps, from the stroma into the lumen, or is wasted as heat.

By the time an electron has reached photosystem I, it has delivered or “lost” much of its original excitation energy. But because the pigments in photosystem I also channel the light energy they receive into boosting the electrons, the electron is at an even higher energy level than it was before..

Summary

Electrons are excited by energy absorbed from light photons, first in photosystem II and then in photosystem I. The photon energy is used to eject a high-energy electron from chlorophyll, which is then passed from one electron acceptor to another via redox reactions. This releases energy in a step-by-step manner, and finally ends up being incorporated into NADPH, upon its reduction. The electrons are replaced by free electrons that were generated by the splitting of water at the beginning of the process.

3. Transformation of light energy into chemical potential energy (ATP and NADPH)

Energy containing molecules: NADPH and ATP

The energy in the photons of light captured by chlorophyll is used to make two kinds of energy-containing molecules: NADPH and ATP.

ATP is produced after photosystem II.

NADPH is produced after photosystem I. NADP reductase uses the energy carried by the excited electrons to attach protons (H+) from the stroma to NADP+ (nicotinamide adenine diphosphate, oxidized), producing NADPH (reduced) on the stroma side of the thylakoid membrane.

ATP Production using Electrochemical Energy

ATP is produced using the electrochemical energy provided by hydrogen protons (H+). Electron transport creates an electrochemical potential of hydrogen protons across the photosynthetic membrane through two reactions:

  1. First, the release of H+ during the oxidation of water by photosystem II

  2. Second, the movement of H+ from the stroma side to the lumen side by the coupled reactions of photosystem II and the cytochrome 6-f complex. This last process results in a higher concentration of H+ on the lumen side of the thylakoid membrane.

    The concentration difference of protons across the membrane is responsible for the proton motive force that drives chemiosmosis, which is used to generate ATP. This is the same ATP-generating process that you learned about in the previous lesson on respiration.

Proton Motive Force, Chemiosmosis and ATP production

In this process, H+ ions become so concentrated in the lumen that they are pushed out by their own numbers. Due to their charge, the H+ ions cannot effectively diffuse across the membrane. Instead, they are channelled through a transmembrane enzyme complex called ATP synthase, which converts the proton motive force into the chemical energy of ATP. This enzyme catalyzes a phosphorylation reaction, forming ATP through the addition of inorganic phosphate (Pi) to ADP, as protons pass through it to the stroma. The energy generated in these light-dependent reactions, stored in ATP and NADPH, is used in the next stage of photosynthesis, known as light- independent reactions.

Non-cyclic photophosphorylation

It is important to note that green plants predominantly use non-cyclic photophosphorylation. This means that the electrons that are donated by chlorophyll a are never returned to it. Instead, the electrons are accepted by NADP, at the end of the process, to form NADPH. The electrons being donated from chlorophyll a need to be replaced, however, which is accomplished by supplying electrons that are continuously being stripped from water.

The path of the electrons is a linear sequence that looks like this:

Water → photosystem II → ETC → photosystem I → ETC → NADPH

The ETC for non-cyclic photophosphorylation shown. The pathway of the excited electron is shown as the dotted line. The hydrogen protons generated by the oxidation of water and the ETC build up inside the thylakoid lumen, where they help to create a proton gradient across the thylakoid membrane. This gradient (proton motive force) is used by ATP synthase to generate ATP, whose energy is used later to help make glucose.

Cyclic photophosphorylation

In contrast, during cyclic photophosphorylation, which occurs in some photosynthetic bacteria, the electrons that are donated by chlorophyll a are returned to it via the ETC. Only ATP is generated from the H+ proton gradient that is created. Since there is no free electron available to attach to NADP+, there is no NADPH produced. The path of the electrons is a loop that cycles between photosystem I and the ETC.

Stage 2: Light-independent reactions (Calvin cycle)


Overview

The second stage of photosynthesis, whose reactions are called the light-independent reactions (or the Calvin Cycle), uses the ATP and H+ protons from the light-dependent reactions to reduce carbon from CO2 to form carbohydrate molecules like glucose. The light-independent reactions can occur in sunshine or in darkness, since they do not depend on photons for energy.

Carbon reduction involves a series of enzyme-mediated reactions in the stroma of the chloroplast. To eventually build complex organic molecules containing many carbon atoms, such as sugar, plants must start by attaching single carbon atoms to smaller carbon-containing molecules. All plants and algae obtain the single carbon atoms from CO2, available from the environment, and eventually use these atoms to produce glucose through the Calvin cycle. To form one molecule of glucose (C6H12O6), six carbon atoms must be combined. Since one carbon atom is combined per loop of the cycle, the Calvin cycle must run six times for every glucose molecule produced hence the equation for photosynthesis:

6CO2 + 6H2O + light energy → C6H12O6 + 6O2

The Calvin cycle can be broken down into three phases:

  1. Carbon fixation
  2. Reduction of PGA
  3. Regeneration of RuBP

    Carbon fixation
  • Phase 2:
    Reduction of PGA
  • Phase 3:

Phase 1: Carbon fixation

In the first phase of the Calvin cycle, a CO2 molecule is added to a five-carbon compound called RuBP (ribulose 1,5-bisphosphate), which is already present in the stroma. The enzyme, RuBP carboxylase (commonly called rubisco), catalyzes the exergonic reaction between RuBP and CO2. This phase is called carbon fixation because carbon atoms from carbon dioxide are being incorporated into organic molecules. Biochemists call this incorporation “fixing” the carbon atoms.

The combination of RuBP and CO2 creates an unstable intermediate compound, which immediately splits, yielding two molecules of the 3-carbon compound, PGA (3-phosphoglycerate). Three CO2 (1C x 3 = 3C) and three RuBP (5C x 3 = 15C) undergo this stage of the Calvin cycle to produce six PGA (18C) required to eventually make one molecule of the 3-carbon product of this stage, glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate, or G3P). Since one entire turn of the cycle represents the reactions involved for each CO2 that enters it, the cycle must be repeated three times to produce one molecule of G3P (3C). Since it takes two G3P molecules to make one glucose molecule (6C), the cycle must run six times per glucose molecule.

Phase 2: Reduction of PGA

In the second phase, each of the six PGA molecules created from carbon fixation is raised to a higher energy level by the addition of a phosphate group. The phosphate comes from the ATP generated during the light-dependent reactions.

The resulting molecule is called 1,3-bisphosphoglycerate (PGAP). This molecule is quickly converted to G3P in a reduction reaction, using the NADPH that was also generated during the light-dependent reactions. Thus, six ATP and six NADPH are used to produce one G3P molecule that goes on to become glucose.

One of the six G3P leaves the cycle, while the other five remain behind to replenish the supply of RuBP to keep the cycle going. The G3P molecule that leaves the cycle is used to produce a variety of carbohydrate compounds such as glucose and fructose which can then be synthesized into sucrose or starch.

Phase 3: Regeneration of RuBP

In the third and final phase of the Calvin cycle, the remaining five G3P molecules, in combination with three phosphates and three ATP, synthesize three energy-rich RuBP molecules. This process is called regeneration. Recall that one RuBP is required to fix each CO2 molecule as it enters the Calvin cycle; therefore, regeneration is a crucial factor in the continuation of the Calvin cycle.

The three phases of the Calvin cycle. It produces one molecule of G3P for every three molecules of CO2 that enters the cycle. G3P is used to produce glucose and other sugars. Where does the energy in NADPH and ATP that feeds the Calvin cycle come from?

The energy in ATP and NADPH comes from the light-dependent reactions. It is transferred to the Calvin cycle during the dark reactions.


Summarize the events of the light-independent reactions by referring to the reactants, products, and cellular components involved.

CO2 molecules enter into a three-step metabolic pathway called the Calvin cycle. ATP provides energy for enzymes to add the carbon from the CO2 to intermediate carbon- and phosphate-rich molecules, eventually yielding the energy-rich G3P molecule, which leaves the Calvin cycle. NADPH is then used with more ATP to regenerate the carbon-rich intermediate RuBP, so that the cycle can begin again with the entry of more CO2.

What is the relationship between the light reactions and the Calvin Cycle?

The light reactions use light energy and water to provide the energy (in the form of ATP and NADPH) for the Calvin Cycle, which fix carbon from gaseous carbon dioxide into energy-rich sugar molecules.

What happens to the 3-carbon sugar produced at the end of photosynthesis.

G3P can be joined to form glucose, which can be added to other monosaccharides to make a disaccharide, such as sucrose, or a polysaccharide, such as starch or cellulose.

Energy use and storage in plants

Glucose is produced by enzyme-catalyzed reactions in the stroma of the chloroplast or in the cytoplasm of the plant cell. Glucose can then be used immediately by mitochondria in the cell to release energy for metabolism or provide raw materials, so that cellulose for cell walls can be built.

Under optimal conditions, plants often produce more glucose than they require immediately, so they will store extra glucose molecules as starch. Sucrose may also be produced and transported to other parts of the plant through the phloem (vascular tissue) and ends up in fruits (for example, in strawberries), in the stalk (for example, in sugarcane), or in the roots (for example, in beets and onions).

Types of photosynthesis: C3, C4, and CAM

There are variations to the type of photosynthesis you have just learned about in the previous section. These variations in how photosynthesis occurs, divide plants into two groups, based on the number of carbons of the first molecule formed from the fixing of carbon in the production of G3P. The groups are called:

  1. C3 plants
  2. C4 plants. Within C4 plants there is another division, based on how the CO2 is processed, called CAM.

C3 and C4 plants are structurally different at the cellular level and their structure is closely linked to their function.

C3 and C4 plants adapt to temperatures that are less than optimal for photosynthesis by using different methods to manage or prevent photorespiration. Photorespiration is a process that reduces the efficiency of photosynthesis, thus preventing photosynthesis when unfavourable temperatures are present. Rubisco, the enzyme that catalyzes the first step in the Calvin cycle, is required to fix carbon dioxide. However, Rubisco can also react with oxygen instead of carbon dioxide. If this occurs, it would lead to a less efficient photosynthetic rate. Photorespiration results when plants try to conserve water loss when the temperature rises above the optimal temperature for photosynthesis as seen in the image below.

As it turns out, C3 and C4 plants differ in their rate of photorespiration. The higher the plant’s rate of photorespiration, the less efficient it is at converting sunlight into food. C4 plants have important adaptations that make them more efficient at higher temperatures than C3 plants, but less efficient at lower temperatures.

C3 Plants

Plants that fix carbon into molecules containing three carbon atoms (like G3P via the Calvin Cycle, as described previously) are called C3 plants.

This designation differentiates C3 plants, such as trees, wheat, barley, potatoes, cotton, and sugar beets from other plants.

These C3 plants use the enzyme rubisco (also known as RuBP carboxylase) to join CO2 taken from the air and fix it to a 5-carbon molecule (RuBP) to create two copies of the 3-carbon molecule, G3P.

The C3 plants make up 95% of the earth’s plant biomass. However, C3 plants cannot grow well in hot, dry areas because these conditions produce higher rates of photorespiration for them, which limit their growth.

For example, at a growing temperature of 25oC, 20% of the photosynthetically fixed carbon is lost by C3 plants to photorespiration. At temperatures higher than 28℃, increased rates of photorespiration can cause the loss of 25–50% of their photosynthetically fixed carbon.

C4 Plants

Plants that fix carbon into molecules containing four carbon atoms are called C4 plants.

This group of plants includes corn, sugar cane, sorghum, and crabgrass. C4 plants are more efficient at photosynthesis under hot, dry conditions.

C4 plants have important adaptations that make them more efficient at higher temperatures than C3 plants, but less efficient at lower temperatures.

Crassulacean acid metabolism (CAM)

Crassulacean acid metabolism, or CAM, is another way that some water-storing succulent plants like pineapples, cacti, aloe vera, and orchids cope, especially in hot and dry climates.

These plants limit photorespiration by carrying out the C4 pathway at night and switching to the Calvin cycle during the day.

CAM plants differ from C4 plants because they combine the C4 pathway and Calvin cycle within the same cell, but carry them out at different times.

This temporal separation is accomplished in part by reversing the timing of stomatal openings that you would normally see in most other plants.

The stomata (singular, stoma) are specialized pores on the surface of leaves that allow CO2 in and H2O out, and which can be opened or closed by the plant. Most plants keep their stomata open during the day to absorb CO2 when photosynthesis is active, and closed at night to prevent water loss.

At night they fix CO2, using the same C4 pathway as C4 plants, to accumulate carbon compounds. These compounds are then decarboxylated during the day.

This causes a high concentration of CO2 during the day, which activates the C3 pathway and suppresses photorespiration.

Thus, by modifying leaf structure, photosynthetic processes, and the timing of these processes, C4 and CAM plants can work around the problem of photorespirative loss of carbon (as CO2) that is found in C3 plants.

C4 plants do this by spatially isolating high concentrations of CO2 in two different cells, while CAM plants temporally isolate CO2 (day versus night) within the same cell.
