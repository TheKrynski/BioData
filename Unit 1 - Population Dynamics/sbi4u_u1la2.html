Defining populations

A population is defined as a group of individual organisms of the same species that live together in the same geographic area.

Questions to consider while studying population dynamics:

  1. How can you define this area?
  2. Is it just a local patch of forest or does it include an entire country or region?

The boundaries between populations are not usually clear-cut. Biologists define populations based on the needs of their research. Sometimes they define a population over a wide area, like “all of the monarch butterflies in eastern North America.” At other times, they define the population within a much smaller area, like “the number of monarch butterflies in a field."

Biologists describe populations characteristics using two measures, population density and population dispersion.
Population density describes how many individuals there are in a given area. Population dispersion describes how the individuals are distributed over the area.

Population Density

The density of a population is the number of individuals per unit area. It tells us more about the status of the population than is possible with just a simple count of its size. For example, density can be used to compare populations of different sizes to see if both are using their available habitat to the same degree.

Population density (D) is calculated by taking the total number of individuals (N) and dividing it by the space (S) that they occupy. (Record this formula in your notebook).

Notebook

Population Density ( D ) =   Total number of individuals (N) Space Occupied by Individuals (s)

D = N S

Space is measured in units of area (for example, m 2 , k m 2 , ha, and so on). The unit chosen will depend on the population being investigated.

Measuring the density of an area occupied by ants: Density per square metre would be a good measure.
Measuring the density of an area occupied by elephants: Density per square kilometre would be a good measure.

Ecological Density

Sometimes, there are portions of an area surveyed that do not provide ecologically suitable habitat for the species. This means that individuals would not normally be found there. A density estimate that includes those areas would not reflect the true density for that population.

For example, even though monarch butterflies are found throughout most of Ontario, they are not normally found in deep forests. Monarchs feed on flowers that bloom in open areas like roadsides and fields. If you included the deep forests in your calculation of area, then you would be greatly underestimating actual monarch density.

The size of the area that is actually usable by a species is called the ecological space (SE).

To get a better estimate of how dense a population really is, biologists use ecological density (DE), which adjusts the area used in the calculation to reflect the amount of suitable habitat (SE) available for the species.

Ecological Density   ( D E ) =

Total number of individuals (N) Amount of suitable habital available for the species   ( S E )

D E = N S E

Population Dispersion

Organisms are not always evenly distributed (dispersed) over the area they occupy. There are three main patterns of dispersion (or spatial distribution):

Clumped dispersion

Clumped dispersion occurs when organisms group together in an area.

In animals clumped dispersion, may occur for:

  • protection
  • exploitation of a resource

For example: Pigeons, sparrows, and crows are often seen clustered together in flocks, feeding on seeds or hiding in trees.

In plants, clumped dispersion can also occur because of asexual reproduction. For example, some grasses grow in clumps that sprout around one individual. In between these clumps there are few, if any, individuals present.

Uniform dispersion

Uniform dispersion means that there is an equal spacing of individuals across an area.

In animals, uniform dispersion can occur when each individual is guarding a similar-sized territory for feeding or nesting.

You see this in many animals that nest in large colonies, such as gulls and sea lions. They spread themselves out evenly on a beach area, with each individual defending a small territory on which to raise their young.

In plants, a uniform dispersion can happen when each individual plant is using up all of the resources around it, so that no other individual can get close. This creates a uniform dispersion of plants with equal-sized territories around each individual plant.

Random dispersion

Random dispersion Occurs when individuals are scattered randomly over an area. This is not the same as uniform distribution because the distance between individuals does not have to be equal. Random dispersion can even result in small clumps that form just by chance.

Random dispersions tend to happen when habitat conditions are uniform across the area and the individuals are not affected by each other.

Random dispersion is rare in nature, but some plants (for example, rainforest trees) and microscopic marine organisms (for example, algae) do disperse randomly.

Few populations will match one distribution exactly. Most of them are a mixture of the different types.

Sampling populations

In nature, it is rarely possible or practical to count every individual in a population. Organisms do not line up and wait to be counted. Sometimes, there are too many in an area to count all of them individually.

For example, would you want to count every clump of grass in a field? It could take you months. At other times, the population is too spread out, or the individuals travel too far, to be easily captured in one survey. As well, many organisms, like fish in the ocean, are not easily observed, making them almost impossible to count directly.

Because of these difficulties, biologists often use sampling techniques to estimate population size.

Several sampling methods can be used, depending on:

  • how large the population is
  • the way that it is dispersed
  • how difficult it is to see

Next, you will learn about the two most common types of sampling methods used by biologists, quadrat sampling and mark and recapture sampling.

Quadrat Sampling

Quadrat sampling is used for counting abundant organisms, like plants, or organisms living beneath the soil, like earthworms, that do not move much and can easily be seen. Instead of counting every individual in a habitat such as an open field, counts are done within small patches that are chosen at random, in order to represent the entire habitat.

Quadrat sampling steps

  1. An area in which a population is being studied is mapped out.
  2. A grid is drawn over the map.
  3. Grid squares within the mapped area are chosen at random to be sampled.
  4. The actual sampling area on the ground is defined using a square made of wooden sticks or plastic pipes, called a quadrat. In surveys of small plants, or soil-inhabiting organisms like worms, the quadrat is usually 1 m 2 in size.

Calculating the average value per quadrat

  • First, all of the individuals found within a quadrat are counted.
  • This process is repeated several times over the grid.
  • The counts within each quadrat are summed up and the average value per quadrat is calculated.
  • Because the locations of the quadrats are chosen at random, the average count per quadrat should give a good estimate of the average count per square metre for the entire area.

Limitations

The quadrat sampling method provides an estimate of population size. The accuracy of that estimate depends on whether

  • the quadrats sampled are representative of the area; if not, then the estimate may be either too high or too low.
  • the organism has a heavily clumped dispersion; if so, the quadrats may have missed the clumps just by chance, making the population estimate too low. Or, too many clumps may have been sampled just by chance, and so the estimate will be too high. Taking more samples will help limit the chances that clumps are either overlooked or overrepresented in the study.

Mark and Recapture Sampling Method

Unlike plants, most animals move around, so they can’t be captured easily within a quadrat. Some organisms, like fish, are difficult to count, as they live in areas where they can’t easily be seen. For organisms like these, the mark and recapture sampling method can be used to estimate population size.

Mark and recapture sampling procedure

  1. To estimate population size, a sample of individuals is taken from the population and marked in some way.
  2. Marked individuals are then returned to the population.
  3. After a short time, a second sample is taken.
  4. The ratio of the number of marked individuals in the second sample (called recaptures) to the unmarked ones gives an estimate of population size.

Thus the mark and recapture formula to estimate the population size is:

Total Population (N) = Total # marked (M) x Size of second sample (n) # of recaptures (m)

N = M n m

N = M n m

N = 56   ×   75 6

N = 700

The biologist estimates that the population size of lake trout in the small lake in Ontario is 700.


Limitations of the mark and recapture method

The mark and recapture method can only provide an estimate of population size. For the estimate to be accurate, the method assumes that:

  • the proportion of marked and unmarked individuals between the first capture and the second sample does not change. This can only happen if the population has no immigration or emigration, and if the length of time between samples is short enough for no births or deaths to occur.
  • the marked individuals have the same chance of getting caught in the second sample as any of the unmarked ones. This means that marking should not affect their survival or their chance of being recaptured.

The polar bear is the top predator in the Arctic. As the top predator, it plays a key role in the Arctic ecosystem. It is also very important to northern aboriginal cultures. Polar bears are found throughout the Arctic in Russia, Alaska, Canada, Greenland, and Norway. The world population of polar bears is around 20,000 to 25,000, with about 10,000 to 12,000 found in Canada.

For thousands of years, polar bears thrived in the Arctic, but now their existence is threatened. Globally, polar bears are impacted by pollution, over-hunting, and climate change. The loss of their sea ice habitat due to climate change is now considered the greatest threat to their survival.

The U.S. Geological Survey predicts that two-thirds of the world’s polar bears could disappear by 2050 because of loss of sea ice due to climate change. Over the last 30 years, the area covered by Arctic sea ice has been steadily shrinking.

The importance of sea ice

Why is less ice a bad thing for bears?

Polar bears depend on the sea ice for hunting seals, which make up over 99% of their diet. The seals live on the ice in the middle of the Arctic Ocean and Hudson Bay. Polar bears need to travel far out onto the sea ice to reach them. Bears need to eat enough seals during the winter to build up sufficient fat reserves to last them through the summer.

During summer, the sea ice melts and the bears are forced onto the land, where they can’t get food until the ice reforms within about four months. As the Arctic continues to warm up annually due to climate change, this ice-free period lasts longer, increasing the risk that the bears will starve.

Female polar bears are particularly vulnerable, because they have to provide milk for their cubs. The cubs are born on hillsides in protected dens that their mothers dug out of the snow in late autumn. In early spring, the mother and her cubs emerge from the den. The mother has not eaten for months and is losing body weight rapidly as she provides milk for her growing cubs. She has to take her cubs out onto the sea ice as soon as possible, so that she can start feeding on seals.

She does not have much time. As spring turns to summer, the sea ice starts to melt. Large gaps of open water appear in the ice between the shore and where the bears are feeding. Adult polar bears can swim long distances, but will eventually drown. The cubs have a harder time swimming. Many more now risk drowning, as the sea ice melts three weeks earlier than it did 30 years ago

Polar bears in Hudson Bay

The Canadian population of polar bears in the Southern Arctic, around the western shores of Hudson Bay, is the best studied polar bear population in the world. Biologists use a variety of methods to study this population, including satellite tracking

The polar bears in Hudson Bay are already near the southern limit of their range. As the Arctic warms up, the rate of sea ice loss is most dramatic in southern areas like Hudson Bay. So, this is the area where you would expect to find polar bear populations threatened the most. Hudson Bay is warmer now than it was 20 years ago. The sea ice in Hudson Bay is now melting earlier in the spring and forming later in the fall than it did before. This shortens the period when polar bears can feed on seals

All bears in the Hudson Bay population must go without food (which is called fasting) for at least four months during the summer ice-free season (July to October).

Pregnant females, however, may go without eating for up to eight months (July to February) as they give birth and care for their cubs in the den until the next spring. This fasting period is growing longer, since the ice is melting sooner every year.

As a result, the bears are getting thinner and their cubs are getting smaller, which increases their risk of mortality.

The lack of ice is causing this polar bear population to decline at an average rate of 1% per year (a 22% decline between 1987 and 2004). Further evidence of stress in these bears is that some are resorting to cannibalism—a behaviour never seen before in this population.

However, as the bears become more desperate for food in early fall, some visit villages and hunting camps looking for garbage. This can give the false impression that the bear population is increasing, because people see them more often. That is why getting accurate population data on these bears is critical.

Canadian scientists are studying this population of polar bears around Hudson Bay to identify ways to conserve them.

Some important questions they need to answer are:

  • how big is the population
  • how fast is it changing
  • how far do the bears travel to find food?

To answer these questions, they need to find a way to count the polar bears and track where they go. The techniques they use include mark and recapture studies and satellite tracking.

Population growth and survivorship curves

The lifespan of a given species plays a large role in the population growth characteristics of a given population. Below are some examples of the lifespans of different types of organisms.

House flies live short lives and produce thousands of eggs in their lifetime. If all of their eggs survived to become adults, the world would be knee-deep in house flies within a few years. Fortunately, this doesn’t happen.
Other species, like elephants and humans, have long lives, but produce only a few offspring during their lifetime. These different strategies determine how quickly their populations can change in response to changing environmental conditions.
Species differ in their survival. Many species, especially insects, complete their entire life cycle in a few weeks.
Others, like the giant cedar trees in British Columbia, may live for hundreds of years. Some whales, sea turtles, and humans can live to be over a hundred years old.
Not all individuals in a species get to live to the maximum age. Some species have high infant mortality rates, but if they can survive past that stage, then their chances of living to the maximum age are pretty good. Many fish species fit this pattern.

The pattern of how mortality rates change with age is called the species survivorship pattern or curve. There are three general patterns of survivorship that describe how species’ mortality rates change with age.

Type I

Species with a type I survivorship curve have relatively few offspring, but each one has a high probability of living to the maximum age. Mortality rates are low and constant for most of their life, but then increase rapidly as they approach the maximum age. The parents generally invest a lot of energy in feeding, caring for, and teaching their offspring, who have a better chance of survival, as a result. Species showing type 1 survivorship curves include birds, humans, and other mammals.

Type II

These species have a constant mortality rate throughout their life, so young and old have an equal chance of surviving. Many insects, like the honey bee, show this type of survivorship curve. This is because parents do not protect their young and experience offers little advantage in avoiding predation.

Type III

These species show a high mortality rate when they are young, but this declines as they grow older. Many species of fish and plants, as well as sea turtles, show this type of survivorship curve. For example, a maple tree produces thousands of seedlings every year, but most die within the first few weeks. Each year sea turtles lay hundreds of eggs in nests on tropical beaches, but most hatchlings are eaten by predators, like gulls, within hours of their emergence. As a result, only 1% of sea turtle hatchlings survive to breeding age. Species with this type of survivorship curve tend to produce many offspring in their lifetime, to compensate for the high early mortality rates

Survivorship and fecundity

The number of offspring an individual can produce over its lifetime is called its fecundity.

Species like house flies and the common field mouse can produce many offspring, so they have high fecundity. Species like hippopotamuses, polar bears, and humans produce few offspring, so they have low fecundity. Species with high fecundity can potentially grow their population size very quickly.

In addition to fecundity, a population’s ability to reproduce is strongly dependent on its age structure. If a high proportion of individuals in the population are at reproductive age, then the population growth rate will be high, even if each individual does not have many offspring. This is because there are simply more individuals reproducing, so the absolute number of offspring will be high. In populations with a low proportion of individuals at reproductive age, the population growth rate will be low, even if each individual has high fecundity. That’s because fewer offspring are being born overall.

For example, the age structure of the population in Mexico has a very large percentage of young people. Many of them will have children over the next few years, so the population of Mexico will continue to grow rapidly.

Japan is the opposite. Its population has a high percentage of elderly people, so it will produce fewer children overall and its population is expected to gradually decline.
