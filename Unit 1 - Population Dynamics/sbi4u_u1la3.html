Animals (and plants) have a natural habitat to which they belong. A habitat is an area with a particular set of features that is defined by two characteristics:

  • physical (abiotic)
  • biological (biotic)

Habitats can be defined using any number of abiotic and biotic features.

For example, the habitat of a chimpanzee will be tropical and will have lots of fruit trees in it.

The habitat of a cedar tree in the coastal rainforest of British Columbia will be wet, warm, and full of moss.

How populations change

No population in nature remains unchanged for long. Population size is always changing due to:

  • new individuals are added through births (natality)
  • immigration (individuals moving into a population)
  • loss through death (mortality)
  • emigration (individuals moving out of a population)

All populations are changed by the number of:

  • births (b)
  • deaths (d)

Some populations are also changed by:

  • immigration (i)
  • emigration (e)

In general, populations can be described as either open or closed depending on different factors described in the table below.

Closed Population Open Population

Impact of immigration and emigration

No barrier preventing them from moving in and out of the population.

An island in the middle of the ocean is generally closed to immigrants that can’t swim or fly

A population of moose in a patch of boreal forest in northern Ontario is considered to be an open population, because the moose can move in and out of it easily.

Population dynamics

Population dynamics are the changes in population size that occur over time.

The four factors that affect population dynamics are:

  • births
  • deaths
  • immigration
  • emigration

Population dynamics are the result of an interaction between:

  • internal factors (for example, reproductive ability)
  • external factors (for example, weather, food supply, predation, and disease)

Some populations remain relatively stable over long periods of time, while others may fluctuate dramatically or go through cycles. Population dynamics research studies how the internal and external factors affecting population size interact to produce changes in population size. This can get very complicated, since populations are simultaneously affected by density, the amount of resources available, their environment, and interactions with other species.

Population ecology is the area of biology that studies how populations change overtime in relation to their environment and other species. Conserving biodiversity, such as the Atlantic puffins (Fratercula arctica) on bird island in Elliston, Newfoundland, is very important in population ecology research.

Modelling and predicting population change

As you learned in the previous learning activity, human activities impact all species on the planet.

This forces us to become global resource managers. However, to do so, we need to know how populations respond to combinations of factors influencing their dynamics. We also need to be able to predict how populations will act in response to the actions we take.

For this purpose, biologists create models of population dynamics to see how populations respond to changes in various input factors.

Before you can predict how a population will change, you need to create a model of how its population dynamics work. Models are made up of equations that describe how the population responds to changes in internal and external factors. Models of population growth range from the simple to the complex. They are built starting with the simplest set of factors thought to be necessary. If the simple model fails to produce accurate predictions, then more factors are added, which makes the model more complex.

In the next sections, you will learn about the three fundamental population growth models: the geometric growth model, the exponential growth model and the logistic growth model.

1. It happens in populations where individuals can only breed once per time period (like deer, which breed once per year).

Exponential growth model

Some populations, like humans, can reproduce at any time of the year. This means that the population can grow continuously, since it is not restricted to fixed time periods.

This produces exponential population growth. Like geometric growth, the population is growing at a fixed rate over a fixed time period. A graph of exponential growth looks almost identical to that of geometric growth, but it has a slightly steeper slope because it increases continuously.

Both the geometric and the exponential growth graphs produce a J-shaped curve.

Here is the equation to determine exponential growth rate at a particular time:

d N d t = r N

Where:

  1. N is population size
  2. r is the growth rate per individual

d N d t = r N

is called the instantaneous growth rate of the population, because it tells you how much the population size is changing at any particular instant in time.

With unlimited environmental resources, the value for r reaches its maximum. This is called the biotic potential of the population. Biotic potential means the maximum population growth rate that could occur under ideal conditions for an organism.

A population of 8,000 algae plants in a pond is growing exponentially. The growth rate per individual, r, is 0.25 per day.

What is the instantaneous growth rate of the population?

= 0.25 × 8,000

Logistic Growth Model

Of course, geometric and exponential growth can’t continue forever. At some point, the population runs out of resources like food or space. All populations eventually face limits on their growth. When this happens, the growth curve changes shape. The logistic growth model describes this pattern.

The logistic growth model more closely resembles what happens in natural populations. Most populations begin with only a handful of breeding individuals.

Since the population is small, the amount of available resources in the area may be much greater than the population needs. So, initially, population growth will not be limited by the resources available. This leads to geometric or exponential growth.

At some point, the population increase begins to slow down because the supply of available resources becomes limited, until eventually, the size stabilizes near its carrying capacity. Because the resulting curve on the graph looks S-shaped, it is sometimes also called a sigmoid curve (sigma is the Greek letter, S).

Using the logistic growth model

Mathematically, the logistic growth model is expressed using the following equation:

d N d t = r N   ( K − N ) K

W h e r e : d N d t is the instantaneous growth rate of the population,

N is the population size at time t

r is the growth rate per individual

K is the carrying capacity

This equation is similar to the one you saw previously for exponential growth. The difference here is the addition of the term (K – N)/K. This term gives you the proportion of the carrying capacity that is still unused. As N gets closer in value to K, (K – N)/K gets closer to zero. This causes the population growth rate to slow down (late log phase). When N reaches K, this term is zero, and so population growth stops (stationary phase).

The logistic growth model is useful for modelling the behaviour of natural populations, because it accounts for the fact that resources are always finite.

However, it assumes that the amount of resources stays constant. In natural systems, there are often variations in resource availability.

For example, the amount of space and food available may be reduced by the arrival of a new species that competes for these resources. Abiotic factors, like extreme weather, fire, or floods can also change the amount of resources available.

As a result, natural populations generally display logistic growth patterns, but not as cleanly as shown in the theoretical model seen in the previous figure.

Density-dependent and density-independent factors

Population growth is affected by internal and external factors. Populations can theoretically undergo exponential growth forever, but are prevented from doing so because of the limits imposed on them by the carrying capacity. The carrying capacity is determined by a combination of internal and external factors.

  • The internal factors are called density-dependent factors, because their influence changes with the density of the population.
  • The external factors are called density-independent factors, because their influence does not depend on the density of the population.

Density-dependent factors

The factors that affect a population’s growth rate because of its density are called density-dependent factors. The effect of these factors intensifies as density increases.

For animals, these factors can include:

  1. the amount of food
  2. breeding territory available
  3. social dynamics
  4. predator avoidance opportunities
  5. the risk of parasites and disease, and so on.

For plants, the availability of resources like sunlight, water, and soil will depend on how many other plants are competing for them in the same area.


Examples of density-dependent factors

Density-dependent factors include:

  1. Predator focus
    Predators may focus their hunting on the most common prey, because they are so visible. As prey density goes up, predation pressure may increase, causing higher mortality.
  2. Resource availability
    When density is high, individuals have to share resources. In some cases, this increases the amount of energy they spend acquiring and protecting resources(for example, feeding territories),which leads to lower birth rates and higher mortality rates.
  3. Aggression
    Under high density some species, like mice, change their behaviour and become more anti-social and aggressive against their own species.
  4. Emigration
    As density increases, some individuals may migrate away from the area, seeking better conditions and thus decreasing the population size.
  5. Waste accumulation
    In dense populations, there can be an accumulation of toxic wastes, such as fecal matter and urine, which pollute the habitat.
  6. Disease
    Diseases and parasites can often spread more quickly in densely crowded populations.
  7. Stress
    High density increases stress levels in many animals, leading to weakened immune systems and other health problems.

Density-independent factors

Density-independent factors affect the population growth rate in ways that are not related to the population’s density. Density-independent factors can wipeout populations, whether they contain a thousand individuals or only ten.

Density-independent factors include abiotic factors such as:

  • Extreme weather events
  • Fires
  • Droughts
  • Volcanic eruptions
  • Pollution

Many insect populations are regulated by density-independent factors, especially weather.

  • Cold weather can wipe out entire populations.
  • Warm weather can cause their numbers to rise exponentially.

There is dramatic evidence of this in populations of the Japanese beetles (Popillia japonica) in western Canada.

The Japanese beetle has traditionally not been very abundant in Canada. However, with climate change, it is spreading northwards, through British Columbia, and east into Alberta, as the climate warms up. The beetles lay their eggs in pine trees and when the eggs hatch, the larvae eat the tissue under the bark, eventually causing the trees to die. Huge areas of western Canada are becoming deforested, due to the recent increase in pine beetle populations.

Cold winters used to kill off most of the Japanese beetles every year, keeping their numbers low. As winters become milder, the beetles are surviving better, which is resulting in an explosion in their numbers. This is an example of a density-independent factor because the warm weather benefits all beetles, whether their population size is high or low.

Population cycles and predator-prey dynamics

Some populations do not follow the logistic or exponential growth pattern. Instead, their numbers go through a population cycle, during which dramatic increases and decreases occur over a regular time period.

For example, the forest tent caterpillar (Malacosoma disstria) is the larval form of a moth found throughout eastern North America. This insect has high fecundity and can lay 300 eggs at a time. It also experiences regular population cycles. These cycles occur about every 10 years and the peak lasts for two to four years.

In a year of peak abundance, the tent caterpillars will eat all of the leaves (defoliate) on most of the Aspen trees, over a wide region. The trees will eventually recover if they are given time to grow their leaves again.

After a few years of very high abundance, the population of tent caterpillars will suddenly decline and stay relatively low for the next eight to 10 years. The causes of these outbreaks and declines are not fully understood, but biologists have shown that weather, predation, and parasites all play a role.

Sometimes, population cycles involve more than one species. This is sometimes seen in the case of a predator and its main prey, where both show a similar fluctuation in population size. A famous example of this is the predator-prey relationship between the Canadian lynx (Lynx canadensis) and the snowshoe hare (Lepus americanus), in Canada’s boreal forest.

Case study: Canadian lynx and snowshoe hare

The example of the population cycles shown by the lynx, a medium-sized predatory cat, and its main prey, the snowshoe hare (a kind of rabbit) is probably the best documented and studied example in the world.

The data

During the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries, employees of the Hudson’s Bay Company in northern Ontario reported that the Ojibwe First Nations people would go through periods of starvation approximately every 10 years. They attributed this to the very obvious periodic decline in a major food source of these indigenous people: the snowshoe hare.

At that time, both the hare and its main predator, the lynx, were trapped for their fur, which was sold commercially through the Hudson’s Bay Company. Because of this, the Hudson’s Bay Company kept detailed records of the numbers of pelts (skins with fur) collected every year, over a period of several hundred years. These records revealed a cycling of the population sizes of lynx and hares, with each cycle lasting about 10 years. A sample of this data is shown below:

You can see that the numbers of hare are generally higher than the numbers of lynx, but that both reach peak levels about every 10 years, and then dramatically drop off again. You can also see that the population peaks in lynx seem to occur a year or two later than those for the hares. In the 1950s, biologists analyzed these records to try and understand what caused these regular cycles. The idea that populations cycled naturally was considered unlikely, as the scientific consensus at the time was that natural populations were generally stable. This data set has since become a textbook example of natural population cycling, and the research into why it occurs continues to this day.

How the cycle happens?

The preferred food of hares is the mature leaves (needles) of white spruce trees that grow everywhere in the boreal forest. However, these leaves grow very slowly in the cold climate, so hares eat all of the available needles in a short time. Lynx are major predators of the hares and will travel many kilometres to find areas in which hares are abundant.

The population cycle of lynx and hares is synchronized over a very wide area of the boreal forest region of northern Canada. Movement of individual animals in and out of the population can’t explain the pattern over an area this large, so it must be the result of births and deaths.

Biologists have investigated many possible causes for this pattern and no single cause has emerged as the driving force. It is now understood that the cycles result from a complex interaction of many density-dependent and density-independent factors.

The latest research indicates that a combination of two main factors primarily drives the cycle. They are:

  • Food Supply
  • Predation

The interactions of these factors cause the cycle to have three phases:

The Increasing Phase

When density and numbers of hares are low:

  1. there is a lot for them to eat and their fecundity can be high.
  2. females can produce 16–18 offspring per summer.
  3. they can also start breeding at one year of age, so their population can increase quickly.
  4. the numbers of their predators is low also.
  5. most predators, like the lynx, foxes, and owls, will switch to other prey. This gives the hare population a chance to increase.

However, as the hare numbers increase, they begin to run out of their preferred food and shift to eating less nutritious vegetation, especially during winter.

Peak Phase

Hare numbers increase rapidly when there is:

  1. abundant food
  2. low predation

Within three years, they can reach peak abundance. However, as their density increases:

  1. their mortality increases
  2. their reproduction rate declines
  3. mostly likely because less food is available and there is more social stress

When hares are abundant, the food supply for lynx is plentiful, so they can also reproduce rapidly. However, the lynx only gives birth in spring, which means that the population increase in lynx is delayed, in relation to that of the hare. The increase in the lynx population generally lags behind the increase in the hare population by a year or two.

Decline Phase

Once the hares reach their peak abundance, their numbers start to fall quickly because

  1. they are running out of high-quality food.
  2. there are now increasing numbers of predators eating them and their offspring. Lynx eat the adult hares, while opportunistic predators like hawks, owls, and foxes eat both adults and juveniles.

Consequently, as the hare population decreases drastically the Lynx are impacted because:

  1. their food supply disappears, and some lynx starve.
  2. they become susceptible to disease, causing increased mortality and decreased fecundity.

This causes the population of lynx to drop dramatically as well, but this only occurs after the hares have begun their decline. With low numbers of hares, the vegetation now begins its recovery. Eventually, with more food available and lower numbers of predators, the hare population increases, and the cycle begins again.

Population cycles and predator-prey dynamics

2) Why does the population cycle of lynx usually lag behind the cycle of the hare by about one year? Answer the question below and check your response with the suggested answer.

The population increase in the lynx takes about a year to occur after the hare population increases, because the lynx only gives birth once per year in spring. As the abundance of hares declines, there is less food for the lynx, but this does not cause starvation or a decline in the lynx population until most of the hares are gone, and this takes many months to occur.
