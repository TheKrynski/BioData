Species don’t exist in isolation. Their populations interact directly and indirectly with the other species around them. Understanding these interactions is the key to managing our natural resources and saving the earth’s diminishing biodiversity. Biologists are in a race against time to get this knowledge.

In the last learning activity, you saw how a direct interaction—predation—could drive population dynamics. In this learning activity, you will learn about two more types of important direct interaction: competition and symbiosis.

Species also interact indirectly with each other in a web of life so complex that we are only beginning to understand it. A change in one population can affect the entire hierarchy of living things in that system, often in surprising ways. A case study on the decline of the Atlantic cod fishery dramatically reveals how humans can upset the natural ecosystem hierarchy with dramatic and unforeseen consequences.

Just like the fibres in this cobweb are connected and are directly or indirectly interacting with each other, species also interact directly or indirectly in the complex web of life.

Levels of organization in ecological systems

Populations of species interact with each other and also with individuals from other species who live in the same habitat. The combined interactions of species together in an area form communities. The boundaries of communities are sometimes hard to define. Communities can be as small as a patch of soil or as large as a hundred square kilometres of boreal forest, depending on the type of interactions you are studying. Groups of communities interact together to form ecosystems.

Ecologists recognize a hierarchy of organizational levels to describe how individuals and species interact within an area.

  • Individuals form the lowest level of the hierarchy.
  • Populations are formed at the next level when individuals of the same species combine.
  • Communities form when populations of different species interact.
  • Ecosystems are formed when communities interact with each other, and with other biotic and abiotic processes.

Habitat

Ecologists define “habitat” as the space in which individuals of a species carry out their daily lives. While many species may occupy the same habitat, each species uses slightly different aspects of it to ensure their survival. How an organism uses its habitat, and the way it utilizes the resources within it, is called its ecological “niche.”

One way to remember the difference between a habitat and a niche is to think of the habitat as the “address” of the organism and the ecological niche as its “way of life.”
For example, the wolves, deer, moose, and squirrels living around a lake in northern Ontario may all share the same habitat, but each has its own ecological niche.
The niche of the wolf includes what it eats (for example, deer and moose), the way it reproduces, and all of the other factors that describe its pattern of living.
The niche of the squirrel includes the types of trees it likes to nest in, the seeds it eats, and the time of day it likes to be active, and so on.

Types of interactions among organisms

The interactions among organisms in an ecosystem can be broadly divided into two groups:

  1. Interactions between two organisms (or species)
  2. Community-level interactions

Competition

A competitive relationship occurs when:

  • two or more organisms are using the same set of resources in the same area to the disadvantage of each.
  • the interaction results in a negative outcome for each organism.

Competition can defined as:

  • Interspecific: between members of different species.
  • Intraspecific: among members of the same species.

Competition can be further categorized into two types:

  • Interference competition
  • Exploitative competition

Interference competition occurs when individuals are directly interacting with each other over access to the resource. This is what you usually think about when you imagine competition (for example, two lions fighting over a kill, or a gull and a crow fighting over some spilled garbage).

Exploitative competition is indirect competition (for example, when one organism eats all of the fruit off a tree a day before another organism can arrive to feed on it). Each organism is exploiting the resource that could be used by the other, even if they are never in the same place at the same time.

Interspecific competition

Theoretically, if there are not enough resources to support all of the competing species, then the populations of the less successful ones will decline. Even the successful species are not expected to reach their potential maximum population growth rate for a while, because they aren’t getting all of the resources for themselves. But how important is interspecific competition in nature?

This question was answered in a landmark study on barnacles living on rocks by the seashore. It provided the first strong evidence of the importance of interspecific competition in shaping community structure.

Dr. J.H. Connell, a researcher at the University of California, conducted a study of the interaction between two species of barnacle that inhabit the coast of Scotland. Barnacles are sedentary filter feeders that permanently attach to a rock for life. They vary in size and shape, and have different adaptations that make them suited to different habitats within the marine environment.

The first species of barnacle that Connell studied is called Chthamalus stellatus.

  • It was found only in the exposed intertidal zone. This meant that these barnacles lived on rocks that were underwater at high tide but exposed to the air at low tide.
  • Chthamalus spent part of their day exposed to the dry air of the atmosphere, so they adapted physically and behaviourally, to avoid becoming dried out when the tide was low.

The second species studied was Semibalanus balanoides.

It was present on the rock surface below the low tide mark, so these barnacles were always submerged. They adapted so that they would succeed in the underwater environment.

These two species shared the same shoreline habitat and lived on the same rocks, but their populations were neatly divided at the low tide line, with Chthamalus above and Semibalanus below.

Experiment 1 Results:

When Semibalanus were removed from the rocks Chthamalus were able to spread onto the newly available rock surface underwater.

Experiment 2 Results:

When Chthamalus were removed Semibalanus could not spread onto the available surface above the tide line. This was because it was not able to adapt to the conditions out of the water.

However, in an example of interference competition, Semibalanus were able to undercut and pry off Chthamalus wherever they tried to attach to surfaces below the tide line. The following image shows how these two species divided the habitat.

From his experiments, Connell concluded that each species had both a “fundamental” niche and a “realized” niche, and that this helped to explain how communities of species were structured.

  • The fundamental niche of an organism is the entire niche that it is capable of using, based on its physiological requirements.
  • The realized niche of an organism is the actual niche that it occupies, based on the local competitive conditions.
Chthamalus Semibalanus
Fundamental niche

Because Chthamalus could live underwater, as well as in the air, its fundamental niche included all of the shoreline rock surfaces, both above and below the low tide line.

The fundamental niche for Semibalanus only included the rocks below the low tide line, since it died when exposed to the air in the intertidal zone above.
Realized niche

Chthamalus was experiencing its realized niche, which was smaller than its fundamental niche.

For Semibalanus, the fundamental and realized niches in this case were identical, since it occupied the entire rocky habitat on which it was physically capable of living.

The fact that Chthamalus was not exploiting its entire fundamental niche was due to Semibalanus competing for the resources through direct interference under the water. Semibalanus was able to exploit its entire fundamental niche.

Most species are restricted to existing in just their realized niche, because of the actions of the other species with which they share the habitat. This causes a lot of the variation in community patterns that are seen in nature.

Competitive exclusion

Luckily for these two species of barnacle, their fundamental niches are not identical to each other; otherwise one of them would probably be extinct by now. The reason why was demonstrated in experiments conducted by the Russian ecologist, G.F. Gause, in the 1930s.

Gause studied the effects of competition on two closely related species of protozoa in the genus Paramecium.

Experimental procedure

  1. He grew two closely related species of protozoa in the genus Paramecium separately in test tubes provided with food and recorded their population growth.
  2. Then he grew both in the same test tube so that they had to compete with each other for food.
  3. He recorded their population growth under this new condition.

Results

  • When grown separately, each population would stabilize at its carrying capacity and persist indefinitely.
  • When grown together, one species would always out-compete the other for food, and so drive it to extinction.

Conclusion

  • The paramecium populations would never coexist.

Press here for long description(Opens in a new window)

Gause concluded that when resources are limited and two species share similar requirements, they can’t coexist in the same community. This became known as Gause’s principle of competitive exclusion. The result of competitive exclusion in nature is that it is very rare for two species with similar niche requirements to be in competition for very long. One will eventually out-compete the other, resulting in the disappearance of the loser.

How can species coexist?

Interspecific competition in a community should eventually result in the loss of one of the competitors. So, why do we see similar species coexisting in nature?

The answer lies in how the species share the resources. Similar species that share the same area usually divide up the resources using a method called resource partitioning. This means that each species specializes in harvesting one particular subset of the resource.

For example, on islands in the Caribbean, several species of lizard in the genus Anolis can be found on the same tree. These lizards all eat a similar diet of insects, vegetation, or fruit.

How can so many species of lizard coexist on the same tree, when they apparently all use the same resources?

Through careful observation, it was noticed that each Anolis species was associated with certain parts of the tree. Some species preferred branches near the top, some perched on the tips of branches lower down, and others clung to the trunks. By splitting the niche the whole tree) into parts and specializing in using only their part, the different Anolis lizards were able to share the tree without directly competing for the same resources. This allowed several species to coexist.

Predation

Predation occurs when a predator feeds on its prey, causing the immediate death of the prey.

Predators may:

Hunt actively for prey, like a lion chasing down a zebra.

Sit and wait for their prey to approach within striking distance, like a spider in its web.

Predation is a major force shaping community structure. Like many interspecific interactions, predation can cause changes in the community that reach beyond the individual species directly involved.

Since predation is fatal to the prey species; there is strong selection pressure on the prey to evolve ways to defend against predators. For example, individual hares that can outrun the lynx will survive to breed and pass on those speedy traits to their offspring. But running isn’t the only defense possible and a species may incorporate several of the defense strategies described next, to maximize their chance of survival.

Defending against predation is not restricted just to animals; plants can also be prey. For example, every time a deer eats a young tree seedling, it is killing that tree. That is why defences against predation are a big factor in the evolution of plant diversity.

Types of defenses

Prey species have evolved many forms of defence against their predators. This is one of the reasons why life on earth is so diverse.

A morphological defence is one involving the shape or structure of an organism. Have you ever grabbed a plant stem and felt the shooting pain of a thorn? Thorns, spines, and stinging spikes are all examples of morphological defences that plants use against their potential consumers.

Chemical

Animals and plants have evolved chemicals that they can use to reduce predation.

For example, skunks can spray a noxious-smelling chemical in self-defence—even at you, if they see you as a threat! Since plants can’t run away from their predators, they tend to rely on chemical defences the most.

Many plants produce secondary chemical compounds to keep predators away. These chemicals are not essential to the plant’s metabolism, so they are considered secondary to the plant. These chemical compounds can range in severity, from those that are toxic and kill the predator, to those that interfere with its metabolism, to those that just leave a nasty taste in the predator’s mouth.

For example, milkweed plants, found in fields throughout Ontario, are named for the sticky white sap they produce, which is a chemical that is unpalatable to most animals.

Plants may seem to be a passive and unprotected organism, but there are so many different ways that plants can defend themselves. To learn about some of these mechanisms, you may read about all the different ways they can do this.

To learn more you may choose to read this article on plant defences or perform a search on your own. (Opens in new window)

Prey use colour in two ways:

  • Cryptic colouration (or camouflage): to hide themselves from predators. By camouflaging themselves, the prey can avoid predation by not being noticed. Many fascinating examples of cryptic colouration exist in nature, particularly among insects.
  • Aposematic colouration: to warn predators that they are not good to eat. Instead of trying to hide from predators, these species have evolved dramatic colour displays to warn predators. This warning colouration is usually bright red, yellow, or blue.

Symbiosis

Besides predation or competition, two species may live closely together and interact in ways that benefit at least one of the species. Relationships like this are called symbiosis, which means “living together.” Recall from the Minds-on activity at the start, there are many types of relationships between organisms.

What is symbiosis? (Opens in new window)

Symbiotic relationships can take three main forms:

Mutualism (where both species benefit)

Mutualism occurs when both participating species benefit from living closely together.

The relationship between flowering plants and their pollinators is a striking example of mutualism.

  • The pollinators are usually insects, birds, or mammals (such as bats).
  • The plants benefit because their male gametes (pollen) attach to the bodies of the pollinators, who then transport them to distant flowers. This ensures that the plants are spreading their genes over a wide area and increases their reproductive success.
  • The pollinators benefit because the plant usually provides them with a high energy meal, in the form of nectar or fruit.

Mutualism occurs in humans as well. Many of the bacteria living in your intestines have a mutual relationship with you.

  • You benefit because they help you to chemically break down food, such as some types of fibre, which you can’t process on your own.
  • The bacteria benefit because they have a safe place to live, with a regular supply of the nutrients they need.

Commensalism (where one species benefits and the other is unaffected)

The relationship in which one species benefits while the other species does not (but is also not harmed) is called commensalism.

A popular example of commensalism is the relationship that exists between various species of sea anemones and clownfish on coral reefs.

Sea anemones look like flowers but are actually predators related to coral and jellyfish. They feed on small fish and marine invertebrates that swim through their paralyzing tentacles. Because their sting can be dangerous, most large fish avoid going near them. This creates an opportunity for several small species of fish, known as clownfish, to find a safe place to hide from predators.

The clownfish evolve immunity to the toxins of their particular host species of sea anemone. In that way, the clownfish can live amongst the stinging tentacles of the anemone, safe from predators. They are also able to eat some of the food left behind, after the anemone has digested its prey. This relationship is commensal because, even though the clownfish gains protection, and even food, the anemone seems to gain no advantage or disadvantage from the relationship.

Parasitism

Parasitism is a broad category of interspecific interactions in which one species benefits at the expense of another. There are numerous examples of parasitism found in all of the kingdoms of life.

Parasitic relationships can be categorized by the:

  • relative size of the parasite to its host (microparasites and macroparasites)
  • whether the parasites live on the outside of the host (ectoparasites) or inside it (endoparasites)
  • whether they parasitize the behaviour of the host (social parasitism).
Microparasites

Microparasites are those parasites that are microscopic in size. A microparasite that is particularly important to human health is the protozoan, Plasmodium, which causes the disease, malaria. Every year, this disease affects over 300 million people worldwide, killing more than a million, most of whom are children.

Macroparasites

Larger parasites that are visible to the naked eye are called macroparasites. Macroparasites can either live inside of a host (e.g. adult tapeworms) or live on the external parts of their hosts (e.g. lice on the skin of animals).

Social parasitism

A different approach to parasitism is found in social parasitism. This does not involve a parasite consuming resources from its host’s body. Instead, the parasite manipulates the social behaviour of the host. A dramatic example of social parasitism is when a bird’s nest is taken over by another bird that uses the nest for its own eggs. This type of parasitism is called brood parasitism, because the parasitic bird manipulates the host bird into raising its own offspring or “brood.”

A famous example of this type of parasitic bird found in Ontario is the common cuckoo (Cuculus canorus). The mother cuckoo watches other nesting birds until it detects that they have left their nest unattended. It then quickly flies over and lays its eggs in the nest, after throwing the host bird’s own eggs out of the nest. The cuckoo then quickly abandons the eggs. Upon their return, the host parents do not recognize the switch and incubate the cuckoo’s eggs as their own. When the young cuckoos hatch, the host parents feed the chicks. As a result, the host parents do the cuckoo parents’ work for them.

Community-level interactions

There can be thousands of species in a habitat. All may be interacting with each other, to varying degrees, through predation, competition, or symbiotic relationships. How can you make sense of this complexity?

Ecologists organize these interactions in two ways, to help them understand patterns in natural communities.

1. First, species are grouped into energy-flow hierarchies based on what they eat. These feeding hierarchies are called trophic levels.

2. Second, the connections among species in a community are analyzed as networks. These networks are called food webs.

Food webs

The linear feeding relationships through the trophic levels are sometimes called food chains. In real communities, most chains are cross-linked to other food chains, to form complex networks of feeding relationships called food webs. It is through these networks that changes in the population dynamics of one species can have a ripple effect on many other species in the community.

A simplified food web of a typical small lake in Ontario is shown in the following figure.

  • Green plants like algae, pond lilies, and reeds are the producers.
  • They are eaten by herbivorous fish, insect larvae, and crayfish, which are the main primary consumers.
  • Secondary consumers, like leopard frogs, carnivorous fish (for example, bass), and insects (for example, water beetles) eat the herbivores.
  • Finally, tertiary consumers, such as predatory birds (for example, the blue heron) eat the primary consumers (for example, crayfish), as well as the secondary consumers (for example, leopard frogs and bass).

You can follow the flow of energy through individual food chains within this food web to see how the feeding relationships might affect the population dynamics of each species.

For example, one chain is:

Algae → insect larvae → leopard frogs → bass → blue heron

while a second is:

Pond lily → crayfish → bass → blue heron

Both chains begin with the photosynthetic capture of sunlight energy and end with the consumption of bass by blue herons. The second chain has fewer levels (four versus five). Because the second chain is fewer steps away from the original energy source (i.e. the sun), it is more efficient at transferring the sun’s energy. You can see that the trophic levels for the bass and blue heron change, depending on which chain you use.

Using food webs to predict species population dynamics

When the abundance of a species changes, for whatever reason, it can cause a shift in the energy flow throughout the ecosystem. These changes can then affect the populations of the other species in the food web, either directly or indirectly. To see how this can happen, imagine what would happen in the Ontario lake’s food web, if the frogs were to suddenly disappear.

With the frogs gone:

  • the populations of insect larvae they feed on would increase, since they would be under less predation pressure.
  • the bass would be without a major food source. Either its population would decline or the bass would have to shift to eating more of the other prey species, such as crayfish, herbivorous fish, or water beetles. If the bass shifted their diet, then those prey species would probably suffer population declines.
  • the situation for the water beetles and algae is unclear.
  • the beetles would get to eat more insect larvae, but the bass might prey upon them more.
  • the abundance of algae would probably decrease, because there would be more insect larvae to feed on it unless the number of water beetles became very high, in which case the algae could become even more abundant.

As you can see, predicting what would happen is not always easy, even in this very simplified version of a food web.

Case study: The collapse of the Atlantic cod fishery

Atlantic cod (Gadus morhua) normally live near the bottom of the ocean, along the continental shelves surrounding the North Atlantic Ocean. They can live to be over 20 years old. Adults are usually about a metre long and weigh about five kilograms, but some can grow to be over two metres long and weigh over 100 kilograms. Atlantic cod are a top predator in the food chain, feeding on smaller fish, shrimp, and crab.

In 1497, English explorer John Cabot reached the North Atlantic fishing grounds southeast of Newfoundland, in an area now called the Grand Banks. Cabot reported that the cod fish were so abundant there, that his crew could just drop baskets over the side of the ship to haul in all the fish they could eat. Over the next 450 years, the cod were fished sustainably. This stopped in 1992, when the Government of Canada was forced to close the Grand Banks cod fishery because there were no fish left.

The story of the cod fishery collapse has become a famous example of how human overconsumption and the tragedy of the commons can destroy even the most abundant resource. It also reveals the complexity of food webs and how changes in one species can cause unpredictable consequences for the entire ecosystem. How could such an abundant population of fish be wiped out and what were the consequences for the ecosystem?

Why the fishery collapsed?

The fishery changed dramatically in 1951, with the arrival of the first factory freezer ship. These new ships combined the roles of a fish processing plant and a fishing boat. Because they could be used to process and freeze their catch at sea, they could be taken out for weeks at a time, catching fish continuously. They were also equipped with sonar that was designed to find large schools of fish.

These ships were called “bottom trawlers” because they trawled (dragged) huge nets along the ocean floor. The nets scooped up everything in their path, while scraping the seabed. This destroyed the feeding and spawning grounds of fish like cod. The nets strip-mined the ocean floor’s ecosystem. By the mid-1960s, there were hundreds of these ships, from various countries, on the Grand Banks.

Annual catches of cod and other large predatory fish rose dramatically. In 1968, the cod catch peaked at 810 000 tonnes, four times greater than the previous average annual catch. The factory ships were so effective at unsustainably catching cod that it took them only 15 years to catch as much cod as had been caught in the previous 300 years combined, and thousands of years before that when Indigenous peoples fished sustainably.

The high fishing pressure caused the numbers of cod to decline rapidly, during the 1970s. The Canadian government responded by lowering the quota on the number of cod that could be caught within its territorial waters, but this effort was too little and too late. In 1992, the fishery was closed.

Ecosystem consequences

Everyone assumed that once the cod were protected from fishing, their numbers would increase again, probably exponentially. This hasn’t happened yet, and recent research suggests it may not happen at all, because of permanent changes to the food web. You can see how this happens by examining a simplified food chain for the cod.

Simplified cod food chain:

Phytoplankton (producers) → zooplankton → small fish, shrimp, crabs → cod

Once the cod, and other large predatory fish, disappeared due to overfishing, their prey experienced a population explosion. This was expected, based on the food chain relationship. The top predators in the ecosystem are now the small fish, shrimp, and crabs.

One unforeseen development is that the small fish predators also compete with, and eat, the very young cod. This means that cod are more likely to be the prey of the small fish, than to be their predators. The cod population can’t increase because competition with, and predation by, the small fish prevents most cod from growing large enough to become the top predator in the web. Before the collapse, there were enough large cod to keep the numbers of small fish low, which gave the young cod a chance to grow up. This isn’t the case anymore.

This discovery shows how complex the relationships in food webs can be, and the challenges that exist in making predictions about the dynamics of populations in the real world.
